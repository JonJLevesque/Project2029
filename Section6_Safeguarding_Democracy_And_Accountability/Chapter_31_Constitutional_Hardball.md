### A. Emergency Declaration: Democracy Crisis

**Action:** Declare a national emergency under the National Emergencies Act (50 U.S.C. Â§ 1601) based on the threat to democratic governance.

**Powers Unlocked:**

**Legal Risk:** **Medium.** Courts have been reluctant to overturn emergency declarations (Trump's survived), but progressive use might face conservative judicial resistance. **Strategy:** Frame as temporary pending legislative fixes, use litigation delay to buy time for reforms.

**Action:** Direct IRS to audit and revoke tax-exempt status of 501(c)(4) "social welfare" organizations engaged in political activity.

- IRS has statutory duty to enforce tax code (26 U.S.C. Â§ 7201+)

**Specific Actions:**

## III. Week 1: Structural Power Moves

These require Senate cooperation, but only a simple majority. If Democrats control Senate (even 50-50 with VP tiebreaker), these can be done immediately.

### A. Eliminate the Filibuster

**Action:** Senate votes to change Rule XXII to allow cloture by simple majority (currently requires 60 votes).

**Legal Basis:**
- Article I, Section 5: "Each House may determine the Rules of its Proceedings"
- Constitution says NOTHING about filibusters
- Filibuster is a Senate rule, changeable by simple majority
- Republicans already eliminated filibuster for Supreme Court nominees (2017)

**Process:**
- **Day 1 of new Senate session:** Majority Leader raises point of order that cloture requires only 51 votes
- Presiding officer (Vice President or President Pro Tem) rules on point of order
- If ruled against, majority appeals ruling, wins appeal with 51 votes
- **Alternative:** Amend Rule XXII via simple majority (Senate can change its rules by majority)

**Impact:**
- All subsequent legislation requires only 51 votes to pass
- Unlocks entire reform agenda
- Republicans already broke this norm for judges; extending to legislation is justified

**Arguments Against:** "This will be used against us when Republicans have majority"

**Counter:** Republicans will eliminate filibuster next time they want to anyway (they already did for judges). Not eliminating it just means Democratic reforms die while Republican deregulation succeeds (via budget reconciliation).

**Political Risk:** **High.** Will face enormous criticism. **Strategy:** Do it Day 1, pass flurry of popular legislation immediately to show value.

### B. Supreme Court Expansion to 13 Justices

**Action:** Pass Judiciary Act of 2025 expanding Supreme Court from 9 to 13 justices.

**Legal Basis:**
- Constitution sets no number of justices
- Judiciary Act of 1789 set 6 justices
- Changed to 7 (1807), 9 (1837), 10 (1863), back to 9 (1869)
- Congress has plenary power to set Court size (Article III)

**Process:**
- **Week 1:** Introduce bill
- **Month 1:** Pass via simple majority (filibuster eliminated)
- **Month 2:** President nominates 4 new justices
- **Month 3-4:** Senate confirms all 4 (simple majority)

**New Court Composition:**
- Currently: 6 conservative, 3 liberal
- After expansion: 6 conservative, 7 liberal (assuming Democratic appointees)
- **Result:** Liberal majority, can overrule *Citizens United*, *Shelby County*, gun cases, etc.

**Justification:**
- Match 13 federal circuit courts (1 justice per circuit)
- Restore balance after Garland seat theft and Barrett rushed confirmation
- Prevent ideological hijacking of Court

**Legal Risk:** **None.** Completely constitutional. Republicans could try to expand again later, but unlikely if new Court is legitimate and popular.

**Political Risk:** **Extreme.** Will be called "court packing." **Counter:** "Republicans packed the Court by stealing Garland's seat and rushing Barrett. We're restoring balance."

## V. Legal Framework and Judicial Defense

### A. Anticipating Legal Challenges

**Every aggressive action will be challenged in court.** Here's how to win:

**1. Forum Shopping**
- File lawsuits in favorable jurisdictions (e.g., D.C. Circuit, 9th Circuit)
- Appeal to friendly circuit courts
- Accept some district court losses, win on appeal

**2. Litigation Delay**
- Use procedural tools to slow conservative challenges
- By the time cases reach SCOTUS (2-3 years), reforms are entrenched

**3. Court Expansion Defense**
- Once Court is expanded, it's friendlier to reforms
- New Court can overrule conservative precedents blocking reforms

**4. Political Question Doctrine**
- Some challenges can be dismissed as "political questions" courts shouldn't decide
- Emergency declarations, Senate rules, statehood = political questions

**5. Mootness**
- Once reforms are implemented and popular, courts are reluctant to overturn
- E.g., DC statehood: once DC is a state with senators, hard to undo

### B. Jurisdiction Stripping

If conservative courts block reforms, **Congress can strip their jurisdiction**.

**Article III, Section 2:**
> "The Supreme Court shall have appellate Jurisdiction...with such Exceptions, and under such Regulations as the Congress shall make."

**Examples:**
- Congress could prohibit courts from hearing challenges to campaign finance laws
- Congress could prohibit courts from hearing challenges to voting rights laws
- Congress could require 7-2 majorities for SCOTUS to overturn Acts of Congress

**Precedent:** Congress has stripped jurisdiction before (e.g., *Ex parte McCardle*, 1869)

**Use:** As nuclear option if courts consistently block democratic reforms.
