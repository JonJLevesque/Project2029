## WEEK 2: CHOOSE YOUR FOCUS

### Criteria for Choosing Which Reforms to Organize Around:

1. **Local Relevance:** Does this issue affect your community directly?
2. **Winability:** Can you achieve this at local/state level? Or build momentum for federal?
3. **Coalition Potential:** Can you partner with existing organizations?
4. **Personal Passion:** Do you and your team care deeply about this?
5. **Strategic Impact:** Does success on this reform open doors for others?

## WEEK 3: BUILD YOUR ORGANIZING PLAN

### Components of Successful Campaign:

**1. CLEAR GOAL**
- Specific, measurable, achievable, time-bound
- Examples:
  - "Pass automatic voter registration in our state by November 2025"
  - "Get 10 Congressional candidates to pledge support for Citizens United reversal by primary season"
  - "Convince our city to implement free public transit pilot by 2026"

**2. TARGET**
- Who has power to give you what you want?
- Examples:
  - State legislators (for state-level reforms)
  - Members of Congress (for federal reforms)
  - City council (for local pilots)
  - Candidates (for pledges and commitments)
- Research: What's their current position? What moves them? (Voters? Donors? Ideology?)

**3. TACTICS**

**Spectrum of Tactics (choose multiple):**

**Low-Intensity (Build Base):**
- Social media campaigns
- Petition drives
- Educational events (film screenings, teach-ins)
- Tabling at community events
- Door-to-door canvassing (education)

**Medium-Intensity (Apply Pressure):**
- Town hall bird-dogging (ask tough questions publicly)
- Phone/email/letter campaigns to representatives
- Lobby days (visit offices, in-district meetings)
- Op-eds and letters to editor
- Candidate forums (make this an issue)

**High-Intensity (Escalate):**
- Primary challenges (run against opponents)
- Protests and rallies
- Direct action (sit-ins, banner drops, creative disruption)
- Media-getting stunts (dramatic, visual, newsworthy)
- Electoral organizing (GOTV for supportive candidates)

**Build Power Tactics:**
- Coalition formation (unite multiple groups)
- Leadership development (train organizers)
- Base-building (grow membership)
- Fundraising (sustain operations)

**4. COALITION PARTNERS**
- Who else cares about this? Partner, don't compete
- Examples:
  - Voting rights reform: NAACP, ACLU, League of Women Voters, Common Cause
  - Campaign finance: Every Voice, RepresentUs, Move to Amend, labor unions
  - Civic education: Teachers unions, PTAs, education advocates
  - Tech accountability: Mozilla, EFF, tech ethics groups

**5. TIMELINE**
- Break campaign into phases:
  - Phase 1 (Months 1-3): Build base, educate, recruit
  - Phase 2 (Months 4-6): Launch public campaign, apply pressure
  - Phase 3 (Months 7-12): Escalate, deliver win or pivot
- Set milestones: "By March: 1,000 petition signatures. By June: Meet with 10 legislators. By December: Vote or pledge."

**6. RESOURCES**
- What do you need?
  - People: Volunteers, organizers, leaders
  - Money: For materials, travel, paid staff (if scaling up)
  - Skills: Social media, graphic design, data, legal, media relations
  - Stuff: Office space, technology, printing, transportation
- How will you get these? (Fundraising, in-kind donations, volunteer recruitment)

**7. MESSAGING**
- Develop 3-5 core messages (simple, memorable, emotional)
- Examples:
  - "One person, one vote" (Electoral College abolition)
  - "Democracy shouldn't be for sale" (Citizens United)
  - "Every vote should count" (Voting rights broadly)
  - "Our government should serve us, not donors" (Anti-corruption)
- Test messages: What resonates with your base? With persuadable people? With decision-makers?

**8. COMMUNICATIONS**
- Social media strategy (Which platforms? How often? What content?)
- Email list (Build and maintain‚Äîyour most valuable asset)
- Website or landing page (Central hub for information and action)
- Press strategy (When to engage media? Who's your spokesperson?)
- Internal communications (How does your team stay coordinated?)

### First Month Tactics:

**Build Base:**
- 2-3 educational events (film screening, teach-in, guest speaker)
- Door-to-door canvassing in target neighborhoods
- Tabling at community events (farmers market, festivals, etc.)
- Social media content: 3-5 posts/week (education, inspiration, calls to action)

**Apply Pressure:**
- Phone bank to target legislators (100+ calls/week)
- Office visits (in-district meetings with staff or member)
- Town hall bird-dogging (ask public questions, record responses, share online)
- Op-ed submitted to local paper

**Build Coalition:**
- Reach out to 5-10 potential partner organizations
- Offer to table at their events
- Co-host an educational event
- Cross-promote on social media

**Metrics:**
- Track: Petition signatures, volunteer recruits, social media followers, press hits, meetings with targets
- Goal: Double your base every month (10 ‚Üí 20 ‚Üí 40 ‚Üí 80...)

## LONG-TERM: SUSTAIN AND SCALE

### Months 2-6: Escalate

**Build Power:**
- Every volunteer recruits 2 more ‚Üí exponential growth
- Launch phonebanking and texting programs (scale up contact)
- Fundraise (small-dollar donations from base)
- Leadership development (identify and train new leaders)

**Increase Pressure:**
- Move from education to confrontation (if targets unresponsive)
- Protests/rallies (if needed‚Äîvisual, media-getting)
- Primary challenge threat (if incumbent opposes reform)
- Electoral work (if elections coming‚Äîmake this THE issue)

**Win or Pivot:**
- If winning: Push for final victory (vote, pledge, policy change)
- If stalled: Escalate tactics or pivot strategy
- If losing: Learn lessons, regroup, try different approach or target

---

### Months 6-12: Deliver Victory or Build for Next Cycle

**Possible Outcomes:**

**Victory:**
- State/local reform passed (AVR, RCV, public financing, etc.)
- Candidate pledges secured (Congressional candidates commit)
- Awareness raised massively (issue is now on agenda)
- **Celebrate, publicize, replicate** (help other areas win too)

**Partial Victory:**
- Compromise achieved (not everything, but progress)
- Issue is now mainstream (next legislature/admin will address)
- Base built (you have organization now for next fight)
- **Consolidate gains, prepare for next phase**

**Stalemate or Loss:**
- Target didn't move or moved wrong direction
- **Learn lessons:** What worked? What didn't? Why?
- **Regroup:** Different tactics? Different target? Different coalition?
- **Pivot:** Focus on electoral (replace opponent) or move to different venue (state vs. local vs. federal)
- **Build for long-term:** This is generational work‚Äîlosses are learning

---

### Year 2+: Sustain the Movement

**Avoid Burnout:**
- Rotate leadership (don't rely on one person)
- Celebrate small wins (not just final victory)
- Self-care and community care (this is marathon, not sprint)
- Fun! (Social events, music, art, joy‚Äînot just grinding)

**Institutionalize:**
- Form formal organization (501c3 or c4 if scaling up)
- Hire staff (if you raise enough money)
- Establish regular meetings and structures
- Document lessons learned (so new people can plug in)

**Scale:**
- Help other communities launch campaigns (share resources)
- Coordinate statewide or nationally (if reform is federal)
- Join national coalitions (link local to national efforts)

**Electoral:**
- If reform requires federal action: Elect champions to Congress, Presidency
- Make Project 2029 reforms a litmus test (candidates must commit)
- Primary challenges for opponents (or electoral support for champions)
- 2026, 2028, 2030... make these reforms THE issue

## ORGANIZING BEST PRACTICES

### Dos:

‚úÖ **Start small:** Better to have 10 committed people than 100 passively interested
‚úÖ **Be specific:** Clear goals, clear targets, clear tactics
‚úÖ **Build relationships:** Organizing is about people, not just issues
‚úÖ **Center those most affected:** Voting rights = center BIPOC communities. Economic justice = center working class.
‚úÖ **Embrace tension:** Change requires conflict. Don't shy away from confrontation (but be strategic).
‚úÖ **Celebrate:** Wins, losses, milestones‚Äîmark them. Build culture and morale.
‚úÖ **Tell stories:** Personal stories move people more than statistics
‚úÖ **Train leaders:** Invest in developing new organizers‚Äîmovement must grow
‚úÖ **Be patient and persistent:** This is generational work. Don't give up after one setback.

### Don'ts:

‚ùå **Don't be vague:** "Raise awareness" is not a goal. What specific change do you want?
‚ùå **Don't do it alone:** Coalition is everything. Partner, don't compete.
‚ùå **Don't wait for perfection:** Launch before you're ready. Iterate in public.
‚ùå **Don't ignore power:** If your tactics don't create pressure, you're just making noise.
‚ùå **Don't be mean:** Fierce and loving > vicious and hateful. Organize with dignity.
‚ùå **Don't burn out leaders:** Rotate responsibilities. No martyrs.
‚ùå **Don't ignore electoral:** Policy wins require political power. Elect champions.
‚ùå **Don't give up:** Losses are lessons. Regroup and try again.

## DISCUSSION GUIDE FOR STUDY GROUPS

### Meeting Structure (2 hours):

**Opening (15 min):**
- Check-in: How is everyone? What brought you here today?
- Agenda review
- Ground rules (respect, confidentiality, step up/step back)

**Content (45 min):**
- Topic for the session (e.g., "Week 1 Day 3: Money in Politics" or "Healthcare Reform ‚Äî Ch 14")
- Brief presentation (10 min): Someone summarizes the reading
- Discussion (35 min): Use discussion questions from this guide

**Strategy Session (45 min):**
- What does this mean for our local organizing?
- Brainstorm tactics
- Assign follow-up tasks

**Closing (15 min):**
- Recap decisions and action items
- Next meeting time and topic
- Check-out: One word to describe how you're feeling

### Facilitation Tips:

- **Rotate facilitator:** Build leadership, share responsibility
- **Use a stack:** Track who wants to speak (raise hands, facilitator calls on people in order)
- **Step up, step back:** If you've spoken a lot, step back. If you've been quiet, step up.
- **"Ouch" and "oops":** If someone says something hurtful, say "ouch." They should say "oops" and reflect.
- **Park it:** If discussion goes off-topic, "park" the idea for later discussion
- **Time-keep:** Appoint someone to watch time and keep group on track
