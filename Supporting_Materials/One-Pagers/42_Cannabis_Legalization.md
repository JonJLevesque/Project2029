## THE PROBLEM

**Federal cannabis prohibition is a failed policy that has inflicted 50 years of documented harm — disproportionately on Black and brown Americans — while generating zero federal revenue and failing to reduce use.**

### The Scale of Prohibition's Failure

| Metric | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| Cannabis arrests per year (peak) | **663,000** |
| Share of drug arrests that are cannabis | **43%** |
| Black Americans' arrest rate vs. white Americans | **3.73x higher** (ACLU) |
| States with medical marijuana | **38** |
| States with recreational cannabis | **24** |
| Americans who have used cannabis | **50+ million** |
| Annual cannabis market (U.S.) | **$30+ billion** |
| Federal tax revenue from cannabis | **$0** |

**Cannabis remains a Schedule I controlled substance under the Controlled Substances Act (21 U.S.C. § 812) — the same classification as heroin, and more restrictive than cocaine (Schedule II). This is not science. It is politics.**

## THE RACIAL INJUSTICE AT THE CORE

**Cannabis prohibition is not applied equally. It never was.**

The ACLU's landmark study, *A Tale of Two Countries: Racially Targeted Arrests in the Era of Marijuana Reform* (2020), found:

- Black Americans are **3.73 times more likely** to be arrested for cannabis possession than white Americans
- This disparity persists **regardless of state** — in states with legalization and without
- In some counties, the disparity exceeds **10:1**
- Black and white Americans use cannabis at **nearly identical rates**
- Black Americans represent **29% of cannabis arrests** while comprising **13% of the population**

### The Consequences of a Cannabis Arrest

A single cannabis possession conviction — even a misdemeanor — can:

| Consequence | Legal Authority Enabling It |
|-------------|----------------------------|
| Denial of federal student loans | 20 U.S.C. § 1091(r) |
| Disqualification from federal housing | 42 U.S.C. § 13661 |
| Deportation for non-citizens | 8 U.S.C. § 1227(a)(2)(B) |
| Loss of professional licenses | State law (50 states) |
| Employment background check barrier | Private employer policy |
| Loss of child custody | State family court |
| Ineligibility for federal benefits | 21 U.S.C. § 862 |

**This is not proportionality. This is permanent punishment for a nonviolent act that is now legal in half the country.**

## THE BANKING CRISIS: CANNABIS BUSINESSES CAN'T USE BANKS

**Federal prohibition creates an absurd and dangerous situation for legal cannabis businesses operating under state law.**

Under the Bank Secrecy Act and the Controlled Substances Act, a bank that processes cannabis business transactions risks:

- Federal money laundering charges (18 U.S.C. § 1956)
- Loss of FDIC insurance
- Federal asset forfeiture

**The result:** Most cannabis businesses — legal under state law — are forced to operate entirely in cash. This creates:

- Violent crime targets carrying hundreds of thousands in cash
- Payroll in cash, tax payments in cash, licensing fees in cash
- Inability to obtain business loans or lines of credit
- Accounting chaos, audit vulnerability, and employee safety risks
- IRS prohibition on standard business deductions (26 U.S.C. § 280E)

**Section 280E of the Internal Revenue Code** bars cannabis businesses from deducting ordinary business expenses — including wages, rent, and utilities — because cannabis is federally illegal. A cannabis dispensary may face an effective federal tax rate of **70%+** on gross income, while deducting nothing.

## THE LEGISLATION: WHAT HAS PASSED AND WHAT STALLED

### The MORE Act (Marijuana Opportunity, Reinvestment and Expungement Act)

- Passed the U.S. House **twice** (116th and 117th Congress)
- **Stalled in the Senate** — never received a floor vote
- Key provisions:
  - Removes cannabis from Schedule I of the CSA
  - Mandates automatic expungement of federal cannabis convictions
  - Creates Community Reinvestment Fund ($10 billion over 10 years)
  - Eliminates 21 U.S.C. § 1091(r) (student loan disqualification)
  - Removes Section 280E tax penalty
  - **Constitutional Authority:** Commerce Clause (Art. I, § 8, cl. 3); Taxing and Spending Clause (Art. I, § 8, cl. 1)

### The SAFER Banking Act (Secure and Fair Enforcement Regulation Banking Act)

- Previously known as the SAFE Banking Act
- Passed the House **seven times** — a record for any bill in the 21st Congress
- **Blocked repeatedly in the Senate** by procedural obstruction
- Key provision: Provides safe harbor to banks and financial institutions serving state-legal cannabis businesses
- **Does not** legalize cannabis — addresses only the banking prohibition
- Would immediately resolve the cash-only crisis for 30,000+ businesses

### What Full Legalization Requires

## EXPUNGEMENT: NON-NEGOTIABLE

**Federal legalization without automatic expungement is inadequate.**

Approximately **40,000 people** are currently serving federal sentences with cannabis as the primary or contributing offense. Millions more carry federal cannabis records that permanently restrict employment, housing, and civic participation.

**Automatic expungement is constitutionally grounded:**
- Congress has authority under Art. I to define and limit federal criminal records
- The President holds clemency power under Art. II, § 2 — executive action can begin immediately
- Expungement is not a pardon — it removes the record, not merely the sentence

**Day One executive action:** Issue a blanket executive order commuting sentences for federal cannabis possession and directing DOJ to initiate automatic record sealing for all prior federal simple possession convictions pending legislative expungement.

## SOCIAL EQUITY: WHO BENEFITS FROM LEGALIZATION

**The communities most harmed by prohibition must be first in line to benefit from legalization.**

Without intentional social equity provisions, the cannabis industry replicates existing patterns of racial and economic exclusion. Evidence from early-legalizing states confirms this risk:

- In California, **less than 2%** of cannabis business licenses initially went to Black-owned businesses
- Licensing fees, capital requirements, and local approval processes systematically exclude applicants from communities of color
- Large multistate operators dominate where barriers are highest

### The Social Equity Model

| Provision | Mechanism |
|-----------|-----------|
| Priority licensing | Applicants from over-policed zip codes get preference |
| Fee waivers | For applicants with prior cannabis records |
| Community Reinvestment Fund | $10B over 10 years for community development |
| Technical assistance grants | SBA-administered, equity business development |
| Local hiring requirements | Cannabis businesses hire from impacted communities |
| Ownership caps | Limit multistate operator market share |

## FEDERAL REGULATORY FRAMEWORK: THE FDA MODEL

Cannabis, once deschedule, should be regulated by the FDA similarly to alcohol — not left unregulated, not criminalized.

### Proposed Regulatory Structure

| Regulatory Domain | Federal Authority | Policy |
|------------------|------------------|--------|
| Age restriction | FDA/FTC | 21+ nationwide; no sales to minors |
| Potency labeling | FDA | Mandatory THC/CBD percentage labeling |
| Testing standards | FDA | Pesticide, contaminant, potency testing |
| Advertising | FTC/FDA | No advertising targeting minors; warning labels |
| Packaging | FDA | Child-resistant; no candy-like packaging |
| Interstate commerce | Commerce Clause | Federal licensing for interstate shipment |
| DUI standards | NHTSA/Congress | Impairment-based standards (not per-se THC limits) |

**The alcohol model works.** Alcohol is a legal, taxed, regulated product with age restrictions, labeling requirements, and impaired driving laws. Cannabis prohibition does not reduce use — it just removes regulation, taxation, and safety standards.

## VETERANS AND CANNABIS

**Veterans cannot receive VA cannabis recommendations even in states where it is fully legal.**

Under current VA policy (consistent with federal Schedule I status):
- VA physicians are **prohibited** from recommending cannabis
- Veterans who use state-legal cannabis can be **denied VA services** in some contexts
- Veterans with cannabis use **cannot receive certain federal benefits**

**The evidence:** Multiple peer-reviewed studies show cannabis effective for PTSD, chronic pain, and traumatic brain injury — conditions epidemic among veterans. The VA's own research suggests veterans use cannabis for these conditions at high rates regardless of prohibition.

**Legalization immediately resolves the VA prohibition.** VA physicians can then recommend cannabis within their standard scope of practice, consistent with state law and patient need.

## THE ARGUMENTS AND THE ANSWERS

**"This sends the wrong message to kids."**

> Prohibition sends no deterrent message to children. Cannabis use rates among youth in legal states are **not higher** than in prohibition states — in some cases they are lower, because regulated markets require ID and unregulated dealers do not. The message prohibition sends is: the law is selective, arbitrary, and racially applied.

**"States should decide this."**

> States are deciding — 24 have. But the federal government is actively undermining those decisions by blocking banking, maintaining criminal records, prohibiting VA doctors from discussing cannabis, and taxing legal businesses at punitive rates. Federal policy must align with the majority of states, not obstruct them.

**"Cannabis is a gateway drug."**

> This is not supported by the evidence. The National Academy of Sciences' *The Health Effects of Cannabis and Cannabinoids* (2017) found the gateway hypothesis is not supported by current scientific evidence. Most cannabis users do not progress to other drugs. Treating cannabis like heroin under Schedule I does not reflect science.

**"What about addiction and public health?"**

> Cannabis use disorder is real and affects approximately 9% of users. A regulated, taxed framework generates revenue for treatment — unlike prohibition, which generates none. The FDA model includes required warning labels, prohibition on advertising to minors, and regulated potency — tools prohibition lacks entirely.
