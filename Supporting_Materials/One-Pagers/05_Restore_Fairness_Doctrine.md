# Reform #5: Restore the Fairness Doctrine

## Digital Era Fairness and Accountability Act

The FCC eliminated the Fairness Doctrine in 1987 under a "marketplace of ideas" theory that assumed competitive media markets would produce balanced information. That assumption has been falsified by four decades of evidence. From Rush Limbaugh's syndicated dominance in the early 1990s to the rise of Fox News, MSNBC, and social media echo chambers, the deregulated broadcast landscape has produced partisan monocultures, not marketplace balance. Public trust in media stood at 72% in 1976 (Gallup). By 2024 it had collapsed to 32%. The democratic case for reinstatement is not that the Fairness Doctrine was perfect — it had real enforcement problems — but that the deregulated alternative has been catastrophically worse.

The legal foundation for restoration is settled. In *Red Lion Broadcasting Co. v. FCC*, 395 U.S. 367 (1969), the Supreme Court upheld the Fairness Doctrine 8-0, holding that the scarcity of broadcast spectrum licenses justifies content conditions that would be impermissible for print. Broadcast licensees hold government-granted rights under 47 U.S.C. § 301; the public interest standard encoded in 47 U.S.C. § 309(a) and the Communications Act of 1934 explicitly empowers the FCC to condition those grants on public-interest obligations. Nothing in the intervening case law overturns *Red Lion*. The doctrine was eliminated by FCC policy choice, not constitutional compulsion — and it can be restored by the same mechanism. No legislation is required to restore the Fairness Doctrine for broadcast; FCC rulemaking is sufficient.

The harder problem is the media landscape of 2025. Most Americans who consume partisan content do so through cable television and social media platforms, which were either nascent or nonexistent when the Fairness Doctrine was in force. A broadcast-only restoration would cover approximately 1,700 television and 15,000 radio licensees — significant, but insufficient to address the full architecture of the information crisis. The Digital Era Fairness and Accountability Act therefore extends accountability principles beyond over-the-air broadcast to cable news networks and major social media platforms, updates enforcement mechanisms for the digital environment, and quadruples funding for public broadcasting. Together, these measures restore the information commons that democratic self-governance requires.

The 1987 deregulation was not a passive policy choice. It was a decision to allow broadcast licensees — holders of government-granted monopoly access to scarce public spectrum — to operate without any obligation to serve the public's interest in an informed citizenry. The Reagan FCC framed this as a free-speech expansion. It was, in practice, a transfer of a public asset to private interests with no requirement that those interests serve any public purpose. The consequences are measurable and severe: a 40-point collapse in media trust over 48 years, systematic partisan audience segregation, and the proliferation of disinformation ecosystems that foreign adversaries have exploited to undermine American electoral integrity.

## The Legal Foundation: Why Broadcast Is Different

The First Amendment does not apply uniformly across all media. The Supreme Court has consistently distinguished between print, broadcast, and cable, applying different constitutional standards to each.

**Broadcast spectrum scarcity.** Unlike newspapers, there are a finite number of broadcast frequencies. Because the government must license access to a scarce public resource, it may impose public-interest conditions on those licenses. *Red Lion*, 395 U.S. at 389: "It is the right of the viewers and listeners, not the right of the broadcasters, which is paramount." The scarcity rationale survived reexamination in *FCC v. League of Women Voters*, 468 U.S. 364, 376-77 (1984).

**Public ownership of the spectrum.** Under 47 U.S.C. § 301, spectrum belongs to the United States. Broadcast licenses are not property rights; they are revocable government grants conditioned on operation "in the public interest, convenience, and necessity." 47 U.S.C. § 309(a). A licensee who accepts a government grant of access to a scarce public resource accepts the conditions attached to that grant.

**Cable and social media distinctions.** Cable news networks do not use public spectrum and therefore cannot be regulated under the same scarcity theory. Cable and social media accountability provisions in the Digital Era Fairness and Accountability Act rest on distinct authorities: the FTC's jurisdiction over unfair or deceptive acts and practices (15 U.S.C. § 45), the FCC's authority over cable operators (47 U.S.C. §§ 521-573), and Congress's Commerce Clause power (U.S. Const. art. I, § 8, cl. 3) to regulate large-scale commercial communications platforms.

**Section 230 is not a barrier.** Opponents argue that 47 U.S.C. § 230 immunizes social media platforms from content-based obligations. That argument misconstrues the statute. Section 230(c)(1) immunizes platforms from liability for third-party content; it does not immunize platforms from prospective regulatory obligations governing how their own algorithmic systems amplify or suppress that content. Algorithmic curation is platform conduct, not third-party speech. Congress retains full authority to regulate it.

## The Reform: Digital Era Fairness and Accountability Act

### Part I: Restore the Fairness Doctrine for Broadcast

The FCC shall reinstate Fairness Doctrine obligations for all broadcast licensees — television and radio — operating under licenses granted pursuant to 47 U.S.C. § 301.

**Balanced Coverage Requirement.** Broadcast licensees presenting programming on controversial issues of public importance must afford reasonable opportunity for contrasting viewpoints. This does not require equal time on every issue, but it prohibits licensees from systematically presenting a single ideological perspective on disputed political questions while blocking access to opposing views.

**Opinion Labeling Requirement.** Broadcast content that represents editorial opinion, advocacy, or commentary — rather than factual reporting — must be conspicuously identified as such at the segment's start and end. News divisions must maintain editorial independence from opinion programming and may not present opinion content under news branding.

**Correction Requirement.** When a broadcast outlet disseminates a material factual error, it must broadcast a correction of equal duration and prominence within 24 hours of identifying the error. The correction must clearly identify the original claim and the accurate information.

**Enforcement.** The FCC shall impose civil monetary penalties of $100,000 per violation of balanced coverage and correction requirements. Egregious or repeated violations — including systematic patterns of one-sided presentation or willful failure to correct material errors — shall constitute grounds for license revocation or non-renewal under 47 U.S.C. § 312.

### Part II: Cable News Accountability Standards

Cable news networks reaching more than five million subscribers are not broadcast licensees and are not subject to spectrum-scarcity obligations. The Digital Era Fairness and Accountability Act imposes accountability standards on these networks through the FTC's deceptive practices jurisdiction and Congress's Commerce Clause authority.

**Accuracy-in-Labeling Requirement.** Any cable network operating under a "news" brand must maintain a factually accurate, editorially independent news division that constitutes a minimum of 30% of its daily programming. Opinion, commentary, and advocacy programming must be labeled in on-screen graphics throughout the segment. Networks that cannot meet the 30% news threshold must rebrand to remove the word "news" from their network name within 18 months of enactment.

**Fact-Check and Correction Requirement.** Cable news networks must establish internal standards-and-practices functions responsible for pre-broadcast fact review of scripted news content and post-broadcast correction. Corrections must be broadcast with equal prominence within 24 hours and must be logged in a publicly accessible database maintained by the FCC.

**Enforcement.** The FCC and FTC, exercising joint jurisdiction over cable news networks, shall impose civil monetary penalties of $500,000 per violation of labeling and correction requirements. Repeated, willful violations shall be referred to the FTC for investigation as unfair or deceptive acts and practices under 15 U.S.C. § 45. The FCC may seek injunctive relief in federal district court.

### Part III: Social Media Platform Accountability

Major social media platforms — defined as platforms with more than 100 million monthly active users in the United States — exercise editorial power through algorithmic curation. When a platform selects, ranks, amplifies, or suppresses content through algorithmic processes, it functions as a publisher for purposes of regulatory accountability.

**Algorithm Transparency Requirement.** Covered platforms must publish, in machine-readable form, the material parameters governing content ranking and amplification for political and news content. Platforms must provide users a chronological feed option free of engagement-based algorithmic ranking. Material changes to ranking algorithms affecting political content must be disclosed to the FCC within 30 days of deployment.

**Amplification Balance Requirement.** Platforms may not deploy algorithmic systems that systematically amplify content from one political party or viewpoint while suppressing equivalent content from competing parties or viewpoints. Platforms must conduct and publish quarterly audits of political content amplification rates, with results disaggregated by political affiliation of content source.

**Disinformation Response Requirement.** Platforms must label verified disinformation — defined as claims contradicted by the consensus of peer-reviewed scientific evidence or official government data — within 24 hours of flagging by platform review systems or FCC notification. Organized inauthentic behavior networks and foreign-influence operations must be disclosed publicly upon identification.

**Enforcement.** Violations of transparency and amplification requirements are subject to civil monetary penalties of up to 1% of the platform's annual global revenue per violation. The FCC and FTC exercise joint oversight; either agency may initiate enforcement proceedings. The Act creates a private right of action for political candidates and parties who can demonstrate material harm from algorithm manipulation.

### Part IV: Public Broadcasting Restoration

The Corporation for Public Broadcasting (CPB), established under the Public Broadcasting Act of 1967 (47 U.S.C. §§ 396-398), receives approximately $535 million in annual federal appropriations — less than $2 per American per year. This is structurally inadequate for public media to function as a counterweight to commercially-driven partisan content.

**Funding.** The Digital Era Fairness and Accountability Act increases CPB's annual appropriation from approximately $535 million to $2 billion in Year 1, with a four-year phase-in to $8 billion by Year 4. This brings the United States in line with comparable democracies: Canada allocates approximately $26 per capita annually to public broadcasting; the United Kingdom allocates approximately $86 per capita through the BBC.

**Editorial Independence Mandate.** Increased CPB funding shall be accompanied by statutory protections against political interference in editorial decisions. CPB board appointments shall require Senate confirmation and shall be staggered to prevent any single administration from achieving majority control within a single four-year term.

**Local Station Grants.** Twenty percent of increased CPB funding shall flow directly to local public television and radio stations in underserved communities, prioritizing stations serving rural and low-income audiences where commercial news deserts have expanded most severely.

## What Opponents Argue

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "This violates the First Amendment." | *Red Lion* held 8-0 in 1969 that the Fairness Doctrine is constitutional. Broadcasters use government-granted licenses to access public spectrum; they accept public-interest conditions as a term of that grant. The First Amendment does not prohibit conditions on government-issued licenses to scarce public resources. |
| "Who decides what is 'balanced'?" | The FCC, with public notice-and-comment rulemaking and judicial review — the same mechanism that governs all broadcast regulation. "Balanced" does not mean equal time for fringe positions; it means reasonable opportunity for contrasting mainstream viewpoints on genuinely contested political questions. Courts review for viewpoint neutrality. |
| "This targets conservative media." | The doctrine applies to all broadcast licensees regardless of ideological orientation. MSNBC affiliates using broadcast spectrum are subject to the same requirements as Fox-affiliated stations. The obligation is viewpoint-neutral: present contrasting views, or risk license consequences. |
| "This amounts to government-controlled media." | Government-controlled media dictates content. The Fairness Doctrine dictates process: present multiple perspectives, label opinion accurately, correct material errors. Licensees retain full editorial discretion over what issues to cover and how to frame them, within those procedural constraints. |
| "The 'marketplace of ideas' makes this unnecessary." | The marketplace-of-ideas theory was the Reagan FCC's justification in 1987. Forty years of data have refuted it. A deregulated market produced systematic partisan segregation, not balance. Trust in media collapsed from 72% to 32%. The market failure is the argument for regulation, not against it. |
| "Social media regulation will chill speech." | Transparency and amplification-balance requirements do not prohibit any speech. They require platforms to disclose how their editorial algorithms operate and to refrain from systematically suppressing one political viewpoint. This is comparable to requiring broadcasters to disclose their ownership — not censorship. |

## International Comparisons

The United States is an outlier among peer democracies in its absence of broadcast content standards and its underinvestment in public media. Every comparable country maintains both.

| Country | Broadcast Content Standard | Public Media Funding (per capita/year) | Media Trust (Reuters Institute, 2024) |
|---------|---------------------------|----------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| United Kingdom | Ofcom "due impartiality" standard; applies to all licensed broadcasters including BBC | ~$86 | 40% |
| Canada | CRTC balance requirements; Canadian Broadcasting Standards Council | ~$26 | 42% |
| Germany | Rundfunkstaatsvertrag plurality requirements; public broadcaster ARD/ZDF mandatory | ~$100 | 47% |
| France | Conseil Superieur de l'Audiovisuel balance standards | ~$75 | 36% |
| United States | No broadcast content standards (post-1987); no cable standards | ~$1.60 | 32% |

The correlation is not dispositive — media trust is shaped by many factors — but the pattern is consistent. Every country that maintains broadcast content standards and funds robust public media achieves higher baseline trust scores than the deregulated American model. The United States pays approximately $1.60 per capita annually for public broadcasting and receives, in return, the developed world's lowest media trust rating.

## Fiscal Impact

**Enforcement costs** (FCC/FTC): Approximately $200 million annually, covering 200 additional FCC monitoring and enforcement staff, technology systems for broadcast and cable compliance monitoring, FCC/FTC joint coordination infrastructure, and a public complaint investigation unit.

**Public broadcasting expansion**: Phase-in from $535 million to $8 billion over four years. The Year 4 target of $8 billion represents approximately 0.1% of federal discretionary spending and brings per-capita public broadcasting investment to approximately $24 — below Canada and a fraction of Germany and the UK, but a transformative improvement over the current baseline.

**Partial revenue offsets**: FCC civil monetary penalties from broadcast and cable enforcement are estimated to generate $500 million to $1 billion annually at steady-state enforcement levels, based on comparable penalty structures in FCC spectrum enforcement actions. Social media penalties of up to 1% of annual global revenue could generate substantial additional sums; a single enforcement action against a major platform could exceed $1 billion.

**Net cost at full implementation**: Approximately $6.7 billion annually — the cost of building and maintaining the information infrastructure that democratic self-governance requires.

## Implementation Timeline

### Day One Executive Actions

- Direct the FCC chair to initiate notice-and-proposed-rulemaking (NPRM) to reinstate Fairness Doctrine obligations for broadcast licensees under existing Communications Act authority, citing *Red Lion* and 47 U.S.C. § 309(a).
- Direct the FTC chair to issue guidance on the scope of deceptive-practices jurisdiction over cable news networks' accuracy-in-labeling obligations.
- Direct the Office of Management and Budget to submit a supplemental appropriations request for CPB funding restoration.

### First 100 Days

- Submit the Digital Era Fairness and Accountability Act to Congress.
- Complete FCC NPRM comment period; publish final rule reinstating Fairness Doctrine for broadcast licensees.
- Nominate FCC commissioners committed to public-interest enforcement; submit nominations to Senate.
- Issue Executive Order directing all federal agency media-relations offices to provide equal access to press inquiries from outlets across the political spectrum.
- Direct the FCC to publish a public complaint portal for Fairness Doctrine violations and an annual compliance report for all broadcast licensees.
- Submit supplemental appropriations request to Congress for Year 1 CPB funding increase to $2 billion.

### Year 1: Foundation

- Enact Digital Era Fairness and Accountability Act.
- FCC publishes implementing regulations for broadcast Fairness Doctrine (within 90 days of enactment); regulations must include specific definitions of "controversial issue of public importance," "reasonable opportunity," and "material factual error" to provide clear compliance standards and minimize chilling effect on legitimate editorial discretion.
- FCC and FTC publish joint regulations governing cable news accountability standards (within 120 days of enactment), including the 30% news programming measurement methodology.
- FCC publishes social media platform transparency regulations (within 180 days of enactment); platforms must file initial algorithm transparency disclosures within 90 days of final regulation.
- CPB receives first-year appropriation increase; issues local station grant applications.
- FCC establishes Fairness Doctrine compliance division; hires 200 additional monitoring and enforcement staff.
- Department of Education issues draft standards for K-12 media literacy curriculum under the Media Literacy and Civic Information Act.

### Years 2-4: Enforcement and Consolidation

- First Fairness Doctrine compliance cycle: FCC reviews broadcast license renewal applications for documented compliance; first license non-renewals issued for documented violations.
- First cable news accountability enforcement actions: FCC/FTC impose civil monetary penalties on networks with documented labeling violations; penalties become public record.
- First social media platform algorithm transparency reports published; FCC/FTC review for amplification-balance compliance and issue initial enforcement findings.
- CPB funding reaches $8 billion annual target by Year 4; 500+ local public broadcasting stations in rural and underserved markets receive expansion grants.
- License revocation proceedings initiated against egregious or repeat violators, with public notice and opportunity for hearing under 47 U.S.C. § 312(c).
- FCC publishes first annual media accountability report to Congress documenting compliance rates, enforcement actions, and consumer complaint outcomes.

## Key Legislation

**Digital Era Fairness and Accountability Act**
- **Purpose:** Restore public-interest content obligations to broadcast licensees; extend accountability standards to cable news and major social media platforms; restore public broadcasting as a viable information commons.
- **Key Provisions:** Fairness Doctrine reinstatement under 47 U.S.C. § 309(a); cable news accuracy-in-labeling with 30% news programming floor; social media algorithm transparency and amplification-balance audit requirements; CPB four-year appropriation phase-in from $535 million to $8 billion; FCC civil penalty authority of $100,000 per broadcast violation and $500,000 per cable news violation; FCC/FTC joint jurisdiction over cable and social media; license revocation authority for egregious violations under 47 U.S.C. § 312.
- **Constitutional Authority:** U.S. Const. art. I, § 8, cl. 3 (Commerce Clause); Communications Act of 1934; 47 U.S.C. §§ 301, 309(a), 312, 503(b); *Red Lion Broadcasting Co. v. FCC*, 395 U.S. 367 (1969).

**CPB Independence and Funding Security Act**
- **Purpose:** Insulate public broadcasting from political interference by restructuring CPB governance and creating multi-year funding certainty.
- **Key Provisions:** Senate-confirmed CPB board with staggered terms; prohibition on presidential removal of CPB board members except for cause; advance two-year funding authorization to prevent annual appropriations leverage; 20% local station grant set-aside.
- **Constitutional Authority:** U.S. Const. art. I, § 8, cl. 3; Public Broadcasting Act of 1967, 47 U.S.C. §§ 396-398.

**Media Literacy and Civic Information Act**
- **Purpose:** Fund K-12 and adult media literacy education as a complement to structural media accountability reforms.
- **Key Provisions:** $500 million annual grant program for media literacy curriculum development; state-matching requirements for federal media literacy education grants; Department of Education standards for evaluating media credibility; integration with Civic Education requirements under the American Civic Renewal Act (Reform #19).
- **Constitutional Authority:** U.S. Const. art. I, § 8, cl. 1 (General Welfare Clause); 20 U.S.C. § 1221 (Department of Education's general authority over federal education programs).

## Talking Points

> Broadcast licenses are government grants to use public property. The airwaves belong to the American people under 47 U.S.C. § 301. A company that accepts a government-granted license to scarce public spectrum accepts the conditions attached to that grant. The Fairness Doctrine is not censorship — it is a condition of access to public property. The Supreme Court said so unanimously in 1969.

> The Reagan FCC eliminated the Fairness Doctrine in 1987 based on a theory — that competitive media markets would produce balanced information — that has been comprehensively falsified by four decades of evidence. Public trust in media collapsed from 72% to 32%. Political polarization reached post-Civil War levels. When a deregulation experiment fails this completely, the responsible policy response is to reverse it.

> The Digital Era Fairness and Accountability Act applies equally to all broadcast licensees regardless of ideological orientation. An MSNBC affiliate that broadcasts on public spectrum is subject to the same obligations as a Fox affiliate. The requirement is viewpoint-neutral: present contrasting perspectives on contested political issues, or face consequences under the license terms you accepted. Partisans on both sides who want to use public airwaves accept public-interest obligations.

> The United States spends approximately $1.60 per person per year on public broadcasting. Canada spends $26 per capita. The United Kingdom spends $86 per capita. Every major democracy maintains a well-funded public broadcasting system as infrastructure for democratic discourse. Project 2029's proposal to raise CPB funding to $8 billion annually would bring the United States to approximately $24 per capita — still below Canada, still a fraction of the UK, but sufficient to rebuild public broadcasting as a genuine alternative to commercially-driven partisan content.

> Restoring the Fairness Doctrine does not require the government to dictate editorial content. Broadcast licensees retain full discretion over which issues to cover, which guests to book, and how to frame their reporting. The doctrine requires only that they afford reasonable opportunity for contrasting viewpoints on controversial public issues — and that they label opinion as opinion. Democracy does not require perfect balance. It requires that citizens be able to distinguish news from advocacy, and that they have access to more than one perspective on the public questions they must decide as voters.
