## WHAT IS EXCLUSIONARY ZONING?

**Exclusionary zoning** is the use of local land-use regulations to prohibit housing types that would lower the cost of living in a neighborhood.

### The Standard American Zoning Map

In most U.S. cities, local zoning law divides residential land into roughly these categories:

| Zone Type | What Is Allowed | Share of Residential Land |
|-----------|----------------|--------------------------|
| Single-family residential (R-1) | One house per lot — nothing else | **75%+** |
| Low-density residential (R-2) | Duplexes and small multifamily | 10-15% |
| Medium-density residential | Small apartment buildings | 5-10% |
| High-density/mixed-use | Large apartments, commercial | 3-5% |

**In cities like Seattle, Los Angeles, Charlotte, and Minneapolis (pre-reform), 75-85% of residential land was zoned exclusively for detached single-family homes.** Building an apartment, a duplex, a townhouse, or an accessory dwelling unit in those zones was — and in most cities remains — a criminal code violation.

### What Exclusionary Zoning Prohibits

In a typical single-family zone:
- Duplexes: **Illegal**
- Triplexes: **Illegal**
- Townhouses: **Illegal**
- Small apartment buildings: **Illegal**
- Accessory Dwelling Units (ADUs/granny flats): **Illegal or heavily restricted**
- Mixed-use (residential above commercial): **Illegal**
- Affordable housing developments: **Blocked by density limits**

## THE "MISSING MIDDLE": THE SOLUTION THAT WAS OUTLAWED

**Missing middle housing** refers to duplexes, triplexes, fourplexes, townhouses, courtyard apartments, and small apartment buildings — building types that are affordable to produce, affordable to rent, and compatible in scale with existing neighborhoods.

This category of housing is called "missing" because it was **built in abundance before World War II** — every older American city has pre-war duplexes, three-deckers, and courtyard buildings that are now legally impossible to replicate. After World War II, single-family zoning spread nationwide and made missing middle construction illegal in most residential land.

### Why Missing Middle Is the Solution

| Attribute | Result |
|-----------|--------|
| Lower construction cost per unit | More affordable without subsidy |
| No elevator or structured parking required | Below the "luxury threshold" |
| Compatible with neighborhood character | Lower political resistance |
| Can be owner-occupied with rental unit | Builds wealth for small landlords |
| Dispersed throughout neighborhoods | Avoids concentrated poverty |
| Faster to permit and build | Quicker supply response |

**The math is simple:** A single-family lot that produces one $600,000 home can, under duplex zoning, produce two homes — reducing the per-unit cost substantially while adding supply to a constrained market.

## THE RACIAL HISTORY OF ZONING

**Exclusionary zoning was not a neutral planning decision. It was a tool of racial segregation.**

The historical record is explicit:

- **Berkeley, California (1916):** The first residential single-family zoning ordinance in the United States was enacted specifically to keep a Black business owner from expanding into a white neighborhood. This is not disputed by historians.
- **Euclid v. Ambler Realty Co., 272 U.S. 365 (1926):** The Supreme Court upheld zoning as constitutional. The brief supporting Ambler Realty argued explicitly that zoning was needed to protect white neighborhoods from "colored people."
- **Federal Housing Administration (1934-1968):** FHA underwriting manuals rated neighborhoods as uncreditworthy if they were racially mixed or near minority communities — "redlining" — and simultaneously required restrictive covenants excluding non-white residents in neighborhoods that received FHA-backed loans.
- **Post-war suburban expansion:** Federal subsidies for the Interstate Highway System and FHA-backed mortgages funded white flight to single-family suburbs. Black Americans were systematically excluded from those mortgages by FHA policy.

**The result:** American residential segregation was not produced by private prejudice alone — it was constructed by government policy. Single-family zoning was the legal mechanism that locked that segregation in place and made it self-perpetuating.

**Dismantling exclusionary zoning is a civil rights issue.** The Fair Housing Act of 1968 (42 U.S.C. § 3604) prohibits housing discrimination. The "Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing" (AFFH) mandate (42 U.S.C. § 3608) requires HUD and its grantees to actively undo segregation — not merely avoid discrimination. Exclusionary zoning that perpetuates segregation is inconsistent with the AFFH mandate.

## FEDERAL LEVERAGE: HUD'S $50 BILLION IN ANNUAL GRANTS

**The federal government provides over $50 billion annually to states and localities through HUD programs.** This is the constitutional lever.

| HUD Grant Program | Annual Funding |
|------------------|---------------|
| Community Development Block Grants (CDBG) | $3.3 billion |
| HOME Investment Partnerships | $1.5 billion |
| Choice Neighborhoods | $175 million |
| Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers | $28 billion |
| Public Housing Capital Fund | $3.2 billion |
| Emergency Solutions Grants | $290 million |
| Transportation funding (USDOT) | $10+ billion (transit) |
| **Total federal housing/transit grants** | **$50+ billion** |

**The Spending Clause (Art. I, § 8, cl. 1) authorizes Congress to condition federal grants on compliance with federal policy requirements.** This is established law. *South Dakota v. Dole*, 483 U.S. 203 (1987) upheld highway funding conditions requiring minimum drinking ages. *Rust v. Sullivan*, 500 U.S. 173 (1991) upheld funding conditions on speech. Spending Clause conditions are constitutional when: (1) they pursue the general welfare; (2) they are unambiguous; (3) they relate to the federal interest; (4) they do not require unconstitutional conduct.

Conditioning HUD grants on zoning reform satisfies all four prongs.

## WHAT THE BIDEN ADMINISTRATION DID — AND WHAT TRUMP REVERSED

### The Biden HUD Initiative (2023)

HUD under Secretary Marcia Fudge issued guidance requiring recipients of certain HUD grants to:
- Demonstrate reduction of regulatory barriers to housing supply
- Affirmatively certify compliance with AFFH obligations
- Submit Housing Supply Action Plans as condition of CDBG and HOME grants

**This was the first meaningful federal use of Spending Clause leverage on exclusionary zoning in American history.**

### Trump Administration Reversal (2025)

The Trump administration, consistent with Project 2025's explicit hostility to federal housing equity requirements:
- Suspended the AFFH rule (first reversed by Trump in 2020, reinstated by Biden, reversed again in 2025)
- Withdrew HUD guidance on zoning reform conditions
- Eliminated HUD's fair housing enforcement capacity through staffing cuts
- Directed HUD to deprioritize "social engineering" in housing grant conditions

**Project 2025 explicitly called for eliminating the AFFH mandate** *(Project 2025, p. 481)*: the document characterizes fair housing enforcement as federal overreach into local land-use decisions — precisely because it is the mechanism by which federal policy can dismantle exclusionary zoning.

## REFORM MODELS THAT WORK

### Minneapolis, Minnesota (2018)

Minneapolis became the first major American city to **eliminate single-family-only zoning citywide**, legalizing duplexes and triplexes on all residential lots.

**Results:**
- Housing permits increased substantially
- Rent increases slowed relative to peer cities
- New units built across income ranges, including unsubsidized affordable units
- No neighborhood character collapse — scale-compatible infill

### Oregon (2019)

Oregon passed **House Bill 2001** — the first statewide missing middle legalization in the United States.
- Legalized duplexes in all cities over 10,000 population
- Legalized fourplexes and townhouses in cities over 25,000
- Required cities to permit ADUs statewide
- Result: Thousands of new missing middle permits issued within 24 months

### California ADU Reform (2017-2020)

California enacted a series of laws dramatically reducing barriers to **Accessory Dwelling Units** (ADUs — secondary units on single-family lots, also called granny flats or in-law units).

- Eliminated local permit delays and excessive fees
- Prohibited owner-occupancy requirements
- Streamlined permitting to ministerial approval

**Result:** ADU permits statewide went from **5,000 in 2017 to 28,000 in 2022 — a 460% increase.** More than 80,000 ADU permits issued in California in the three years following reform. These units added supply with no land acquisition cost, no public subsidy, and no neighborhood disruption.

### New Zealand (2021)

New Zealand's National Policy Statement on Urban Development required all major cities to allow six-story mixed-use development near transit stations and three-story development citywide. Early results show significant permit increases and slowing rent growth.

## TRANSIT-ORIENTED DEVELOPMENT: CONNECTING HOUSING TO TRANSPORTATION FUNDING

**The federal government spends $10+ billion annually on transit infrastructure through the Federal Transit Administration (FTA).** Federal transit grants fund light rail, subway expansions, bus rapid transit, and commuter rail — infrastructure that dramatically increases land value within walking distance of stations.

**The policy failure:** Federal transit dollars fund infrastructure that enriches landowners, while local zoning prohibits the housing development that would allow workers to live near transit.

**The reform:** Condition FTA Capital Investment Grants on local adoption of transit-oriented development (TOD) zoning requirements:

| Distance from Transit Station | Required Zoning |
|------------------------------|----------------|
| 0-1/4 mile (walking core) | Minimum 15-story mixed-use by-right |
| 1/4-1/2 mile | Minimum 6-story residential by-right |
| 1/2-1 mile | Minimum 3-story multifamily by-right |
| Beyond 1 mile | Local discretion |

**"By-right" is critical:** TOD requirements must prohibit local discretionary review (environmental review, design review, neighborhood input processes) that can indefinitely delay or kill individual projects. The zoning change must create automatic approval — not a pathway to more committee review.

## THE FEDERAL ANTI-NIMBY ZONING REFORM ACT: WHAT IT WOULD DO

### Core Provisions

**1. HUD Grant Conditions (Spending Clause)**
- Condition CDBG, HOME, and Choice Neighborhoods grants on adoption of:
  - Missing middle legalization (duplexes/triplexes in all residential zones)
  - ADU legalization with ministerial permitting
  - Parking minimum elimination within 1/2 mile of transit
  - By-right permitting for affordable housing developments
  - Anti-exclusionary zoning certification

**2. FTA Grant Conditions (Spending Clause)**
- Condition Capital Investment Grants on TOD zoning within 1/2 mile of all new and existing fixed-guideway transit

**3. AFFH Restoration**
- Restore the AFFH rule (42 U.S.C. § 3608) requiring grantees to affirmatively further fair housing
- Require AFFH certification include zoning reform as measurable metric
- Mandate HUD fair housing testing and enforcement

**4. Zoning Atlas and Federal Data**
- Fund a national zoning atlas (building on Lincoln Institute of Land Policy work) to document exclusionary zoning patterns across all U.S. jurisdictions
- Require grant recipients to publicly report zoning maps, permit data, and housing production by type

**5. Preemption for Interstate Commerce (Commerce Clause)**
- Where local zoning demonstrably restricts the interstate housing market, authorize federal preemption of specific exclusionary provisions
- Apply *Gonzales v. Raich* (2005) interstate commerce analysis to housing markets

**6. HUD Pro-Housing Community Designation and Incentive Grants**
- Create competitive grant category for jurisdictions that exceed zoning reform thresholds
- $5 billion annually in Pro-Housing Community Grants (modeled on Biden executive order EO 14097, May 2023)

## WHAT NIMBY ARGUMENTS MISS

**"This will change the character of our neighborhood."**

> The character of your neighborhood is defined by the people who live there, not the number of units per building. Pre-war neighborhoods with three-deckers and courtyard apartments are among the most beloved urban environments in America. "Neighborhood character" is frequently a euphemism for exclusion.

**"Local zoning is a local issue — the federal government shouldn't be involved."**

> The federal government has been deeply involved in shaping local zoning since 1926, when the Commerce Department published the Standard State Zoning Enabling Act that every state adopted. The FHA's redlining policies shaped local land use for 40 years. The Interstate Highway System dictated suburban development patterns. Federal involvement created the problem. Federal leverage is appropriate to address it.

**"New housing just becomes luxury housing."**

> This reflects a misunderstanding of supply and demand. New market-rate housing, even expensive, absorbs high-income households who would otherwise compete for existing units — reducing pressure throughout the market. Studies in cities including Minneapolis, Seattle, and San Francisco confirm that new market-rate development reduces rents in surrounding neighborhoods. The alternative — building nothing — produces only continued rent increases.

**"My home values will go down."**

> The evidence does not support this. Studies of ADU legalization, missing middle zoning reform, and transit-oriented development find no statistically significant negative effect on adjacent single-family home values. In many cases, values increase as neighborhoods become more walkable and amenity-rich.

**"We don't have the infrastructure."**

> Infrastructure capacity should be planned and funded to meet demand, not used as a perpetual excuse to block supply. Federal infrastructure grants, including those for water, sewer, and transit, can be conditioned on housing production targets.

## THE BOTTOM LINE

**For 75+ years, American cities have made it illegal to build the housing their residents need. Single-family-only zoning covering the majority of residential land is not a natural feature of American cities — it is a post-war regulatory choice, deeply rooted in racial exclusion, that has compounded into a national crisis. Median home prices are now 7 times median income. Half of all renters are cost-burdened. Workers cannot afford to live near the jobs that need them.**

**The federal government did not create this crisis through inaction — it created it through active policy: FHA redlining, highway subsidies, and decades of complicity with exclusionary zoning. It can use its $50 billion in annual housing and transit grants to demand the opposite: legalize the missing middle, permit accessory dwelling units by right, require transit-oriented development near federally funded transit, and restore the AFFH mandate that requires grantees to actively dismantle segregation.**

**This is not a war on homeowners. It is an insistence that housing markets must work for everyone — not just those who already own.**
