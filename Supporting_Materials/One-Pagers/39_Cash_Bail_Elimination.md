## The Core Injustice in One Sentence

On any given day in the United States, more than 400,000 people sit in jail cells — not because a judge determined they were dangerous, not because they were convicted of a crime — but because they could not afford to pay bail.

---

## What Cash Bail Is and How It Works

Cash bail is a financial condition of pretrial release. When a person is arrested and charged with a crime, a judge sets a dollar amount the defendant must pay to be released while awaiting trial. If the defendant cannot pay, they remain in jail until their case is resolved — which can take weeks, months, or years.

The constitutional premise of bail is to ensure a defendant appears at trial. The practical reality of cash bail is a wealth test: the same charge, the same defendant risk profile, the same community ties — but two radically different outcomes determined solely by the balance in a bank account.

| Scenario | Defendant A | Defendant B |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| Charge | Misdemeanor theft | Misdemeanor theft |
| Risk assessment | Low | Low |
| Bail set | $1,000 | $1,000 |
| Savings available | $1,000 | $0 |
| Outcome | Goes home | Stays in jail |
| Constitutional status | Presumed innocent | Presumed innocent |

Both defendants are presumed innocent under the 5th and 14th Amendments. Only one of them is treated that way.

## The Wealth Test in Practice

### Same Charge. Same Risk. Different Outcome.

The 8th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution states: "Excessive bail shall not be required." The Founding Framers understood that bail could be weaponized as a tool of pre-conviction punishment. What they did not anticipate — or at minimum, did not adequately prevent — was a system in which the entire mechanism of bail would function as a proxy for wealth.

A defendant who can pay $10,000 walks out of the courthouse and returns to their job, their family, their home. A defendant who cannot pay $500 sits in a cell. The difference is not dangerousness. The difference is not risk of flight. The difference is money.

For the roughly 63 percent of defendants who cannot make bail, the consequences begin immediately:

**Employment:** A 2017 study published in the American Economic Review found that pretrial detention reduced defendants' annual earnings by 24 percent — not because of the eventual case outcome, but because of the detention itself. Employers do not hold jobs for defendants who cannot report to work because they are in jail.

**Housing:** Lease agreements require rent payments. Rent cannot be paid from a jail cell. Eviction proceedings begin. For defendants in unstable housing situations, pretrial detention can mean permanent displacement.

**Child custody:** Family courts treat incarceration — even pretrial incarceration — as a factor in custody determinations. Parents who cannot make bail risk losing custody of their children before a single fact in their criminal case has been adjudicated.

**Plea pressure:** Research consistently shows that pretrial detainees are significantly more likely to accept guilty pleas — including to charges they may not have committed — because a guilty plea to time served means immediate release, while fighting the charge means continued detention. The Vera Institute of Justice documented that pretrial detention increases the likelihood of a guilty plea by 25 percent and increases sentence length by 42 percent.

## The Racial Dimension

### Wealth-Based Detention Is Race-Based Detention

The cash bail system does not operate in a racially neutral manner. Decades of documented disparity reveal that Black and Latino defendants are subjected to higher bail amounts than white defendants charged with identical offenses and presenting identical risk profiles.

| Racial Disparity Metric | Finding | Source |
|------------------------|---------|--------|
| Bail amounts set for Black defendants vs. white defendants | 35% higher for same charge | Arnold Ventures, 2020 |
| Bail amounts set for Latino defendants vs. white defendants | 19% higher for same charge | Arnold Ventures, 2020 |
| Black defendants unable to afford bail | Approximately 2x the rate of white defendants | Pretrial Justice Institute |
| Share of pretrial jail population that is Black or Latino | Over 65% | BJS, 2022 |

This disparity compounds every downstream consequence. Black and Latino defendants are more likely to be detained pretrial, more likely to lose employment and housing as a result, more likely to accept plea deals under coercive detention pressure, and more likely to receive longer sentences — a cascade initiated by a wealth test that operates with racial precision.

## Who Profits: The Bail Bond Industry

### A $2 Billion Industry Built on Pretrial Detention

When a defendant cannot pay bail in cash, they can turn to a commercial bail bondsman. The bondsman posts the full bail amount in exchange for a non-refundable fee — typically 10 percent of the bail. The defendant's family pays this fee and receives nothing back regardless of case outcome.

This is a $2 billion per year industry in the United States. No other developed democracy operates a commercial bail bond system. In the United States, it is a politically powerful lobbying force with a direct financial interest in maintaining high bail amounts and blocking reform.

The American Bail Coalition and state-level bail industry associations have spent millions of dollars lobbying against bail reform legislation, funding opposition research, and financing campaigns against reform-minded prosecutors and legislators. Their business model requires pretrial detention. Reform threatens their revenue. Their lobbying must be understood in that context.

## Proven Alternatives: Risk Assessment and Supervised Release

### Reform Does Not Mean Releasing Dangerous People

The primary argument against cash bail elimination — that removing financial conditions will allow dangerous defendants to be released into the community — misunderstands the alternative. Elimination of cash bail does not mean elimination of conditions of pretrial release. It means replacing a wealth test with an evidence-based risk assessment.

**Public Safety Assessment (PSA):** Developed by Arnold Ventures, the PSA uses nine validated factors — including age, current charge, prior convictions, prior failure to appear — to generate a risk score for each defendant. Judges use this score to determine whether a defendant should be released, and under what conditions. The PSA does not use race, income, or neighborhood as factors.

**Supervised release:** Defendants assessed as moderate or high risk can be released under supervision conditions including regular check-ins with pretrial services officers, drug testing, travel restrictions, and GPS monitoring. High-risk defendants posing genuine danger to the community can be held through preventive detention — a separate legal mechanism that does not depend on inability to pay.

**Electronic monitoring:** GPS ankle monitoring provides courts with real-time location data for defendants released pending trial, addressing flight risk concerns without incarceration.

## Jurisdictions That Have Eliminated Cash Bail

### The Evidence Is In: Reform Works

The empirical evidence from jurisdictions that have eliminated or substantially reduced reliance on cash bail is consistent: jail populations fall, court appearance rates are maintained or improved, and crime does not increase.

| Jurisdiction | Reform | Year | Result |
|-------------|--------|------|--------|
| Washington, D.C. | Near-complete elimination; risk-based release | 1990s | 88% of released defendants do not re-arrest; court appearance rate exceeds 90% |
| New Jersey | Bail reform eliminated cash bail for most charges; PSA adopted statewide | 2017 | 44% drop in pretrial jail population; no increase in crime rates; court appearance rates maintained |
| Kentucky | PSA adopted statewide; reduced cash bail reliance | 2013 | Improved appearance rates; reduced pretrial detention costs |
| Illinois | Complete elimination of cash bail statewide | 2023 | First full state elimination; implementation ongoing; initial data shows no crime increase |
| Harris County, TX | Consent decree reformed misdemeanor bail | 2019 | Significant reduction in pretrial detention; no public safety deterioration |

New Jersey's 2017 reform is the most rigorously studied. A 2019 study by the Drug Policy Alliance and Luminosity found that after reform, New Jersey's jail population fell by 44 percent, crime rates did not increase, and court appearance rates were maintained above 90 percent. The bail bond industry predicted catastrophe. The evidence documented success.

## Constitutional and Legal Framework

### The 8th Amendment: Excessive Bail

The 8th Amendment prohibition on excessive bail has been interpreted by federal courts to prohibit bail set at an amount higher than necessary to ensure appearance at trial. Stack v. Bowen, 342 U.S. 1 (1951) established that bail must not be used as an instrument of oppression and cannot be set at a figure higher than necessary.

The constitutional problem with cash bail is not that judges set amounts that are individually excessive — it is that any amount is effectively excessive for a defendant who cannot pay it, while the same amount is trivially low for a defendant with resources. The 8th Amendment's protection becomes income-contingent, which is constitutionally untenable.

### The 14th Amendment: Equal Protection

The 14th Amendment prohibits states from denying any person equal protection of the laws. San Antonio Independent School District v. Rodriguez, 411 U.S. 1 (1973) established that wealth classifications trigger rational basis review. However, when wealth-based distinctions result in deprivation of a fundamental liberty interest — freedom from pretrial incarceration — heightened scrutiny is warranted.

Multiple federal circuit courts have found cash bail systems unconstitutional as applied when defendants are detained solely due to inability to pay. ODonnell v. Harris County, 892 F.3d 147 (5th Cir. 2018) held that Harris County's misdemeanor bail system violated the 14th Amendment by detaining defendants solely because they were poor.

## The Legislative Path

### Pretrial Integrity and Safety Act

**Purpose:** Eliminate money bail as a condition of pretrial release in federal courts; create federal incentive funding for states that adopt risk-based pretrial systems.

**Key Provisions:**
- Eliminate cash bail for all federal defendants; replace with risk-based assessment using validated tools
- Establish federal Pretrial Services enhanced capacity to supervise defendants on release
- Create $500 million annual federal grant program for states that eliminate or substantially curtail cash bail
- Require states receiving grant funding to adopt validated, racially neutral risk assessment tools
- Mandate data collection and reporting on pretrial detention rates, racial disparities, and court appearance rates
- Prohibit commercial bail bondsmen from operating in federal courts

**Constitutional Authority:** Article I, Section 8 (spending power); 8th Amendment; 14th Amendment

**Day One Executive Actions:**
- Direct DOJ to issue guidance to federal prosecutors that pretrial detention should be sought only when the government can demonstrate specific danger or specific flight risk, not inability to pay
- Reinstate and strengthen federal Pretrial Services capacity
- Issue Executive Order directing federal agencies to study racial disparities in pretrial detention

**On the evidence:**

"New Jersey eliminated cash bail in 2017. Their jail population fell by 44 percent. Crime did not go up. People still showed up to court. Washington, D.C. has operated without cash bail for decades. It works. We have the evidence. The question is whether we have the political will."

## What Opponents Argue — and Why They Are Wrong

### The Opposition Case and the Evidence Against It

**Argument: "Eliminating bail will let dangerous people out."**

This conflates cash bail with detention. Under a reformed system, genuinely dangerous defendants can and should be held through preventive detention — a judicially ordered hold based on a finding that no conditions of release can ensure public safety. Preventive detention already exists under the Bail Reform Act of 1984 (18 U.S.C. § 3142(e)) and has survived constitutional challenge in United States v. Salerno, 481 U.S. 739 (1987). The difference is that preventive detention requires the government to prove dangerousness. Cash bail requires only that the defendant be poor.

**Argument: "High-risk defendants will fail to appear at trial."**

Court appearance rates in reformed jurisdictions are not lower than in cash bail jurisdictions. Washington, D.C. has maintained appearance rates above 88 percent for decades without cash bail. New Jersey's appearance rates remained stable after the 2017 reform. The threat of additional criminal charges for failure to appear — which exists regardless of how release is structured — is a powerful incentive. The evidence does not support the failure-to-appear prediction.

**Argument: "The bail industry provides a valuable service."**

The bail industry provides a financial intermediary function that extracts a non-refundable premium from defendants' families. It does not make communities safer. It does not improve court appearance rates over alternatives. It is a fee extraction mechanism that profits from pretrial detention. There is no comparative evidence that jurisdictions with commercial bail have better outcomes than those without it. Every other developed democracy operates without commercial bail bondsmen.

**Argument: "Risk assessment tools have their own racial biases."**

This is the most serious critique and deserves serious engagement. Some risk assessment algorithms have been found to produce racially disparate outputs — flagging Black defendants as higher risk at disproportionate rates. This critique does not vindicate cash bail, which produces racial disparities that are larger and more thoroughly documented. The answer to a flawed algorithmic tool is a better tool, implemented with transparency, human oversight, and regular auditing — not a return to a system that charges Black defendants 35 percent more to go home.

## The Federal Bail Reform Act: What Current Law Says and Why It Is Insufficient

### The Bail Reform Act of 1984

The federal pretrial detention framework is governed by the Bail Reform Act of 1984, 18 U.S.C. § 3142. The Act was passed in response to concerns about dangerous defendants being released pending trial and directs judges to consider four factors in setting release conditions:

1. The nature and circumstances of the offense charged
2. The weight of the evidence against the defendant
3. The history and characteristics of the defendant
4. The nature and seriousness of the danger to any person or the community posed by the defendant's release

The Act permits detention without bail for defendants who pose a danger to the community or a serious flight risk, subject to a detention hearing at which the government must make its case. This preventive detention provision was upheld in United States v. Salerno.

The problem is not the statutory framework for dangerous defendants. The problem is that in practice, cash bail amounts are set for the full range of defendants — including those charged with nonviolent offenses and assessed as low-risk — and the amounts are calibrated without regard to the defendant's ability to pay. A $500 bail for a low-income defendant charged with a misdemeanor is functionally a pretrial detention order. A $500,000 bail for a wealthy defendant charged with a serious felony may not detain them at all.

The Pretrial Integrity and Safety Act would replace monetary conditions of release with non-monetary conditions — supervision, check-ins, electronic monitoring — for defendants assessed as low to moderate risk, and would preserve preventive detention for genuinely dangerous defendants through the existing § 3142(e) mechanism.

## How This Reform Connects to the Broader Criminal Justice Agenda

Cash bail elimination does not exist in isolation. It is one component of a comprehensive pretrial justice reform that includes:

- **Discovery reform:** Defendants making pretrial decisions — including whether to plead guilty — should have access to the prosecution's evidence before entering a plea. Many states still permit guilty pleas before full discovery is disclosed.
- **Speedy trial enforcement:** The Speedy Trial Act (18 U.S.C. § 3161) sets timelines for federal prosecutions. In practice, continuances routinely extend pretrial periods. Strengthening speedy trial enforcement reduces the coercive pressure of pretrial detention.
- **Public defense funding:** The constitutional right to counsel (Gideon v. Wainwright, 372 U.S. 335 (1963)) is systematically underfunded. Public defenders in many jurisdictions carry caseloads of 300–500 clients simultaneously. A well-represented defendant is better positioned to contest pretrial detention and challenge the government's case.
- **Data collection and transparency:** The federal government does not comprehensively track pretrial detention by race, charge, jurisdiction, or bail amount. This data gap is not accidental. It makes systemic accountability harder. Federal grants under the proposed Pretrial Integrity and Safety Act should require standardized data reporting as a condition of funding.
