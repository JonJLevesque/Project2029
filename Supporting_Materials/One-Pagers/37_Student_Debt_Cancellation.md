# Reform #37: Student Debt Cancellation

## The Student Borrower Relief and Higher Education Equity Act

**The Problem:** Forty-four million Americans owe $1.7 trillion in federal student loan debt — a sum larger than the entire GDP of Australia. This crisis did not result from personal irresponsibility. It resulted from a 40-year policy of state disinvestment, stagnant wages, and deliberate substitution of debt for public higher education funding. The federal government issued the loans, services the loans, and holds the loans. It has the legal authority to cancel them. The Supreme Court's 2023 ruling in *Biden v. Nebraska* did not eliminate that authority. It narrowed one legal theory. The path forward is clear.

**The Solution:** Full cancellation for Pell Grant recipients. Broad income-based cancellation for all other federal borrowers. Targeted relief for defrauded students under Borrower Defense. Legislation to close the legal gaps *Biden v. Nebraska* exposed, ensuring future administrations cannot reverse relief already granted.

## How We Got Here

This crisis has a structural cause, not a moral one.

### State Disinvestment from Public Universities

From the 1960s through the 1980s, state governments funded public universities at levels that kept tuition genuinely affordable. A student attending a flagship state university in 1978 could work a minimum-wage summer job and cover tuition, fees, and books. That model collapsed:

- States have cut higher education funding by more than **50% per student** since the early 1990s, when adjusted for enrollment growth
- Public universities absorbed those cuts by shifting costs to students through tuition increases
- The result: tuition at public four-year universities increased from approximately $2,100 (inflation-adjusted 2023 dollars) in 1980 to **$11,260** by 2023 — a 437% real increase even after adjusting for inflation

### Federal Loan Policy Replaced Federal Grant Policy

- In 1980, Pell Grants covered approximately **80%** of the cost of attending a public four-year university
- By 2023, Pell Grants covered less than **30%** of public tuition costs
- Federal policy responded to the affordability crisis not by restoring grants, but by expanding loan availability — subsidizing student borrowing while leaving tuition growth unchecked
- The loan system created a feedback loop: easy credit enabled tuition increases; tuition increases forced more borrowing; more borrowing expanded the debt overhang

### Wage Stagnation

College graduates in 1980 entered a labor market where real wages grew predictably with productivity. Graduates in 2000 and beyond entered a market where:

- Real median wages for workers aged 25-34 grew less than 10% from 1980 to 2023
- The wage premium for a college degree has been partially offset by increased debt service costs
- A borrower with $40,000 in debt at 6% interest spending 10 years in standard repayment makes $4,300 in interest payments — money that leaves household consumption and economic circulation

## Who Holds the Debt

The student debt crisis is not evenly distributed. It is concentrated along racial, gender, and income lines — the predictable result of a system that charged the most to those with the fewest alternatives.

### Race and Student Debt

**Black borrowers are disproportionately burdened:**

- Black borrowers graduate with approximately **$25,000 more in debt** than white borrowers with equivalent degrees from equivalent institutions
- Four years after graduation, the typical Black borrower owes **more than they borrowed** — interest accumulation exceeds payments for the median Black graduate
- The racial wealth gap is directly amplified by student debt: white families are more likely to receive parental support that reduces borrowing; Black families carry less intergenerational wealth and thus less capacity to subsidize education costs
- Black students are more likely to attend institutions with lower completion rates (including for-profit colleges), resulting in debt without a degree — the worst possible outcome

### Gender and Student Debt

- Women hold approximately **two-thirds of all outstanding student loan debt** despite comprising roughly 56% of enrollment
- Women earn less on average after graduation, extending repayment timelines and increasing lifetime interest costs
- Women are more likely to work in public service fields (education, social work, healthcare) where income-based repayment and Public Service Loan Forgiveness are critical but have been inconsistently administered

### First-Generation and Low-Income Borrowers

- First-generation college students borrow at higher rates and attend less selective institutions that confer weaker labor market returns
- Borrowers who did not complete their degrees — approximately **30%** of those who borrow — carry debt without the credential, leaving them with maximum harm and minimum benefit

## The Biden Cancellation Attempts and *Biden v. Nebraska*

The Biden administration pursued three major cancellation pathways, each blocked by adverse legal or political action.

### The HEROES Act Approach ($10,000/$20,000 Relief)

In August 2022, the Biden administration announced cancellation of up to $10,000 in federal student loan debt for borrowers under $125,000 annual income, and up to $20,000 for Pell Grant recipients, using authority under the HEROES Act of 2003 (20 U.S.C. § 1098bb). The HEROES Act grants the Secretary of Education authority to "waive or modify" any student financial assistance provision during a national emergency.

**The Supreme Court struck this down in *Biden v. Nebraska*, 600 U.S. 477 (2023):**

- The Court held 6-3 that the HEROES Act did not grant authority to cancel debt at this scale
- Chief Justice Roberts applied the **major questions doctrine**: when an agency claims authority over a matter of vast economic and political significance, Congress must have spoken clearly
- The dissent (Kagan, Sotomayor, Jackson) argued the statutory text plainly authorized the action and the majority had invented a new veto over executive action

The *Nebraska* ruling did not hold that student debt cancellation is unconstitutional. It held that this particular statutory authority, as construed, did not reach this particular action at this scale. It left open other legal pathways.

### The SAVE Plan (Blocked 2024)

The Biden administration subsequently promulgated the SAVE (Saving on a Valuable Education) income-driven repayment plan under Higher Education Act authority, which would have provided near-cancellation relief to millions of borrowers with low balances. Courts blocked implementation pending litigation; the plan remained tied up in the circuit courts through the 2024 election.

### Borrower Defense and Closed School Discharge

The administration did successfully discharge approximately **$22 billion** in debt for defrauded borrowers (Borrower Defense to Repayment, 20 U.S.C. § 1087e(h)) and for students whose schools closed before they could complete their programs. This relief survived legal challenge and established precedent for targeted discharge authority.

## The Legal Authority That Remains

### Higher Education Act Section 432(a): Compromise and Settlement

The strongest surviving legal authority for broad cancellation is **20 U.S.C. § 1082(a)(6)**, which grants the Secretary of Education authority to:

> "...compromise, waive, or release any right, title, claim, lien, or demand, however acquired, including any equity or any right of redemption..."

This is the same authority used by the Department of Education to settle defaulted loan claims for decades. Legal scholars including Luke Herrine (University of Alabama School of Law) have argued this provision authorizes broad cancellation on equitable grounds, independent of the HEROES Act theory rejected in *Nebraska*.

The argument: the Secretary's settlement and compromise authority is a general administrative power granted by Congress for the management of the loan portfolio. It does not depend on emergency conditions. It does not require individualized findings. Applied to a class of borrowers — for example, all borrowers who attended schools with demonstrated patterns of deceptive marketing — it fits squarely within existing authority.

### Borrower Defense at Scale

Borrower Defense to Repayment (20 U.S.C. § 1087e(h)) authorizes discharge when borrowers were defrauded by their institutions. Evidence of systemic fraud, predatory marketing, and misrepresentation of job placement rates is documented across the for-profit college sector. Applied class-wide to all borrowers who attended schools that violated consumer protection laws, this authority reaches hundreds of billions in debt without requiring new legislation.

### The Path After *Nebraska*: Legislative Fix

*Biden v. Nebraska* identified the gap: Congress had not spoken clearly enough about cancellation at scale. The solution is to have Congress speak clearly. The **Student Debt Relief and Equity Act** (described in Legislative Requirements below) explicitly authorizes the Secretary of Education to cancel federal student loan debt in specified categories and amounts. It makes cancellation mandatory for defined groups, removing the discretionary framing that the major questions doctrine targeted.

## What Cancellation Means

### The Proposed Relief Structure

| Borrower Category | Relief |
|-------------------|--------|
| Pell Grant recipients (all years) | **Full cancellation** of federal student loan debt |
| All other federal borrowers, income under $75,000 | **$50,000 cancellation** |
| All other federal borrowers, income $75,000-$125,000 | **$25,000 cancellation** |
| Borrowers defrauded by institutions (Borrower Defense) | **Full cancellation** (existing authority, expedited) |
| Borrowers at closed schools | **Full cancellation** (existing authority, automatic) |
| Borrowers in income-driven repayment 20+ years | **Full cancellation** (existing statutory requirement, enforced) |

### What Cancellation Does Not Include

- Private student loans (not held by the federal government; require separate legislation or servicer negotiation)
- Parent PLUS loans are included for borrowers meeting income thresholds
- Graduate PLUS loans for incomes over $125,000 are excluded from broad relief, though existing IDR forgiveness timelines apply

## The Economic Case

Debt cancellation is not a giveaway. It is a release of a contractual claim the federal government holds against its own citizens — a claim whose existence actively suppresses economic activity.

### The Economic Multiplier

- Federal Reserve Bank of New York research estimates that student debt cancellation would increase GDP by **$86 billion to $108 billion per year** for the decade following cancellation
- The mechanism is direct: freed debt service payments flow immediately into consumer spending, savings, and investment
- Borrowers in early repayment years spend the highest share of freed cash flow — the multiplier is concentrated among the population most likely to spend additional income

### Housing Formation

- Borrowers with high debt-to-income ratios are unable to qualify for mortgages at the same rate as debt-free peers
- Studies find that each $1,000 in student debt reduces homeownership probability by approximately 1-2 percentage points
- Mass cancellation would accelerate household formation and housing purchases among a cohort currently locked out of the housing market

### Entrepreneurship

- Individuals with high student debt are less likely to start businesses — the risk tolerance required for entrepreneurship is suppressed by mandatory debt service
- Small business formation rates among recent graduates have declined sharply since debt levels surged in the 2000s and 2010s

### Closing the Racial Wealth Gap

- Cancellation disproportionately benefits Black borrowers, who carry the highest debt loads relative to post-graduation income
- Targeted full cancellation for Pell recipients is specifically race-conscious in effect: Pell recipients are overwhelmingly low-income and disproportionately students of color

## The Cost in Context

**Estimated total cost of the proposed relief:** Approximately $800 billion to $1.1 trillion, depending on uptake and income verification, distributed over 10 years as existing loans are retired.

This number is large. The following numbers are larger:

| Comparison | Cost |
|------------|------|
| 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) — 10-year cost | **$1.9 trillion** (Congressional Budget Office) |
| Annual U.S. defense budget (FY2024) | **$886 billion** |
| TCJA corporate rate cut alone — 10-year cost | **$800 billion** |
| Mortgage interest deduction (annual, overwhelmingly benefits wealthy homeowners) | **$30 billion** |
| Capital gains exclusion on home sales (annual) | **$40 billion** |
| Total cost of cancellation vs. TCJA | **Less than the tax cut that primarily benefited the wealthy** |

The federal government chose to spend $1.9 trillion on tax cuts concentrated among corporations and high-income households in 2017 without a single hearing on the distributional consequences. The argument that student debt cancellation is fiscally irresponsible, from the same political tradition that passed the TCJA without offsets, is not a serious fiscal argument. It is a political one.

### The GI Bill Comparison

The Servicemen's Readjustment Act of 1944 — the GI Bill — provided free college education to approximately **8 million veterans** at federal expense. It required no repayment. It imposed no means test beyond veteran status. It is universally credited with building the American middle class of the 1950s and 1960s.

The argument that debt cancellation is unfair "forgiveness" for people who "chose to borrow" would, if applied consistently, condemn the GI Bill as an unjust gift to veterans who "chose to serve." No serious person makes that argument. The GI Bill worked because it removed the debt barrier to education entirely. That is precisely what broad cancellation does.

## Responding to Objections

**"They chose to borrow — they should repay what they borrowed."**

The choice to borrow was not made in a vacuum. Students were told, accurately, that a college degree was the gateway to middle-class employment. They were not told that states would continue cutting university funding, that tuition would continue rising, that wages for college graduates would stagnate, or that the labor market they were preparing to enter would be dramatically different from the one the loan counselor described. The system changed the terms after the borrowing decision. That is not a free choice with clear consequences — it is a bait-and-switch at scale.

**"This is unfair to people who already paid off their loans."**

Past repayment was compelled under a system that had no better alternative. The argument that past harm should prevent future relief is an argument for perpetuating injustice to maintain consistency. We do not decline to treat a disease because past patients suffered without treatment. The borrowers who repaid their loans were not harmed by cancellation. They were harmed by the same system that created the debt in the first place.

**"This is regressive — it benefits college graduates who earn more."**

The income-targeted structure of this proposal addresses this directly: the largest benefits go to Pell recipients (by definition low-income at time of enrollment) and to borrowers under $75,000 in current income. The progressivity concern applies to untargeted universal cancellation; it does not apply to the income-stratified proposal here.

**"Cancellation will just inflate tuition further."**

Cancellation addresses the stock of existing debt. Tuition inflation is a flow problem. The correct policy response to tuition inflation is the free public college framework (Reform #27) paired with gainful employment enforcement and state funding conditions. Cancellation and tuition reform are not substitutes — they are complements that address different aspects of the same problem.

## Legislative Requirements

### The Student Debt Relief and Equity Act

**Purpose:** Explicitly authorize broad student loan cancellation, close the major questions doctrine gap identified in *Biden v. Nebraska*, and establish permanent income-driven repayment reforms.

**Key Provisions:**

- **Mandatory cancellation** for Pell Grant recipients of all outstanding federal student loan balances — no application required; automatic based on federal records
- **Statutory authorization** for the Secretary of Education to cancel up to $50,000 in federal student loan debt for borrowers with income below $75,000 and up to $25,000 for borrowers with income between $75,000 and $125,000
- **Explicit congressional finding** that student loan cancellation constitutes a permissible exercise of federal spending authority and is not subject to major questions doctrine challenge (direct legislative response to *Nebraska*)
- **Permanent elimination of interest capitalization** on all federal student loans
- **IBR codification**: 10% of discretionary income, 20-year forgiveness, no tax on forgiven amounts
- **Automatic PSLF enrollment** for qualifying public service workers through IRS data matching

**Constitutional Authority:** Article I, Section 8 Spending Clause; 20 U.S.C. § 1070 et seq. (Higher Education Act); direct statutory authorization superseding prior regulatory interpretations

### The Public Higher Education Restoration Act

**Purpose:** Restore federal-state partnership in higher education funding, conditioning federal higher education aid on state maintenance of per-student funding at 1990 inflation-adjusted levels.

**Key Provisions:**

- States that reduce per-student higher education funding below the 1990 benchmark lose eligibility for federal higher education formula grants
- Tuition increases above CPI at public institutions trigger automatic Pell Grant increases to maintain purchasing power
- New federal matching grants for states that increase per-student funding — 2:1 federal match for increases above 5% per year

## Success Metrics

Within four years of full implementation:

- At least **35 million borrowers** have received complete or partial cancellation
- Pell Grant recipients carry **zero federal student loan debt** at graduation within the new free public college framework
- Homeownership rate among borrowers aged 25-35 increases by at least **5 percentage points** relative to 2024 baseline
- Racial wealth gap narrows — Black-white median net worth gap decreases by at least **8%** attributable to debt cancellation
- Default rate on remaining student loans falls below **3%** under reformed IDR system

**For constituents carrying debt:**
> "You were told that borrowing for college was an investment in your future. What wasn't disclosed is that states were simultaneously cutting university funding, tuition was being allowed to rise without limit, and the loan servicers processing your payments had financial incentives to maximize what you owed. This was a system designed to extract, not educate. Cancellation is not charity. It is restitution."

**For the fiscal responsibility objection:**
> "The 2017 tax cuts cost $1.9 trillion and went overwhelmingly to corporations and wealthy shareholders. Nobody asked those shareholders to pay it back. Student debt cancellation costs less and goes to working people. The selective concern about fiscal responsibility is a tell — it reveals who this system was built to protect."

**For the constitutional objection:**
> "*Biden v. Nebraska* said the old legal theory wasn't enough. It didn't say cancellation was unconstitutional. Congress can authorize this explicitly. That is what this legislation does. The Supreme Court told us to use the right door. This is the right door."

> "Black borrowers graduate with $25,000 more in debt than their white peers with the same degree. Four years after graduation, the typical Black borrower owes more than they borrowed. Student debt is a mechanism of wealth extraction that operates specifically against Black families who were already excluded from the wealth-building opportunities of the 20th century. Cancellation is not race-neutral. Neither was the policy that created the crisis."

## Key Legislation

**Student Debt Relief and Equity Act** — proposed
- **Purpose:** Statutory authorization for broad federal student loan cancellation; mandatory relief for Pell recipients; income-based relief for other borrowers; permanent IDR reform
- **Constitutional Authority:** Art. I, Sec. 8 Spending Clause; 20 U.S.C. § 1087e; explicit override of major questions doctrine gap in *Biden v. Nebraska*, 600 U.S. 477 (2023)

- **Purpose:** Restore state per-student funding to 1990 benchmark; condition federal grants on tuition restraint; establish federal-state matching program for reinvestment
- **Constitutional Authority:** Art. I, Sec. 8 Spending Clause; *South Dakota v. Dole*, 483 U.S. 203 (1987) (conditional spending upheld)
