## The Argument in One Sentence

The United States has sentenced innocent people to death, executed some of them, cannot un-execute a single one, and has produced no credible evidence that doing so made anyone safer — and on those facts alone, it must end.

---

## The Wrongful Conviction Crisis

### 190+ People Sentenced to Death for Crimes They Did Not Commit

Since 1973, more than 190 people have been exonerated from death row in the United States — people who were convicted, sentenced to die, and subsequently proven innocent. This is not a theoretical concern. It is a documented record of the state preparing to kill people it had convicted in error.

| Death Row Exoneration Data | Figure | Source |
|---------------------------|--------|--------|
| Total death row exonerations since 1973 | 190+ | Death Penalty Information Center, 2024 |
| Average time served on death row before exoneration | 11.4 years | DPIC, 2024 |
| States with the most exonerations | Illinois (21), Florida (30+), Texas (12+) | DPIC, 2024 |
| Exonerations based on DNA evidence | Approximately 30% | Innocence Project |
| Ratio of exonerations to executions (1973–present) | Approximately 1 exoneration for every 8 executions | DPIC, 2024 |

The exoneration ratio is the most damning data point. For every eight people executed in the modern era, one person on death row was later proven innocent. These are not reversible errors. Execution is final. There is no appellate process that can restore the life of someone the state has killed in error.

The National Academy of Sciences estimated in a 2014 study that approximately 4.1 percent of defendants sentenced to death in the United States are factually innocent. Applied to the roughly 2,400 people currently on death row, that estimate implies approximately 98 innocent people are currently awaiting execution.

## Racial Disparity: The Arbitrary Geography of Death

### Who Gets the Death Penalty Is Determined by Race and Zip Code, Not by Crime

The application of the death penalty in the United States is neither consistent nor race-neutral. Decades of empirical research document that race — particularly the race of the victim — is a significant predictor of who receives a death sentence, independent of the severity of the crime.

| Racial Disparity Metric | Finding | Source |
|------------------------|---------|--------|
| Black defendants sentenced to death vs. white defendants for similar crimes | 4x more likely | ACLU, Death Penalty Information Center |
| Cases involving a Black defendant and white victim vs. Black defendant and Black victim | Significantly more likely to result in death sentence | Gross & Mauro, 1989; updated studies confirm |
| Share of executions where victim was white (vs. share of murder victims who are white) | Approximately 80% of executions; victims are roughly 50% white | DPIC, 2024 |
| Geographic concentration of executions | 2% of U.S. counties account for majority of death sentences | DPIC, 2024 |

The geographic concentration of death sentences is a constitutional problem that the Supreme Court has never adequately resolved. Two defendants can commit identical crimes — one in Harris County, Texas; one in Montgomery County, Pennsylvania — and receive radically different sentences based entirely on the charging decisions of local prosecutors, the composition of local juries, and the political culture of local jurisdictions. The difference is not justice. It is geography.

McCleskey v. Kemp, 481 U.S. 279 (1987) presented the Supreme Court with the most rigorous statistical study ever conducted on racial disparities in capital sentencing — the Baldus Study, which demonstrated that defendants in Georgia were 4.3 times more likely to receive a death sentence if the victim was white. The Court acknowledged the validity of the study but refused to apply it to individual cases, holding that a defendant must prove intentional discrimination in his specific case. That standard effectively immunizes systemic racial bias from constitutional challenge.

## The Cost Argument: Death Costs More Than Life

### Capital Punishment Is the Most Expensive Form of Punishment the State Inflicts

The argument that the death penalty saves money compared to lifetime incarceration is demonstrably false. Death penalty cases cost more — substantially more — at every stage of the process: investigation, prosecution, trial, appeals, and incarceration itself.

| Cost Comparison | Death Penalty Cases | Life Imprisonment Cases |
|----------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Pre-trial and trial costs | $1M–$3M additional | Baseline |
| State-funded defense (capital-qualified attorneys required) | Mandatory; significantly higher cost | Standard public defense |
| Automatic appeals process | Mandatory multi-year appellate process | Standard appeals only |
| Specialized death row housing | Solitary confinement; higher security costs | General population |
| Total additional cost per case vs. life imprisonment | $1M+ additional | Baseline |

Studies in multiple states confirm this cost differential:

- **California:** A 2011 study found California's death penalty system costs $184 million more per year than a system imposing life without parole. The state had 725 people on death row.
- **Kansas:** A 2014 study found death penalty cases cost 70 percent more than comparable non-capital cases.
- **Indiana:** A 2010 study found death penalty trials cost approximately 38 percent more than life imprisonment trials.
- **Federal government:** The Federal Death Penalty Resource Counsel documented that federal capital cases cost on average $620,932 in defense costs alone, compared to $49,029 for non-capital federal murder defense.

The financial argument for the death penalty does not survive scrutiny. The state spends more money to kill people than to imprison them for life.

## Deterrence: The Evidence Does Not Exist

### No Credible Research Shows the Death Penalty Reduces Murder Rates

The principal utilitarian justification for the death penalty is deterrence: the theory that potential murderers are deterred by the prospect of execution. Fifty years of social science research have failed to produce credible evidence that this is true.

| Deterrence Evidence | Finding |
|--------------------|---------|
| States with death penalty vs. states without (murder rates) | States without the death penalty consistently have lower murder rates than states with it |
| Murder rates in states following abolition | No increase observed in any state that abolished the death penalty |
| Comparative international data | Nations that abolished the death penalty did not experience increases in murder rates |
| National Research Council (2012) | "Fundamentally flawed" prior studies claiming deterrent effect; concluded existing studies "should not be used to inform judgments about the effect of the death penalty on homicide rates" |

The 2012 National Research Council report, commissioned by the National Academy of Sciences, is the most authoritative review of deterrence research ever conducted. Its conclusion was categorical: the existing body of research on deterrence and the death penalty is methodologically inadequate to support any conclusion, and the studies claiming deterrent effect should not be used in policy deliberations.

The murder rate in the United States is substantially higher than in comparable nations that have abolished the death penalty. The presence of capital punishment has not made the United States safer. The absence of capital punishment has not made Canada, Germany, France, or the United Kingdom more dangerous.

## International Isolation

### The Company the United States Keeps

The United States is the only member of the G7 that executes its citizens. In 2023, the countries that carried out the most executions were:

1. China (thousands, estimated; exact figures not disclosed)
2. Iran (approximately 853)
3. Saudi Arabia (approximately 172)
4. Somalia (approximately 38)
5. United States (approximately 24)

The United States executed more people in 2023 than Egypt, Iraq, or Yemen. Every member of the European Union has abolished the death penalty. Every member of the Council of Europe has abolished it as a condition of membership. Canada abolished it in 1976. Australia abolished it in 1984. The United Kingdom abolished it in 1969.

The United States, in company with China, Iran, and Saudi Arabia, is one of the world's leading executioners. This is not a distinction consistent with the claim to be a global leader in human rights and the rule of law.

## The Trajectory of Abolition

### The Death Penalty Is Already Dying — We Must End It

The use of the death penalty in the United States has declined dramatically over the past twenty-five years. The trajectory is unmistakable.

| Year | Executions Carried Out |
|------|----------------------|
| 1999 | 98 (modern era peak) |
| 2005 | 60 |
| 2010 | 46 |
| 2015 | 28 |
| 2020 | 17 |
| 2023 | 24 |

| Abolition Status | Count |
|-----------------|-------|
| States that have abolished the death penalty | 23 |
| States with official moratoriums on executions | 4 |
| States that have not executed anyone in 10+ years | 10+ |
| States actively using the death penalty | Approximately 13 |

The political map of capital punishment in the United States shows a practice that is geographically concentrated, declining in use, and increasingly inconsistent with national and international norms. The question is not whether the death penalty will end. It is whether it will end before more innocent people are killed.

## Constitutional Framework: The Evolving Standards of Decency

### The Supreme Court Has Already Charted the Path to Abolition

The 8th Amendment prohibits "cruel and unusual punishments." The Supreme Court has interpreted this clause using the "evolving standards of decency" doctrine — the principle that constitutional meaning must be assessed against contemporary norms, not frozen in 18th-century practice. This doctrine has produced a series of decisions narrowing the application of the death penalty:

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| Atkins v. Virginia | 2002 | Execution of intellectually disabled defendants violates the 8th Amendment |
| Roper v. Simmons | 2005 | Execution of defendants who committed crimes as juveniles violates the 8th Amendment |
| Kennedy v. Louisiana | 2008 | Death penalty for non-homicide crimes (including child rape) violates the 8th Amendment |
| Hall v. Florida | 2014 | States cannot use rigid IQ cutoffs to determine intellectual disability in capital cases |
| Glossip v. Gross | 2015 | Challenged lethal injection protocols; Court upheld 5-4; four justices expressed doubts about the penalty itself |

Justice Brennan's dissent in Gregg v. Georgia (1976) argued that the death penalty is per se cruel and unusual. Justices Brennan and Marshall maintained that position throughout their tenures. Justices Stevens, Blackmun, and Ginsburg each expressed, at various points in their careers, the view that capital punishment cannot be administered in a constitutional manner. The trajectory of the Court's jurisprudence — successive categorical exclusions based on evolving standards — points toward the conclusion that the death penalty itself, as a category, fails the constitutional test.

## Federal Death Penalty: The Administrative Path

### What a New Administration Can Do Without Waiting for Congress

The federal government has 40 people currently on federal death row. President Biden imposed a moratorium on federal executions in January 2021, halting the Trump administration's execution spree (which carried out 13 federal executions in its final six months — the most federal executions in any comparable period since the Civil War era). The Trump administration resumed federal executions immediately upon taking office in 2025.

A new Democratic administration can take the following actions on Day One:

**Executive actions available immediately:**
- Reimpose moratorium on all federal executions
- Direct DOJ to cease seeking the death penalty in new federal cases
- Initiate clemency review for all 40 federal death row inmates
- Order comprehensive review of all prior federal capital convictions for evidence of racial disparity, ineffective counsel, or prosecutorial misconduct

These are executive actions that require no congressional authorization. They can be implemented on Inauguration Day.

## The Legislative Path

### Federal Death Penalty Abolition Act

**Purpose:** Abolish the federal death penalty; create federal incentive structure for state abolition; establish clemency review process for all federal death row inmates.

**Key Provisions:**
- Repeal 18 U.S.C. § 3591–3599 (Federal Death Penalty Act of 1994)
- Replace death as a sentencing option with life imprisonment without possibility of parole for all currently death-eligible federal offenses
- Establish independent Federal Capital Case Review Commission to review all 40 current federal death row cases for wrongful conviction, racial disparity, and constitutional violations
- Create $200 million federal grant fund for states that abolish the death penalty
- Require DOJ to publish annual report on racial disparities in state capital sentencing
- Fund innocence review programs in states that retain the death penalty pending abolition

**Constitutional Authority:** Article I, Section 8; Article II, Section 2 (clemency); 8th Amendment; 14th Amendment

**Legislative pathway:** Reconciliation is not available for criminal law reform; this requires 60 Senate votes or filibuster reform. A moratorium can and should be imposed by executive action immediately.

## Talking Points

**The irreversibility argument:**

"We have executed innocent people. We know it with certainty. We cannot un-execute them. There is no remedy, no compensation, no appeal that restores a life the state has taken in error. That fact alone — that we have killed innocent people and will kill more if we continue — is sufficient reason to stop."

**On race:**

"Black defendants are four times more likely to be sentenced to death than white defendants for the same crime. The death penalty is not applied to the worst crimes. It is applied to the wrong people. It is applied to poor people, Black people, and people without good lawyers. That is not justice. That is discrimination with a needle."

**On deterrence:**

"The states without the death penalty have lower murder rates than the states with it. The countries without the death penalty have lower murder rates than the United States. If the death penalty deterred murder, you would see it in the data. You do not, because it does not."

**On cost:**

"It costs more than a million dollars more per case to execute someone than to imprison them for life. We are paying a premium — with taxpayer money — for the privilege of killing people. And we are getting it wrong one out of every eight times."

**On international standing:**

"The United States executes more people than any other developed democracy on Earth. In 2023, we were fifth globally, behind China, Iran, Saudi Arabia, and Somalia. If we claim to lead the world on human rights, we cannot be in that company."

**On the trajectory:**

"Twenty-three states have already abolished the death penalty. Executions have fallen from 98 a year to fewer than 25. The country is already moving away from this. We are not asking for something radical. We are asking the federal government to get to the same place the country is already heading — and to get there before we kill anyone else we shouldn't have."

## What Opponents Argue — and Why They Are Wrong

### The Opposition Case and the Evidence Against It

**Argument: "The death penalty is reserved for the worst of the worst."**

The evidence contradicts this. Geography determines death sentences more reliably than the severity of the crime. Two counties — Los Angeles County, California and Harris County, Texas — produced more death sentences in recent decades than most states combined. Whether a defendant receives a death sentence depends critically on the jurisdiction, the prosecutor, and the race of the victim — not exclusively on the nature of the crime. If the penalty were truly reserved for categorical worst-case scenarios, application would be consistent across jurisdictions. It is not.

**Argument: "We have safeguards. Innocent people don't get executed."**

This is false. The safeguards have failed. The National Academy of Sciences estimates that approximately 4.1 percent of death row inmates are factually innocent. Cameron Todd Willingham was executed by Texas in 2004 for the arson murder of his three daughters. Subsequent fire science investigation found that the forensic evidence used to convict him was based on debunked theories. Texas conducted an investigation that was shut down by the governor before its conclusion. We almost certainly executed an innocent man. "Almost certainly" is not a confidence level compatible with the irrevocable act of execution.

**Argument: "Victims' families want justice, and the death penalty provides closure."**

This argument deserves respect and honest engagement. Many victims' families do advocate for the death penalty. Many others — including prominent survivors and family members organized through groups such as Murder Victims' Families for Reconciliation — actively oppose it. The empirical evidence on whether the death penalty provides closure is mixed; the decades-long appellate process associated with capital cases often prolongs rather than resolves family grief. The justice system is not obligated to impose a punishment because some victims' families request it. Life without the possibility of parole is a severe, permanent punishment that delivers certainty — the convicted person will not harm anyone else — without requiring the state to risk executing the innocent or maintaining a racially arbitrary system.

**Argument: "Abolition is legislating from the bench."**

Statutory abolition is the opposite of judicial legislation — it is the legislature acting within its constitutional authority to define criminal penalties. Moratorium by executive action is the exercise of prosecutorial discretion and clemency authority explicitly granted to the executive by Article II of the Constitution. Neither is illegitimate. Both are squarely within the constitutional authority of the branches exercising them.

## The Connection to Broader Criminal Justice Reform

Death penalty abolition does not exist in isolation. It is the logical terminus of a criminal justice reform agenda that includes:

- **Wrongful conviction review:** Every state should have a conviction integrity unit with independent authority to investigate claims of innocence, including for defendants who are no longer appealing and for those who accepted plea deals.
- **Prosecutorial accountability:** Prosecutors who suppress exculpatory evidence (Brady violations), present false testimony, or engage in racially discriminatory charging face virtually no professional consequences under current law. This must change.
- **Public defense funding:** The Supreme Court in Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668 (1984) set an absurdly low standard for ineffective assistance of counsel. Defense attorneys who slept through capital trials have been found not to have violated their clients' constitutional rights. Adequate funding for capital-qualified defense counsel is a precondition for any claim that the system has meaningful safeguards.
- **Forensic science reform:** Multiple death row exonerations have followed the discrediting of forensic disciplines — bite mark evidence, hair comparison, arson science — that were presented to juries as settled science. A federal investment in forensic science validation is overdue.

Abolishing the death penalty is both a standalone reform and a signal about what kind of criminal justice system we intend to build: one that values accuracy over finality, equality over vengeance, and human dignity over the satisfaction of state power.
