# REFORM #17: DECENTRALIZE FEDERAL POWER
## Direct Federal-City Funding. Regional Autonomy. End State Strangleholds.

## üö® THE PROBLEM

**Federal system gives too much power to states‚Äîcities and people suffer:**

**Current system problems:**

1. **State Stranglehold on Cities:**
   - Cities generate 80%+ of GDP but state legislatures control them
   - Rural-dominated state legislatures block city policies
   - Preemption laws: States override city decisions
   - Cities can't raise revenue, set minimum wage, regulate housing without state permission

2. **Federal Funding Filtered Through States:**
   - Federal money ‚Üí states ‚Üí cities (states take cut, delay, attach strings)
   - States can block federal programs in cities
   - Cities can't access federal funds directly (must beg states)
   - Political retaliation: Red states punish blue cities, blue states punish red areas

3. **Unresponsive Government:**
   - State capitals far from major cities (literally and politically)
   - State legislators don't represent cities (gerrymandering + rural bias)
   - One-size-fits-all state laws don't fit urban or rural needs
   - Federal government even more distant

**By the numbers:**
- 80% of Americans live in urban/suburban areas
- Cities generate 85% of US GDP
- But: State legislatures skew rural (Senate even worse)
- 50+ major cities have policies overridden by states (minimum wage, paid sick leave, plastic bag bans, etc.)
- Federal funds delayed 6-18 months by state bureaucracy

**The result:**
- Cities can't solve local problems (homelessness, transit, housing)
- Rural areas feel ignored by cities
- State governments satisfy no one
- Democracy unresponsive and frustrating

## ‚úÖ THE SOLUTION

### Federal-Local Partnership Act + Regional Autonomy Framework

---

## **PART I: DIRECT FEDERAL-CITY FUNDING**

**Cities Can Access Federal Funds Without State Intermediary:**

**Eligible Cities:**
- Population 100,000+ (250 cities qualify)
- Can apply directly to federal agencies
- No state veto or interference

**Eligible Programs:**
- Infrastructure (transit, roads, water, broadband)
- Housing (affordable housing, homelessness)
- Education (school funding, early childhood)
- Healthcare (community health centers, mental health)
- Climate (resilience, adaptation, mitigation)
- Economic development (small business, workforce training)

**How It Works:**
- Cities submit applications to federal agencies (DOT, HUD, EPA, etc.)
- Federal agencies review and approve (based on merit)
- Funds go DIRECTLY to cities (bypass state entirely)
- State can still receive funds separately (not either/or)
- Transparency and accountability (federal oversight)

**Benefits:**
- Faster (no state bureaucracy delay)
- More efficient (no state middleman taking cut)
- Better targeting (cities know local needs)
- No political retaliation (red state can't punish blue city)

**Safeguards:**
- Federal standards apply (civil rights, labor, environmental)
- Audits and oversight (prevent corruption)
- Performance metrics (must show results)
- Can be revoked if misused

---

## **PART II: CITY CHARTER RIGHTS (Constitutional Framework)**

**Federal Recognition of City Autonomy:**

**Cities Have Constitutional Right To:**
- Set local minimum wage (above federal/state minimum)
- Regulate local housing (zoning, rent stabilization, inclusionary zoning)
- Establish local environmental standards (above federal/state minimums)
- Create local worker protections (paid sick leave, scheduling, etc.)
- Regulate local businesses (licensing, health, safety)
- Establish local taxes and fees (within federal limits)

**State Preemption Limits:**
- States CANNOT override city decisions on local matters
- "Local matters" = issues primarily affecting city residents
- Federal law establishes baseline (cities can exceed, states can't prevent)
- State laws apply only if statewide concern (not local)

**Constitutional Amendment (29th Amendment Proposed):**
```
Section 1: Cities with population exceeding 100,000 shall have right
to local self-governance on matters primarily affecting their residents.

Section 2: States shall not preempt city ordinances unless demonstrating
compelling statewide interest.

Section 3: Congress may regulate to protect city autonomy and prevent
state overreach.
```

**Until Amendment:**
- Federal legislation limits state preemption
- Conditions federal funding on respecting city autonomy
- DOJ can sue states that violate city rights

---

## **PART III: REGIONAL GOVERNANCE STRUCTURES**

**Multi-City Regional Authorities:**

**Cities Can Form Regional Compacts For:**
- Transit (metropolitan transit authorities)
- Housing (regional affordable housing)
- Environmental protection (air quality, watersheds)
- Economic development (regional strategies)

**Federal Recognition:**
- Regional authorities can access federal funds directly
- Can exercise powers across city/county boundaries
- States cannot veto (if cities agree)

**Examples:**
- Bay Area Rapid Transit (BART) - multi-city transit
- Port Authority of NY/NJ - regional infrastructure
- Metropolitan Planning Organizations - transportation
- Scale these models nationally

**Benefits:**
- Regional problems (transit, housing, environment) need regional solutions
- Coordination without state interference
- Economies of scale
- Better outcomes for residents

---

## **PART IV: RURAL COMMUNITY EMPOWERMENT**

**Small Town Direct Access:**

**Communities Under 100,000 Can:**
- Form regional coalitions (pool resources)
- Access federal rural development funds directly
- Coordinate on shared challenges (broadband, healthcare, economic development)
- Not forced into one-size-fits-all state programs

**Rural Regional Authorities:**
- Multi-county coalitions
- Direct federal funding for rural priorities
- Local control of development strategies
- Not beholden to urban-dominated state policies

**Benefits:**
- Rural areas get responsive government (not ignored by state capitals)
- Can address rural-specific needs (broadband, healthcare access, economic diversification)
- End urban-rural divide (both get autonomy)

## üí¨ TALKING POINTS

**"Doesn't federalism require state control?"**
‚Üí Federalism is BALANCE between levels of government. Current system gives too much power to states, too little to cities (where people actually live). Cities are creatures of state law, yes‚Äîbut that doesn't mean states should micromanage. This restores balance: federal, state, AND city governments all have appropriate roles.

**"Won't this undermine states?"**
‚Üí No. States keep most powers. This redirects SOME federal funds to cities directly and protects cities from state overreach. States can still regulate statewide issues. But cities should control local issues. State governments will adapt‚Äîfocus on statewide coordination, not micromanaging cities.

**"What about rural areas‚Äîwon't cities dominate even more?"**
‚Üí OPPOSITE. Rural areas can form regional coalitions and access federal funds directly too. Current system has state legislatures (urban or rural dominated, depending on state) controlling everyone. This gives BOTH urban AND rural communities direct federal access. Empowers both, dominates neither.

**"Is this constitutional?"**
‚Üí Yes. Congress has broad spending power‚Äîcan attach conditions to federal funds. Can require states to respect city autonomy. Constitutional amendment would strengthen this, but not required for direct funding. Plus, Tenth Amendment reserves powers to states "or to the people"‚Äîpeople live in cities, so cities should have powers too.

**"Won't this create chaos with 250+ cities dealing with federal government?"**
‚Üí Federal agencies already deal with thousands of entities (states, counties, schools, nonprofits). 250 major cities is manageable. Plus, regional authorities can coordinate. This is MORE efficient than funneling everything through 50 state bureaucracies. Federal agencies will adapt.
