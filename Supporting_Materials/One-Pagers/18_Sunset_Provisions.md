## **PART I: 20-YEAR SUNSET PROVISION**

**All Federal Laws (New and Existing) Expire After 20 Years:**

**How It Works:**
- Every law has explicit expiration date (20 years from passage)
- To continue, Congress must affirmatively renew
- Renewal requires new vote (simple majority, like original passage)
- Can renew as-is, amend, or let expire
- If expires: Law removed from US Code (no longer in effect)

**Timeline:**
- **New laws (after 2026):** 20-year expiration automatic
- **Existing laws:** Phased review over 20 years (1/20th per year)

**Exceptions (Permanent Laws):**
- Constitutional provisions (can't sunset the Constitution)
- Core government functions (Congress, courts, basic agencies)
- Civil rights protections (voting rights, equal protection)
- Treaties and international obligations
- Social Security, Medicare (can be reformed, but not sunsetted without replacement)

**Why 20 Years?**
- Long enough for laws to be implemented and show effects
- Short enough to prevent permanent accumulation
- Generational: Each generation reviews previous generation's laws
- Balanced: Stability + adaptability

---

## **PART II: MANDATORY REVIEW PROCESS**

**Congressional Review Cycle:**

**Years 1-19: Ongoing Evaluation**
- GAO conducts impact studies of expiring laws
- OMB assesses costs and benefits
- Public comment periods (open hearings)
- Stakeholder input (affected parties, experts)
- Congressional committees hold hearings

**Year 20: Renewal Decision**
- Committee votes on renewal (with or without amendments)
- Floor vote in both chambers (majority required)
- President signs (or veto â†’ override or let expire)

**Review Criteria:**
1. **Effectiveness:** Is law achieving stated goals?
2. **Necessity:** Is law still needed? (Has problem changed or been solved?)
3. **Unintended Consequences:** Is law causing harms not anticipated?
4. **Cost-Benefit:** Do benefits outweigh costs?
5. **Alternatives:** Are there better approaches now?
6. **Constitutionality:** Does law still pass constitutional review?

**Outcomes:**
- **Renew as-is:** Law continues unchanged
- **Renew with amendments:** Law updated for modern context
- **Replace:** New law addressing same issue differently
- **Let expire:** Problem solved, law no longer needed, or law harmful

## **PART III: PHASED ROLLOUT (EXISTING LAWS)**

**Cannot Review All Existing Laws at Once:**

**20-Year Phase-In:**
- Divide existing US Code into 20 tranches (alphabetically or by date of original passage)
- Year 1: Review 1/20th of existing laws
- Year 2: Review next 1/20th
- ...
- Year 20: Review final 1/20th

**By 2046:**
- All existing federal laws reviewed once
- Outdated laws removed
- Important laws renewed
- Harmful laws reformed or eliminated
- Legal code dramatically simplified

**Prioritization:**
- Oldest laws reviewed first (most likely outdated)
- High-impact laws reviewed early (major economic or social effect)
- Controversial laws reviewed carefully (thorough debate)

## **PART IV: SIMPLIFICATION & CODIFICATION**

**Continuous Legal Code Simplification:**

**As Laws Reviewed:**
- Redundant laws consolidated
- Contradictory laws reconciled

**Goal:**
- US Code reduced 50% in size over 20 years

- Published by GAO and OMB
