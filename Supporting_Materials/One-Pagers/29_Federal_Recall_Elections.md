# Reform #29: Federal Recall Elections

## Proposed 37th Constitutional Amendment

**The Problem:** Between elections, there is no legal mechanism by which American voters can remove a sitting president, senator, or representative — for any reason, no matter how severe. An official can commit documented misconduct, abandon the positions they ran on, refuse to represent their constituents, or simply stop functioning — and voters have no recourse until the next scheduled election, which may be years away. The only removal tools available are impeachment and expulsion, both of which require the very institution housing the offending official to police itself. That system has failed every major test.

**The Solution:** The 37th Amendment establishes the first federal recall mechanism in American history. A petition signed by 15% of registered voters in the relevant constituency — certified by the Federal Accountability Office — triggers a recall election within 90 days. This reform does not circumvent democracy. It expands it.

---

## The Accountability Gap

Federal officeholders operate under a system of deferred accountability that has no parallel at the state level or in most peer democracies. The mechanisms that are supposed to fill the gap have failed systemically.

| Mechanism | Why It Fails |
|-----------|-------------|
| Impeachment (President) | Requires House majority + Senate 2/3 supermajority. Has removed exactly **0** presidents in 235 years. |
| Impeachment (executive officials) | Same thresholds. Rarely initiated. Almost never completed. |
| Congressional expulsion | Requires 2/3 vote of the chamber — members protecting members. Historically rare. |
| Congressional elections | House: every 2 years. Senate: every 6 years. President: every 4 years. Years of unaccountable conduct before any electoral check. |
| State recall of federal officials | Constitutionally impossible. States have no authority over federal office. |
| Federal recall election | **Does not exist.** No constitutional mechanism. |

The result: a federal official who wins election can behave in ways their constituents find disqualifying — abandoning campaign positions, engaging in documented corruption, refusing to perform the basic functions of the office — and voters have no power to act until the next scheduled election. The 37th Amendment changes that.

---

## What 19 States Already Know

The federal government is the only level of American democracy where recall is unavailable. **19 states** permit recall elections for state officials. It is not a radical concept. It is a proven democratic tool.

| State | Recall Provisions | Notable Use |
|-------|------------------|------------|
| California | All state officials; 12% petition threshold | Gov. Gray Davis recalled October 2003; Arnold Schwarzenegger elected successor |
| Wisconsin | All state officials | Gov. Scott Walker survived recall in 2012 — but the election happened; voters had the choice |
| Colorado | All state officials | Two state senators recalled in 2013 over gun legislation |
| Michigan | All state officials | Multiple local and state officials recalled over decades |
| Oregon | All state officials | Rarely used but available |
| Alaska | All state officials | Available but seldom triggered |
| Minnesota | State officials after 6 months in office | Used at local level |

**The pattern:** States with robust recall provisions do not experience constant political chaos. Recall petitions are difficult to qualify, elections are expensive to run, and most efforts fail — exactly as designed. What recall provides is not routine removal but a structural backstop: the knowledge that sufficiently egregious conduct will trigger accountability, and the knowledge that voters retain ultimate power.

California's 2003 recall of Governor Gray Davis — triggered by a budget crisis and a 55% disapproval rating — required approximately **9.1% of voters** to sign a petition (the threshold was 12% of the votes cast in the previous governor's race). The election produced a result. Voters decided. That is democratic accountability in practice.

## The Proposed 37th Amendment

### Full Text

**Section 1.** Any person holding an elected federal office — including the offices of President, Vice President, Senator, and Representative — may be subject to a recall election upon petition of the voters of the relevant constituency.

**Section 2.** A recall election shall be triggered when a petition signed by **15% of registered voters** in the relevant constituency is certified by the Federal Accountability Office as valid within 180 days of initial filing. For the President and Vice President, the relevant constituency is the national electorate.

**Section 3.** Upon certification of a valid recall petition, a recall election shall be held within **90 days.** If a majority of voters cast ballots in favor of recall, the official is removed from office upon certification of the results. A successor shall be chosen pursuant to the procedures established by Congress for filling that vacancy.

**Section 4.** No recall election shall be held within the first year of an official's term or within 12 months of a scheduled general election for that office. No official may be subject to more than one recall election per term.

**Section 5.** Congress shall have power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation establishing petition verification procedures, election administration, and vacancy succession rules.

---

### How the 15% Threshold Works

The 15% threshold is calibrated to be high enough to prevent frivolous or purely partisan recall attempts while low enough to be achievable by a genuinely mobilized electorate.

| Office | Relevant Constituency | 15% Threshold (approx.) |
|--------|----------------------|------------------------|
| President / Vice President | National registered voters (~160M) | ~24 million signatures |
| U.S. Senator (large state) | California registered voters (~22M) | ~3.3 million signatures |
| U.S. Senator (small state) | Wyoming registered voters (~290K) | ~43,500 signatures |
| U.S. Representative | Average district (~500K registered) | ~75,000 signatures |

For comparison: The California Davis recall required approximately 900,000 valid signatures from a state of roughly 15 million registered voters at the time. A 15% federal threshold is deliberately higher than most state thresholds — reflecting the gravity of removing a federal official and the need to ensure genuine broad-based constituency demand rather than organized-money petition drives.

## The Certification Role of the Federal Accountability Office

The 37th Amendment specifies that petitions must be **certified by the Federal Accountability Office (FAO)** — the independent oversight institution proposed in Reform #34.

FAO certification performs several critical functions:

**Signature verification:** Every signature is verified against official registered voter rolls. Signatures are matched by name, address, and voter registration status. Duplicate signatures are flagged and removed.

**Residency confirmation:** For congressional recall petitions, signatures must come from registered voters in the specific district or state of the official being recalled. Out-of-district signatures are automatically disqualified.

**Fraud detection:** The FAO maintains forensic capacity to identify coordinated fraud — bulk signature collection using ineligible signatories, forged signatures, paid signature mills violating disclosure requirements.

**Timeline enforcement:** The 180-day petition window begins from the date of first public filing. The FAO publicly tracks petition progress in real time. Upon certification, the FAO formally notifies the relevant election authority, triggering the 90-day election clock.

**Independence from political pressure:** Because the FAO is structurally independent of all three branches (see Reform #34), it cannot be pressured by the White House, congressional leadership, or party apparatus to delay, accelerate, or invalidate a certification for political reasons.

## Constitutional Authority and Architecture

**Article V — The Amendment Process:** The 37th Amendment, like all constitutional amendments, requires a two-thirds vote of both chambers of Congress and ratification by three-quarters of states (38 of 50). This is the same process used for every amendment since the Bill of Rights.

**Precedent within the Constitution:** The Constitution already contains mechanisms for mid-term removal of federal officials — it is not a principle-free document on this question. Article I, Section 5 permits each chamber to expel a member with a two-thirds vote. Article II provides impeachment. Section 3 of the 14th Amendment disqualified former Confederate officeholders from federal service. The principle that federal officeholders may be removed between elections is embedded in constitutional text. The 37th Amendment adds a democratic mechanism to accompany the existing legislative ones.

**State recall precedent as democratic legitimacy:** The fact that 19 states have operated recall mechanisms for decades, without constitutional catastrophe, provides the most powerful practical argument: it works. The fears raised against it — frivolous use, partisan weaponization, instability — have not materialized at scale in the states that have it.

**First Amendment considerations:** The petition process is a protected form of political speech and association under the First Amendment. Congress has broad authority to regulate the manner of elections under Article I, Section 4 and Article II, Section 1. The combination provides robust constitutional grounding for enabling legislation.

## Structural Safeguards Against Abuse

The 37th Amendment's built-in protections address the legitimate concerns raised by critics:

| Safeguard | How It Works |
|-----------|-------------|
| **15% threshold** | High enough that purely partisan organizing cannot easily qualify a petition without genuine broad-based public grievance |
| **No recall in first year of term** | Prevents immediate post-election harassment campaigns by the losing party |
| **No recall within 12 months of next scheduled election** | Prevents a recall from being used to extend or manipulate the election calendar |
| **One recall per term** | A failed recall attempt cannot be immediately refiled; the official has a full remaining term to govern without repeated petition campaigns |
| **FAO certification** | Independent verification prevents organized fraud from manufacturing qualifying petitions |
| **Majority required for removal** | The recall election itself requires a majority — a plurality of an unusual turnout cannot remove an official against a majority's will |

## Comparison: Federal vs. State Recall Procedures

| Feature | California (State) | 37th Amendment (Federal) |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------------|
| Threshold | 12% of votes in previous governor's race | 15% of registered voters |
| Petition window | 160 days | 180 days |
| Election timeline after qualification | 60-80 days | 90 days |
| Cooldown after taking office | None in current law | 1 year |
| Maximum per term | No explicit limit | 1 per term |
| Certification authority | Secretary of State | Federal Accountability Office |
| Who is subject | State officials | President, VP, all senators and representatives |
| Successor selection | Special rules (varies) | Congress establishes by legislation |

The federal framework is deliberately more protective than most state frameworks — reflecting the difference in scale, the national consequences of removing a federal official, and the need to prevent well-funded minority factions from gaming the process.

## Arguments Against — and the Answers

### "We already have elections as the check on elected officials."

This argument proves too much. Elections exist at every level of government — yet 19 states concluded that elections alone were insufficient and created recall mechanisms. Elections are scheduled in advance, fixed in timing, and in competitive races can turn on issues entirely unrelated to the conduct that prompted accountability concerns. A senator who commits gross misconduct in year two of a six-year term has, under the current system, four more years of unaccountable power regardless of what they do. That is not a functioning accountability system — it is a structural protection for misconduct.

### "This would destabilize government with constant recall attempts."

State experience refutes this. California has had one successful gubernatorial recall in its history. Wisconsin's Walker recall, the most high-profile in recent history, proceeded orderly, was decided by voters, and Walker remained in office. The 15% threshold plus the FAO certification process plus the one-per-term limit create an extremely high bar. Most recall attempts at the state level fail to qualify — the petition process itself filters out non-serious efforts.

### "The 37th Amendment requires a constitutional amendment — too hard."

Correct. It does. Constitutional amendments are difficult — they require two-thirds congressional support and ratification by 38 states. That difficulty is a feature: it ensures that only reforms with broad, durable democratic support become part of the constitutional order. The case for this amendment must be made to the public and to state legislatures. The record of state recall mechanisms provides the empirical case. The failures of impeachment and expulsion to provide accountability provide the urgency.

### "Rich interests will fund recall campaigns against officials who cross them."

This concern is real and serious. The enabling legislation authorized by Section 5 of the amendment must impose robust campaign finance requirements on recall elections — including strict limits on outside spending, mandatory donor disclosure, and prohibition on foreign funding. The same concerns apply to regular elections and are addressed through campaign finance law. They do not argue against recall; they argue for well-designed enabling legislation.

### "It's just partisan — the minority party will always try to recall the other side."

A 15% petition threshold in a national context means approximately 24 million signatures for a presidential recall. No purely partisan minority can manufacture that. For House districts, 75,000 verified signatures from a specific geographic constituency is a genuine organizing challenge requiring real community mobilization. The threshold is calibrated to require a genuine cross-partisan or super-majority constituency grievance — not just an organized opposition.

## Implementation: The Enabling Legislation

Section 5 of the 37th Amendment grants Congress authority to establish the procedural framework. The **Federal Recall Election Act** — companion legislation to be enacted upon ratification — should include:

**Petition procedures:**
- Standard petition form with required fields (name, address, voter registration number, signature)
- Digital petition option with identity verification (matching to voter registration databases)
- Prohibition on paying per-signature; all petition circulators must be volunteers or on flat salary
- Real-time public tracker maintained by the FAO showing petition progress

**Election administration:**
- Recall election administered by state election authorities using federal funding
- Same voting access standards as general elections (no special restrictions)
- Ballots must include both the recall question and a replacement candidate selection
- If recall succeeds, the replacement candidate with the most votes takes office

**Vacancy succession:**
- For President: VP becomes President; VP vacancy filled per 25th Amendment
- For VP: 25th Amendment vacancy procedures apply
- For senators: Governor appoints replacement pending special election within 90 days
- For representatives: Special election within 90 days; no appointment

**Campaign finance:**
- Recall elections subject to all Federal Election Campaign Act provisions
- Independent expenditure limits for recall campaigns (to be established by statute)
- Mandatory disclosure of all contributions over $200

## Talking Points

**For constituents:**
> "Nineteen states have recall elections. Your state representative can be recalled. Your mayor can be recalled. Your governor can be recalled. But the president and your senators and your member of Congress? Untouchable until the next scheduled election — no matter what they do. The 37th Amendment fixes that."

**On why the threshold is set at 15%:**
> "Fifteen percent of registered voters is not a small number. For a presidential recall, that is approximately 24 million verified signatures. That is a genuine national movement — not a partisan stunt. The threshold is designed to ensure that only sustained, broad-based public outrage triggers a recall. Most recall petition drives fail to qualify. That is the point."

**For skeptics who say 'elections are the check':**
> "Tell that to constituents of a senator in year two of a six-year term who has committed documented misconduct. Their next chance to hold that senator accountable is four years away. That is not accountability — that is a waiting room. The recall mechanism adds a democratic option that voters in 19 states already have. The federal government should not be the only level of democracy where voters are powerless between elections."

**For constitutional scholars:**
> "The Constitution already provides for mid-term removal through impeachment and expulsion. Both of those mechanisms require the institution housing the offending official to police itself — and both have proven structurally inadequate. The 37th Amendment adds a third mechanism that routes accountability through the voters rather than through Congress. That is more democratic, not less."

**On stability concerns:**
> "California has had one successful gubernatorial recall in its history. Wisconsin's Walker recall happened, was orderly, and voters chose to keep him. The fears about instability have not materialized in states with recall. What has materialized is the check — the knowledge that sufficiently egregious conduct will trigger accountability. That knowledge alone changes how officials govern."

## International Comparisons: Recall in Established Democracies

Federal recall is not unprecedented in the democratic world. Multiple established democracies have mechanisms for mid-term removal of elected officials through popular vote.

| Country | Mechanism | Threshold | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| Switzerland | Cantonal recall provisions | Varies by canton | Long-established tradition of direct democracy at sub-federal level |
| Venezuela | Constitutional recall (Article 72) | 20% of registered voters | Applied to all elected officials including president; Chavez survived 2004 recall with 59% |
| Bolivia | Presidential and gubernatorial recall | Referendum by legislative request | Used in 2008 to confirm Evo Morales with 67% support |
| Ecuador | Presidential recall | 15% of registered voters | Constitutional provision established in 2008 |
| British Columbia (Canada) | MLA recall | 40% of registered voters; 60-day window | Very high threshold; attempted but no successful recalls |

**The lesson from comparative practice:** Countries and jurisdictions with recall mechanisms have not experienced the destabilizing effects that critics predict. In most cases, recall attempts fail — they serve as a democratic pressure valve that reinforces rather than undermines government legitimacy. When recalls succeed, they reflect genuine majority discontent. Both outcomes are consistent with democratic governance.

The United States, at the federal level, is an outlier. The argument that recall is incompatible with American constitutional governance is contradicted by 19 states that operate recall systems and by multiple established democracies that have operated them for decades.

## Why This Must Accompany the Federal Accountability Office

The 37th Amendment and Reform #34 (the Federal Accountability Office) are designed as a paired system. They address different dimensions of the same accountability gap.

The FAO has robust removal power over **appointed officials** — cabinet secretaries, agency heads, senior executive branch employees. When the FAO finds probable cause of serious misconduct, it can place appointed officials on administrative suspension pending investigation. That power is effective precisely because it does not depend on the political will of Congress or the President.

For **elected officials**, the FAO has investigative and referral authority — it can investigate, document, and formally refer findings to Congress. But it cannot directly remove an elected official. The FAO's mandatory referral provisions require Congress to hold a public vote within 30 days of a formal FAO referral. That is a significant improvement over the current system of buried committee reports. But it still depends on Congress acting against one of its own members — or the House initiating impeachment against a president.

The 37th Amendment provides what the FAO alone cannot: a democratic mechanism that routes accountability through voters rather than through Congress. If Congress refuses to expel a member despite an FAO referral and documented misconduct, voters in that member's constituency retain the power to act. The recall election does not depend on congressional will. It depends on constituent will.

Together, the FAO and the 37th Amendment create a complete accountability architecture:
- Appointed officials: FAO suspension + FAO prosecution + existing removal procedures
- Elected officials: FAO investigation + mandatory congressional referral + 37th Amendment recall + existing impeachment/expulsion

Neither mechanism makes the other unnecessary. Both are required for the system to function without structural gaps.

## Public Support and the Political Path

Public support for recall mechanisms, when polled as a general proposition, is consistently strong. The principle — that voters should have a mechanism to remove officials who egregiously fail them between elections — is intuitive and widely shared across partisan lines.

**Key data points:**
- A 2023 YouGov/Economist poll found **61% of Americans** support the ability to recall elected federal officials
- Support is majority among Democrats (67%), independents (63%), and Republicans (54%) — demonstrating this is not a partisan issue at the public level
- Support is highest among voters who rate congressional approval most negatively — which currently means support is very high

**The path to ratification:** Constitutional amendments require two-thirds of both chambers and ratification by 38 states. The political difficulty is real. But public support above 60% with cross-partisan backing provides the foundation for a sustained ratification campaign. A Democratic administration committed to this reform would use the full weight of the presidency — speaking tours, grassroots organizing, presidential endorsement — to build state-by-state ratification coalitions.

**The state experience accelerates the argument:** As more egregious federal misconduct occurs without consequence — as officials document their own corruption and remain in office through election cycles — the public case for recall strengthens. The reform is most persuasive precisely when the existing system is most visibly failing.

**The filibuster question:** The 37th Amendment requires a two-thirds congressional vote — which means it bypasses the Senate filibuster's 60-vote threshold entirely. Joint resolutions proposing constitutional amendments are not subject to filibuster. If two-thirds of both chambers can be assembled, the amendment proceeds to state ratification without obstruction by Senate minority. This is one of the few structural reforms that does not require filibuster reform to initiate.

- **Key Provisions:** Petition standards; digital verification; election administration; campaign finance limits for recall campaigns; vacancy succession procedures
