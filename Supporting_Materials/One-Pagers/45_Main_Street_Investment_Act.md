## The Argument in One Sentence

The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act delivered approximately $150 billion per year to the Fortune 500 and the wealthiest Americans; this proposal delivers $100 billion per year to the 33 million small businesses that employ 61 million workers and generate 44 percent of U.S. economic output.

---

## The Problem: A Tilted Playing Field, Documented

### What the Federal Government Has Done for Large Corporations — and Not Done for Small Business

The United States government does not maintain a neutral posture toward business. It actively chooses who benefits from its tax code, its lending authority, its regulatory structure, and its procurement dollars. The choices made over the past three decades have systematically favored large corporations, monopoly platforms, and billionaire-controlled enterprises over the small businesses that make up the foundational layer of the American economy.

The evidence is direct and statutory.

**The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA), Pub. L. 115-97,** reduced the corporate income tax rate from 35 percent to 21 percent, a 14-point cut that saves Fortune 500 companies an estimated $150 billion per year. The Joint Committee on Taxation scored the corporate provisions of the TCJA at approximately $1 trillion in revenue loss over ten years. The benefit flowed overwhelmingly to publicly traded corporations and their shareholders — not to the 33 million small businesses organized as sole proprietorships, S corporations, LLCs, and partnerships that pay taxes through the individual income tax system.

**The carried interest loophole** (26 U.S.C. § 1222, as applied to fund manager compensation) allows private equity fund managers to characterize their management compensation as long-term capital gains taxed at 20 percent, rather than ordinary income taxed at 37 percent. The Congressional Budget Office estimates this treatment costs the Treasury approximately $14 billion per year. The employees of the businesses those private equity firms buy and restructure pay 37 percent on their wages. The managers who collect the gains pay 20 percent.

**Amazon paid $0 in federal income taxes** in 2017 and 2018, reporting $5.6 billion in U.S. profits across those two years and receiving net tax rebates of approximately $137 million from the federal government. This was not tax fraud. It was the result of TCJA bonus depreciation provisions, tax credits, and stock-based compensation deductions that are specifically designed for, and primarily available to, large corporations with sophisticated tax departments. The local retail businesses Amazon's logistics network displaces pay their full tax bill.

The result of these choices, compounded over three decades:

| Group | Annual Federal Investment or Tax Benefit |
|-------|------------------------------------------|
| Fortune 500 (TCJA corporate rate cut) | ~$150 billion/year |
| Top 0.1% (estate tax exemption increases, TCJA) | ~$20 billion/year |
| Private equity (carried interest loophole) | ~$14 billion/year |
| Amazon alone (2017-2018 average net tax position) | ~$2 billion+/year |
| All 33 million small businesses (entire SBA budget, FY2024) | ~$34 billion/year |
| **Proposed Main Street Investment Act** | **$100 billion/year** |

The question is not whether the federal government picks winners and losers. It already does. The question is which winners it picks.

## The Scale of the Damage: Small Business Under Consolidation Pressure

### What the Data Shows

The 33 million small businesses operating in the United States are not a sentimental category. They represent 44 percent of U.S. GDP and employ approximately 61 million workers — 47 percent of the private sector workforce. These are not lifestyle businesses. They are the primary employment mechanism for the majority of the American working class.

They are failing at a documented and accelerating rate, and undercapitalization is the primary cause.

| Small Business Survival Data | Figures |
|-----------------------------|---------|
| New business applications filed (2024) | 5.5 million |
| Businesses that fail in Year 1 | approximately 20% |
| Businesses that fail by Year 5 | approximately 45% |
| Businesses that fail by Year 10 | approximately 65% |
| Primary cited reason for failure | Undercapitalization (SBA Office of Advocacy, consistent across surveys) |
| Community bank branches closed since 2010 | 10,000+ |
| Increase in large business share of private employment (2000-2022) | 45% to 58% — directly displacing small business employment |

The capital access problem is not uniformly distributed. It is concentrated among businesses that conventional lending institutions have historically underserved:

**Women-owned businesses:** 13 million firms. Receive approximately 2 percent of conventional bank lending (National Women's Business Council, 2023).

**Black-owned businesses:** 3.1 million firms. Receive 20 cents in bank lending for every dollar received by comparable white-owned businesses (Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta, *Small Business Credit Survey*, 2022). The median revenue of Black-owned businesses is approximately 1 percent of the median revenue of white-owned businesses — a gap that is a direct and documented consequence of differential capital access, not of differential effort or business quality.

**Latino-owned businesses:** 4.6 million firms. The fastest-growing segment of small business formation. The least served by conventional credit institutions relative to their growth rate.

**Rural businesses:** 8.5 million firms. The highest closure rates nationally. The least access to Small Business Development Center counseling, which is concentrated in metropolitan areas where university affiliations exist.

These are not new findings. They appear, with consistent methodology and consistent conclusions, in Federal Reserve survey data, SBA Office of Advocacy reports, and National Community Reinvestment Coalition analyses spanning two decades. The failure to address them with commensurately scaled intervention is a policy choice.

## The International Comparison: What Works Elsewhere

### The Mittelstand Standard

The United States is not experimenting with an untested approach when it proposes scaled federal investment in small and mid-sized businesses. Germany, Japan, and Canada have implemented comparable frameworks for decades — with documented outcomes.

**Germany's Mittelstand and KfW Bank:** Germany's small and medium-sized enterprise sector employs approximately 60 percent of the German private workforce and accounts for 35 percent of German exports. German SMEs are globally competitive in manufacturing, precision engineering, and specialty products despite Germany's high labor costs and strong environmental regulations. The mechanism: Germany's KfW Kreditanstalt fur Wiederaufbau — a state development bank capitalized at over 500 billion euros — provides credit guarantees, subsidized loans, and equity participation instruments at an annual scale exceeding $100 billion U.S. equivalent. This is not charity. Germany treats SME capital access as industrial policy. The outcome: Germany exports more per capita than the United States despite being a fraction of its size.

**Japan's SME Agency:** 99.7 percent of all Japanese companies are small and medium-sized enterprises. Japan's Agency for Small and Medium Enterprises operates a comprehensive support structure including subsidized credit, technical assistance, and export promotion at a scale proportional to the domestic economy. Japanese SMEs serve as Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers to major manufacturers, maintaining the industrial base in a way that U.S. offshoring policy has systematically eroded.

**Canada's Business Development Bank (BDC):** The BDC provides approximately $3.5 billion Canadian annually in lending specifically to businesses that commercial banks decline to finance — with a mandate to accept risk that private lenders will not accept. The BDC reported that 80 percent of its clients had been previously declined by conventional lenders. The mission is explicit: serve the market failure, not compete with the market.

**The U.S. Comparison:** The SBA's 7(a) loan guarantee program authorized approximately $35 billion in lending in FY2024. The SBA's direct appropriation — funding for the agency itself, for SBDC counseling, for SCORE mentorship — was approximately $1 billion. For a $28 trillion economy in which small businesses represent 44 percent of GDP, this is structurally inadequate by any international standard.

## The Main Street Investment Act: Five Funding Streams

### $100 Billion Per Year, Five Targeted Mechanisms

The Main Street Investment Act directs $100 billion per year through five distinct funding streams, each designed to address a specific, documented market failure in small business capital access, employment support, tax treatment, and infrastructure.

---

### Stream 1: Small Business Opportunity Grants ($25 Billion Per Year)

Direct grants — not loans, not tax credits, not deferred obligations — targeting the specific failure point that SBA data and Federal Reserve survey data consistently identify: the absence of equity-equivalent capital at the founding and early-growth stage for businesses outside the venture capital ecosystem.

Venture capital provides this capital to technology founders, primarily in coastal metropolitan areas, without repayment requirements. The Main Street Investment Act provides it to everyone else.

**New Business Formation Grants ($5 billion/year):** Grants up to $50,000 per new business in its first two years of operation, with priority weighting for businesses in Opportunity Zones (26 U.S.C. § 1400Z-1), rural areas, and businesses majority-owned by women, Black, Latino, or Native American entrepreneurs. Administered through the expanded SBA grant infrastructure, modeled on the existing Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) program (15 U.S.C. § 638) but available across all industry sectors.

**Main Street Innovation Grants ($10 billion/year):** Competitive grants of $25,000 to $250,000 for established small businesses with annual revenue under $5 million, to fund capital investment in technology, equipment, product development, or workforce training. No repayment. Income reportable to IRS under 26 U.S.C. § 61 but excluded from gross income by statute, analogous to Pell Grant exclusion treatment.

**Community Anchor Business Grants ($10 billion/year):** Targeted at businesses that serve essential community functions — independent pharmacies, locally owned hardware stores, community restaurants, independent bookstores, community banks — that are being closed by corporate chain consolidation and e-commerce displacement. Grants of $10,000 to $100,000, with priority to businesses in ZIP codes that have lost 20 percent or more of their local business establishments in the past decade, based on Census Bureau Business Patterns data.

---

### Stream 2: Low-Cost Small Business Investment Loans ($30 Billion Per Year in New Lending Authority)

**Main Street Opportunity Loans:** 2 percent fixed interest rate — compared to the current SBA 7(a) average effective rate of prime plus 2.25 percent, which at current prime exceeds 10 percent — with 20-year terms and no collateral required for loans below $150,000. This provision directly addresses the documented collateral barrier that Federal Reserve survey data identifies as the second most common reason for small business loan denial after creditworthiness. The $30 billion in annual SBA lending authority is additive to the existing 7(a) program, effectively doubling total small business lending capacity.

**Microenterprise Launch Loans:** $5,000 to $50,000 per borrower, 1 percent interest, 10-year terms, no minimum credit score. Modeled on the existing SBA Microloan Program (15 U.S.C. § 636(m)) but operating at ten times the current scale. The existing Microloan Program is capped at $50 million per year in appropriations; this provision expands available capital to $5 billion. Administered through designated nonprofit intermediaries with demonstrated lending records in underserved communities.

**CDFI Partnership Expansion ($5 billion/year in federal matching capital):** Community Development Financial Institutions — certified under 12 U.S.C. § 4702 — are the institutions that actually lend to small businesses in economically distressed communities that commercial banks systematically decline. CDFIs currently operate under chronic capital constraints; the CDFI Fund's annual appropriation in FY2024 was approximately $300 million. The Main Street Act provides $5 billion in federal matching capital on a 1:1 basis, expanding the CDFI sector's lending capacity by approximately ten times. CDFI certification requirements under 12 C.F.R. Part 1805 ensure accountability for mission compliance.

---

### Stream 3: Payroll Tax Credits for Small Business Employment ($20 Billion Per Year)

Three credits under 26 U.S.C. (Internal Revenue Code), each structured as credits against employer payroll taxes, claimable on IRS Form 941 (Employer's Quarterly Federal Tax Return) and refundable:

**New Hire Credit ($10 billion/year estimated):** $5,000 per net new full-time employee added in Year 1 — up from $3,000 under the existing Work Opportunity Tax Credit (26 U.S.C. § 51) — with $3,000 per year for Years 2 and 3. Available only to businesses with fewer than 100 employees, ensuring the benefit flows to small businesses and not to large corporations using incremental hiring. The existing WOTC targets specific disadvantaged worker categories; this credit applies to all net new hires.

**Health Insurance Provision Credit ($5 billion/year estimated):** 50 percent tax credit on employer-paid health insurance premiums for businesses with fewer than 50 employees. The existing Small Business Health Care Tax Credit (26 U.S.C. § 45R) provides a 50 percent credit but phases out between 10 and 25 full-time equivalent employees and requires participation in the SHOP Exchange — restrictions that have limited its uptake to a fraction of eligible small businesses. This provision eliminates the phase-out and the SHOP requirement, making the credit available to all qualifying small businesses regardless of marketplace participation.

**Apprenticeship and Training Credit ($5 billion/year estimated):** $10,000 per registered apprentice over two years for qualifying apprenticeship programs registered under the National Apprenticeship Act of 1937 (29 U.S.C. § 50 et seq.), or qualifying employer-designed training programs meeting Department of Labor standards. Directly addresses the skilled trade shortage that consistently ranks among the top operational constraints cited by small manufacturers, contractors, and service businesses in SBA survey data.

---

### Stream 4: Small Business Tax Reform ($15 Billion Per Year in Tax Relief)

**Permanent 100% Equipment Expensing for Small Business ($8 billion/year):** The TCJA's 100 percent bonus depreciation provision (26 U.S.C. § 168(k)) allowed businesses to immediately deduct the full cost of qualifying equipment, machinery, and technology investments. The TCJA phased this provision down beginning in 2023 — to 80 percent, then 60 percent, continuing to phase out. This provision makes 100 percent immediate expensing permanent for businesses with annual gross receipts below $10 million (26 U.S.C. § 448(c) threshold), ensuring that small businesses retain the full deduction as large corporations lobby for its general restoration.

**Restored R&D Expensing for Small Business ($3 billion/year):** The TCJA amended 26 U.S.C. § 174 to require research and experimental expenditures to be amortized over five years (domestic) or fifteen years (foreign) rather than immediately deducted. This change eliminated a decades-old deduction that functioned as the primary federal support for small business innovation. The Main Street Act restores immediate deductibility of R&D costs for businesses with annual gross receipts below $50 million, exempting the small businesses that benefit most from immediate deduction while the large-corporation R&D amortization debate proceeds separately.

**Pass-Through Equity for Working Small Businesses ($4 billion/year):** Section 199A of the Internal Revenue Code (26 U.S.C. § 199A), created by the TCJA, allows qualifying pass-through business owners a 20 percent deduction on qualified business income. As enacted, it disproportionately benefits high-income real estate limited partnerships, hedge fund structures, and large professional firms — not the small businesses it was rhetorically designed to serve. This provision makes the Section 199A deduction permanent for pass-through businesses with annual qualified business income below $1 million, while eliminating it for large real estate LLPs and investment fund structures above the $1 million threshold. Net fiscal effect: approximately revenue-neutral through the elimination of the higher-income version, while locking in the benefit for genuinely small businesses.

**Anti-Monopoly Procurement Rebate:** A 10 percent federal excise tax on fees paid by small businesses to dominant platform intermediaries — those holding more than 30 percent market share in a category as determined by the Federal Trade Commission under 15 U.S.C. § 45 — is credited back to the small business at tax filing as a refundable credit. The mechanism: Amazon, Google, and similar platform monopolies charge small businesses selling fees, advertising fees, and fulfillment fees that function as a private tax on access to their customer base. This provision makes that implicit tax explicit and rebates it.

---

### Stream 5: Entrepreneurship Infrastructure ($10 Billion Per Year)

**Community Business Incubator Fund ($4 billion/year):** Competitive grants to local governments, tribal governments, and nonprofit organizations to establish or expand co-working spaces, business incubators, and small business accelerators in communities that lack them. Grant criteria weight rural areas, tribal lands, and communities with concentrated poverty (as designated under the Community Reinvestment Act, 12 U.S.C. § 2901 et seq.). Administered through the SBA with a streamlined application process modeled on the Economic Development Administration's existing grant infrastructure (42 U.S.C. § 3121 et seq.).

**Full SBDC and SCORE Funding ($1 billion/year):** The Small Business Development Center network (15 U.S.C. § 648) operates approximately 1,000 centers nationwide, providing counseling to small businesses through partnerships with universities and state governments. SCORE — the volunteer mentor network — operates through the same SBA authority. Combined FY2024 appropriations: approximately $172 million. The Main Street Act funds both programs at $1 billion per year — approximately six times current funding — with the explicit target of establishing SBDC counseling capacity in every county in the United States, including the rural and tribal counties that currently have no nearby counseling access.

**Main Street Broadband ($2 billion/year):** Dedicated broadband connectivity assistance for small businesses in rural and underserved areas, administered in coordination with the Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment (BEAD) program established by the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021, Pub. L. 117-58. Fiber installation subsidies and satellite service subsidies specifically allocated to businesses, separate from residential broadband programs. High-speed internet connectivity is not a luxury for modern small businesses — it is a prerequisite for point-of-sale systems, inventory management, payroll processing, and customer acquisition. Rural businesses that cannot access it operate at a structural disadvantage to their urban competitors.

**Small Business Export Assistance ($2 billion/year):** Dedicated SBA export assistance funding, administered through SBA's Office of International Trade (15 U.S.C. § 649), to help small manufacturers, agricultural producers, and specialty goods businesses access international markets. International markets currently account for approximately 95 percent of the world's purchasing power but remain effectively inaccessible to small businesses without export infrastructure. Large corporations with international arms capture these markets by default. This provision funds the legal, logistics, translation, and market entry support that small businesses cannot individually afford.

**Legal Services Access Fund ($1 billion/year):** Free legal services for small businesses covering contract review, intellectual property protection, employment law compliance, and regulatory navigation — consistently cited by small business owners as the barrier to business formation and operation second only to capital access. Administered through a network of qualifying law schools, legal aid organizations, and bar association pro bono programs, with SBA certification and quality standards. Small businesses cannot afford general counsel. The consequence is that they sign unfavorable contracts, lose intellectual property, fail to comply with employment law, and do not pursue viable legal claims against larger counterparties who have counsel. This provision levels that access gap.

## Equity Architecture: Who the Money Reaches

### Targeting Design and Accountability Mechanisms

The Main Street Investment Act is not a neutral investment program. It is a structured intervention in a documented pattern of unequal capital access. The targeting is specific and enforceable.

**Fifty percent of all grant and loan funds** under Streams 1 and 2 must be directed, by program design and annual certification, to businesses owned by women, Black Americans, Latino Americans, Native Americans, or businesses located in rural counties (defined as non-metropolitan statistical areas under U.S. Census Bureau classifications) or federally designated Opportunity Zones.

**Demographic reporting is mandatory.** Every SBA program under this Act requires applicants to provide voluntary demographic data; program administrators are required to report annually on the racial, gender, rural/urban, and income distribution of all awards. The SBA Inspector General conducts annual audits of targeting compliance. Results are published in the Federal Register and on the SBA website.

**CDFI partnerships are prioritized.** CDFIs certified under 12 C.F.R. Part 1805 receive first-right access to the CDFI Partnership Expansion capital under Stream 2, with priority to CDFIs with documented lending records in Persistent Poverty Counties as defined by the USDA Economic Research Service.

This is not a quota system. It is a design specification — the same kind of targeting design that governs the existing SBA 8(a) Business Development Program (15 U.S.C. § 637(a)), the HUBZone program (15 U.S.C. § 657a), and the Women-Owned Small Business Federal Contract Program (15 U.S.C. § 637(m)). Those programs exist because Congress determined that market outcomes in federal contracting systematically underserved these communities. The Main Street Investment Act applies the same determination to capital access.

## Constitutional and Legislative Authority

**Constitutional basis:** Congress's authority to establish this program rests on multiple independent constitutional grants:

- **Article I, Section 8, Clause 1 (Spending Clause):** Congress has plenary authority to spend for the general welfare. Small business investment is among the most direct applications of general welfare spending — it is the textbook constitutional case for federal economic intervention. *South Dakota v. Dole*, 483 U.S. 203 (1987), and *Steward Machine Co. v. Davis*, 301 U.S. 548 (1937), establish the breadth of this authority.
- **Article I, Section 8, Clause 3 (Commerce Clause):** Federal regulation of interstate commerce encompasses the credit markets, tax code, and lending institutions that govern small business capital access. *Wickard v. Filburn*, 317 U.S. 111 (1942), establishes that federal authority reaches all economic activity with substantial interstate effect.
- **Article I, Section 8, Clause 1 (Taxing Power) and 16th Amendment:** The payroll tax credits in Stream 3 and the tax reforms in Stream 4 are within Congress's plenary power to lay and collect taxes and to structure the internal revenue code as it determines appropriate.

**Primary statutory vehicles:**
- Small Business Act, 15 U.S.C. §§ 631 et seq. — governs SBA programs and requires expansion of authorization
- Internal Revenue Code, 26 U.S.C. — governs all tax credit and deduction provisions
- CDFI Fund Act, 12 U.S.C. §§ 4701 et seq. — governs CDFI certification and capital deployment
- Budget reconciliation — Stream 3 (payroll tax credits) and Stream 4 (tax reforms) are revenue provisions qualifying for reconciliation under the Congressional Budget Act, 2 U.S.C. § 641, requiring only a Senate majority

## Proposed Legislation

### Main Street Investment Act

- **Purpose:** Establish all five funding streams as permanent annual federal programs; authorize $100 billion in total annual investment in small business capital access, employment, tax relief, and infrastructure
- **Key Provisions:**
  - Creates the Main Street Opportunity Grant Program within SBA (Stream 1)
  - Expands SBA 7(a) lending authority by $30 billion annually with rate cap and no-collateral provisions (Stream 2)
  - Establishes New Hire Credit, Health Insurance Provision Credit, and Apprenticeship and Training Credit under 26 U.S.C. (Stream 3)
  - Makes 100% bonus depreciation permanent for businesses under $10M gross receipts (Stream 4)
  - Restores immediate R&D deductibility for businesses under $50M gross receipts (Stream 4)
  - Establishes Community Business Incubator Fund, SBDC and SCORE full funding, Main Street Broadband, Export Assistance, and Legal Services Access Fund (Stream 5)
  - Mandates 50% targeting to underserved businesses and annual demographic reporting
- **Constitutional Authority:** Spending Clause, Art. I, § 8, cl. 1; Commerce Clause, Art. I, § 8, cl. 3; Taxing Power, Art. I, § 8, cl. 1; 16th Amendment

### Small Business Equity Access Act

- **Purpose:** Establish enforceable anti-discrimination requirements within all federal small business lending and grant programs; mandate CDFI partnerships; require annual demographic audit
- **Key Provisions:**
  - Prohibits any federally funded or federally guaranteed small business lender from engaging in facially neutral practices with demonstrable disparate impact on race, gender, or geographic category (parallel to Equal Credit Opportunity Act, 15 U.S.C. § 1691, extended to SBA programs)
  - Requires all SBA-approved lenders to report loan application, approval, denial, and pricing data by borrower demographic, publicly accessible
  - Establishes CDFI Partnership as mandatory component of any SBA lender operating in a census tract with a median household income below 80% of area median income
  - Annual audit by SBA Inspector General; findings published in Federal Register
- **Constitutional Authority:** Spending Clause; Equal Protection Clause, 14th Amendment; Civil Rights Act of 1964, 42 U.S.C. § 2000a

### America First (Small Business) Tax Act

- **Purpose:** Make targeted tax provisions permanent for small businesses; clean up TCJA provisions that were captured by large corporations at the expense of small businesses
- **Key Provisions:**
  - Permanent 100% bonus depreciation under 26 U.S.C. § 168(k) for businesses with gross receipts below $10 million
  - Restores immediate deductibility of R&D expenditures under 26 U.S.C. § 174 for businesses with gross receipts below $50 million
  - Makes Section 199A deduction permanent for qualified business income below $1 million; eliminates it for real estate LLPs and investment fund structures above that threshold
  - Establishes Anti-Monopoly Procurement Rebate as a refundable credit under 26 U.S.C.
- **Constitutional Authority:** Taxing Power, Art. I, § 8, cl. 1; 16th Amendment; *Helvering v. Davis*, 301 U.S. 619 (1937)

## What Opponents Argue — and Why They Are Wrong

**Argument: "We cannot afford $100 billion per year in new spending."**

The 2017 TCJA delivered an estimated $150 billion per year in corporate tax cuts — a number that is not hypothetical but is recorded in Joint Committee on Taxation and Congressional Budget Office scoring. The carried interest loophole costs $14 billion per year. The estate tax exemption increases in the TCJA cost $20 billion per year. The federal government has already chosen to spend these amounts; the question is not whether to spend but on whom. The Main Street Investment Act proposes to fund itself through a partial corporate rate restoration (21 percent to 28 percent, not 21 to 35 percent), elimination of the carried interest loophole, and reduction in large-corporation subsidies — the revenue sources identified in Reform #41 (Corporate Tax Reform). These are not speculative funding sources. They are documented tax expenditures that can be reversed by statute.

**Argument: "Government should not pick winners and losers."**

The federal government currently picks winners at scale. The carried interest loophole is the government picking private equity fund managers over the workers their funds employ. The 100 percent bonus depreciation provision, as structured in the TCJA, was captured primarily by large corporations with the capital to make large equipment purchases. Allowing Amazon to claim the same depreciation benefits as a local plumber on a $200,000 equipment purchase is the government picking Amazon. The Section 199A pass-through deduction was designed rhetorically for small business but was captured in practice by real estate investors and large professional partnerships. The Main Street Investment Act does not introduce the government into neutrality-disrupting choices. It redirects those choices from concentrated corporate beneficiaries to distributed small business beneficiaries.

**Argument: "Grants create dependency; programs like this should use loans."**

Venture capital — the mechanism by which the largest technology companies in the world obtained their founding capital — provides equity, not loans. Paul Graham's Y Combinator provides cash for equity stakes. Sequoia Capital and Andreessen Horowitz provide cash for equity stakes. None of these arrangements are loans. The founders who received them are not characterized as dependent. The logic that capital without repayment obligation is acceptable for venture-backed technology founders but constitutes problematic dependency for a Black-owned pharmacy in rural Mississippi is not a principled economic argument. It is a racialized double standard dressed in market language.

The German Mittelstand model demonstrates that a combination of grants and low-cost loans, sustained over decades, produces more competitive, more durable, and more equitably distributed small business outcomes than the Anglo-American model of market neutrality combined with venture capital. The SBA's own survival data shows that undercapitalization at founding is the primary predictor of early business failure. A grant at founding prevents permanent business failure far more effectively than a loan that adds debt to an already undercapitalized enterprise.

**Argument: "Fraud risk will be unacceptably high — see the PPP."**

The Paycheck Protection Program's fraud problem resulted from a specific set of emergency decisions: no application verification, no documentation requirements, and turnaround time measured in hours rather than days. Those were deliberate design choices made to get money out immediately in a crisis. The Main Street Investment Act uses SBA's established grant application infrastructure, which requires business registration documentation, income verification, tax return submission, and, for larger grants, a structured review period. The SBIR program — the closest existing analog — has operated since 1982 (15 U.S.C. § 638) with fraud rates that are not materially different from other competitive grant programs. The anti-fraud mechanisms are known and established. The PPP is not the relevant comparison; the SBIR is.

## Implementation Timeline

### Day One Executive Actions

- Issue Executive Order directing SBA to maximize existing program capacity for underserved businesses, with specific numerical targets for women-owned, minority-owned, and rural business approvals in FY2026
- Issue Executive Order directing Treasury to expand CDFI Fund certification processing capacity and expedite certification for qualified organizations in communities currently lacking CDFI coverage
- Issue memorandum to SBA directing emergency expansion of SBDC and SCORE counseling capacity through existing appropriations, including authorization of remote counseling as a primary service delivery mode
- Issue memorandum directing IRS to extend the Employer Health Insurance Credit (26 U.S.C. § 45R) enrollment deadline and simplify the application process through administrative guidance

### First 100 Days

- Submit the Main Street Investment Act, the Small Business Equity Access Act, and the America First (Small Business) Tax Act to Congress as a coordinated legislative package
- Expand the CDFI Partnership Program through existing authority under 12 U.S.C. § 4707, directing the CDFI Fund to prioritize matching capital for rural and Persistent Poverty County CDFIs
- Restore full SBDC and SCORE funding through emergency supplemental appropriations request
- Direct SBA to initiate rulemaking on the Stream 2 loan terms — 2 percent fixed rate and no-collateral provisions for loans under $150,000 — using existing SBA rate-setting authority under 15 U.S.C. § 636(a)

### Year 1: Foundation

- Pass the Main Street Investment Act and companion legislation through budget reconciliation and standard legislative process
- Year 1 New Business Formation Grant and Main Street Innovation Grant programs open for applications; first grant round closes and awards announced
- Stream 2 lending authority deployed; participating lenders certified and operational
- Payroll tax credits in Stream 3 effective for the tax year; IRS guidance issued within 60 days of enactment
- Community Business Incubator Fund requests for proposals published

### Years 2-4: Consolidation and Scale

- Full $100 billion annual program operational; first $25 billion in grants disbursed
- CDFI sector at ten times pre-Act lending capacity; annual demographic reporting published
- Main Street Broadband program fully funded and operational in all 50 states; rural business connectivity gaps measured and tracked
- Small Business Export Assistance reaches 50 or more new international markets
- Legal Services Access Fund operating in all 435 congressional districts
- First annual SBA Inspector General equity audit published; corrective action required for any program falling below 50 percent underserved-business targeting
- Community Business Incubator infrastructure established in every metropolitan statistical area and all rural counties with populations above 10,000

## Talking Points

> The 2017 corporate tax cut gave $150 billion per year to the Fortune 500. We are proposing to give $100 billion per year to the 33 million small businesses that actually employ your neighbors, hire your kids, and make up almost half the American economy. If the question is whether that is too expensive, ask first what we got from the corporate tax cut. Business investment did not surge. Wages did not surge. What surged was stock buybacks — corporations spending over a trillion dollars buying their own shares rather than building anything. We are proposing to spend less than that on the businesses that do build things.

> The American Dream was about starting something — a shop, a firm, a practice, a trade. You worked hard, built something, hired people, and made a life. Private equity and monopoly platform economics have been systematically closing that pathway for thirty years. Amazon's logistics network has displaced an estimated 50,000 local retail businesses. Private equity has bought and stripped thousands of family-owned companies. The carried interest loophole rewards the buyers more than the builders. The Main Street Investment Act is how we rebuild the ladder. It is not a handout. It is a recalibration of a system that has been giving handouts to the wrong people for three decades.

> Black-owned businesses receive 20 cents in bank lending for every dollar white-owned businesses receive. That figure comes from the Federal Reserve. It is not a civil rights advocacy number — it is a central bank survey. A gap that large and that persistent is not a market outcome. It is a structural failure in capital allocation that has compounded over decades. You do not fix it with good intentions. You fix it with dedicated capital, explicit targeting, and accountability for lenders who perpetuate it. That is precisely what this legislation does. That is the only thing that has ever worked.

> Germany's small and mid-sized manufacturing companies are the backbone of the German economy. Germany exports more per capita than the United States — a country five times its size — largely because it treats small business capital access as industrial policy, not charity. Germany does not ask whether supporting the Mittelstand is affordable. It asks whether it can afford not to. We have been asking the wrong question for thirty years. The Main Street Investment Act asks the right one.

> Without active intervention, the trend line runs in one direction. Six corporations control 90 percent of American media. Three companies control 80 percent of grocery distribution. Four airlines control 80 percent of domestic flights. Five technology platforms control most of American digital commerce. In every case, the concentration happened not because large companies were better, but because federal tax and regulatory policy tilted the playing field and then stood aside. The question is not whether government intervenes — it already intervenes, and it does so constantly. The question is whether it intervenes for the monopolies or for Main Street. This act chooses Main Street.
