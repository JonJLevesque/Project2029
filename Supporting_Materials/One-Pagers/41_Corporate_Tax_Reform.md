## The Argument in One Sentence

Fifty-five Fortune 500 companies paid zero dollars in federal income taxes in 2020 while reporting more than $40 billion in combined profits — and the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act was the statutory architecture that made it possible.

## The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act: What It Did and Who It Served

### A $1.9 Trillion Wealth Transfer Disguised as Tax Reform

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 (TCJA), signed by President Trump on December 22, 2017, was the most significant restructuring of the U.S. tax code since the Tax Reform Act of 1986. Its primary effects:

| TCJA Provision | Before (2016) | After (2017) | Effect |
|---------------|---------------|--------------|--------|
| Corporate income tax rate | 35% statutory | 21% statutory | $1 trillion+ corporate tax cut over 10 years |
| Pass-through deduction (Section 199A) | Did not exist | 20% deduction on qualified business income | Windfall for wealthy pass-through owners (real estate, law firms, private equity) |
| Repatriation rate on offshore profits | 35% (deferred) | 15.5% cash / 8% illiquid (one-time) | $400B+ in offshore profits repatriated at reduced rates |
| Global Intangible Low-Taxed Income (GILTI) | Did not exist | 10.5% minimum rate on foreign profits | Far below domestic rate; created continued offshore incentive |
| Base Erosion Anti-Abuse Tax (BEAT) | Did not exist | 10% minimum on certain payments to foreign affiliates | Insufficient to stop profit-shifting |
| Alternative Minimum Tax (corporate AMT) | 20% | Eliminated entirely | Removed backstop against corporate tax avoidance |
| Bonus depreciation | 50% | 100% (immediate full expensing) | Companies could write off entire asset cost in year one |
| 10-year revenue impact | — | -$1.9 trillion (CBO, 2017 estimate) | Deficit-financed tax cut; extended by Trump in 2025 |

The TCJA was not revenue-neutral. The Congressional Budget Office projected it would add $1.9 trillion to the federal deficit over ten years, before accounting for macroeconomic feedback effects. The tax cuts were permanent for corporations and time-limited for individuals — a deliberate structural choice that prioritized corporate beneficiaries.

## Zero Tax: The Documentation

### 55 Fortune 500 Companies. $40 Billion in Profits. $0 in Federal Income Taxes.

The Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy (ITEP) analyzed the 2020 financial statements of all Fortune 500 companies. Its findings:

| Zero-Tax Company Data | 2020 Figures |
|----------------------|-------------|
| Fortune 500 companies that paid $0 in federal income taxes | 55 |
| Combined pre-tax profits of those 55 companies | $40.5 billion |
| Combined federal income tax paid | $0 |
| Combined federal tax rebates received | $3.5 billion (negative tax rate) |

These are not small companies operating near breakeven. They include some of the most profitable corporations in the United States. Among the zero-tax corporations in recent years:

- **Amazon:** Reported $2.3 billion in U.S. profits in 2018; paid $0 in federal income taxes; received a $129 million tax rebate (effective rate: -5.6%).
- **Boeing:** Reported $1.4 billion in U.S. profits in 2019; paid $0 in federal income taxes; received a $20 million tax rebate.
- **Chevron:** Reported $4.5 billion in U.S. profits in 2018; paid $0 in federal income taxes.
- **Eli Lilly:** Reported $2.3 billion in U.S. profits in 2018; paid $0 in federal income taxes.
- **Duke Energy:** Reported $3.8 billion in U.S. profits across 2018–2020; paid a combined effective rate below zero.

These companies use roads, courts, patent protections, a publicly educated workforce, and the full apparatus of the U.S. federal government to generate their profits. They paid nothing for it.

## The Offshore Game: How Corporate America Moves Profits Out of the Country

### Profit-Shifting: The Legal Mechanism for Tax Evasion at Scale

"Profit-shifting" is the practice of using internal corporate transactions — typically involving intellectual property licensing and intercompany loans — to move accounting profits from high-tax jurisdictions (the United States) to low-tax or zero-tax jurisdictions (Ireland, Bermuda, the Cayman Islands, Luxembourg). It is technically legal under current law. It costs the U.S. Treasury an estimated $70–$90 billion annually.

**The Apple Ireland case** is the defining example. Apple established subsidiaries in Ireland structured to take advantage of a mismatch between Irish and U.S. tax law: Irish law taxed based on where a company was managed; U.S. law taxed based on where a company was incorporated. Apple's Irish subsidiaries were incorporated in Ireland but managed from the United States — leaving them resident in neither country for tax purposes. Through this structure, Apple routed tens of billions of dollars in profits through Ireland and paid an effective tax rate of approximately 0.005 percent in 2014. The European Commission ruled this an illegal state aid arrangement in 2016 and required Ireland to collect 13 billion euros in back taxes from Apple. Apple and Ireland jointly appealed. The structure was, under U.S. law, largely permissible.

| Major Offshore Tax Haven | Common Corporate Users | Mechanism |
|--------------------------|----------------------|-----------|
| Ireland | Apple, Google, Facebook | IP licensing; transfer pricing |
| Bermuda | Reinsurance industry; tech firms | Zero corporate tax rate |
| Cayman Islands | Hedge funds; financial services | No income tax; confidentiality |
| Luxembourg | Amazon, PayPal, Skype | Favorable IP and royalty tax treatment |
| Netherlands | Shell, Starbucks, IKEA | Advance tax rulings; holding company structures |

The "Double Irish" arrangement — using two Irish-incorporated entities with different tax residency claims — was the most widespread profit-shifting structure of the 2000s and 2010s. Ireland agreed to phase it out under international pressure, but equivalent structures continue to proliferate in other jurisdictions.

The TCJA's GILTI provision was intended to address offshore profit-shifting by imposing a minimum tax on foreign profits. At 10.5 percent — half the domestic corporate rate — GILTI created an incentive to book profits offshore (10.5 percent is better than 21 percent). It did not end profit-shifting. It subsidized it.

## What Needs to Change: The Reform Package

### Five Interlocking Reforms That Work Together

The following five reforms, taken together, would raise an estimated $300 billion or more over ten years, close the principal avenues of corporate tax avoidance, and restore a tax system in which profitable corporations pay taxes on their profits.

**Reform 1: Restore the Corporate Tax Rate to 28 Percent**

The TCJA cut the corporate rate from 35 percent to 21 percent. Restoring it to 28 percent — the level proposed in President Biden's American Jobs Plan — raises an estimated $1.3 trillion over ten years (CBO, 2021). It is not a return to the pre-TCJA rate; 28 percent is between the old 35 percent and the current 21 percent. It is defensible as a compromise and still below the average of other G7 nations when local and state taxes are factored in.

The competitive argument against a higher corporate rate is weak. The U.S. was competitive with a 35 percent statutory rate for decades. The G7 average corporate tax rate (including subnational taxes) is approximately 25 percent. A 28 percent rate would place the U.S. in a competitive range, not an outlier position.

**Reform 2: Implement the Global Minimum Tax at 21 Percent (OECD Pillar 2 Alignment)**

The OECD/G20 Inclusive Framework on Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS) established a global minimum corporate tax of 15 percent through "Pillar 2," effective for most participating jurisdictions beginning in 2024. The Biden administration negotiated this agreement. The Trump administration rejected it.

The 15 percent OECD minimum is insufficient. The United States should implement a 21 percent domestic minimum on foreign profits (GILTI reform) — doubling the current 10.5 percent rate. This would:

- Eliminate the tax advantage of booking profits in low-tax jurisdictions
- Raise an estimated $350 billion over ten years (Biden administration estimate)
- Position the U.S. to lead global adoption of a higher minimum rate through bilateral and multilateral pressure

**Reform 3: Close Offshore Loopholes — Economic Substance Rules**

Corporate tax inversions — the practice of reincorporating a U.S. company in a foreign jurisdiction (typically Ireland or the UK) through a merger with a smaller foreign company — allow U.S. multinationals to escape the U.S. corporate tax system entirely while maintaining all their U.S. operations. The Treasury Department has used regulatory authority to restrict inversions since 2016; a new administration should codify and strengthen these restrictions.

Proposed reforms:
- Strengthen "economic substance" requirements: to be treated as a foreign corporation, a company must have genuine management, operations, and employees in the claimed jurisdiction — not just a post office box
- Deny deductions for payments to affiliates in jurisdictions with effective rates below the U.S. minimum
- Require country-by-country reporting of profits, employees, and taxes paid, publicly disclosed

**Reform 4: 4 Percent Stock Buyback Excise Tax**

Stock buybacks — corporations repurchasing their own shares on the open market — are a mechanism for distributing profits to shareholders in a form that was historically taxed at lower capital gains rates than dividends. The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 established a 1 percent excise tax on stock buybacks. This was a significant achievement. It is insufficient.

A 4 percent excise tax on stock buybacks would:
- Raise an estimated $124 billion over ten years (Tax Policy Center)
- Reduce the tax preference for buybacks over productive investment
- Maintain the existing structure — corporations can still buy back stock, but they pay a meaningful tax cost for doing so

S&P 500 companies spent approximately $800 billion on stock buybacks in 2022 and over $1 trillion in 2023. At a 4 percent rate, this buyback activity alone would generate approximately $32–40 billion annually.

**Reform 5: End Stepped-Up Basis on Inherited Business Assets**

Under current law, when a business owner dies and passes the business to heirs, the heirs receive a "stepped-up" basis: the cost basis of the asset is reset to the fair market value at the time of inheritance. Unrealized appreciation — the growth in value over the owner's lifetime — is never taxed. This is sometimes called the "Angel of Death" loophole.

Eliminating stepped-up basis on inherited business assets above a threshold (for example, $5 million per heir) would:
- Raise an estimated $320 billion over ten years (Biden administration estimate)
- Subject to taxation the same capital gains that would have been taxed had the asset been sold during the owner's lifetime
- Close a loophole that disproportionately benefits the wealthiest estates

## What the Inflation Reduction Act Did — and Did Not — Do

### The IRA Was a Down Payment, Not a Solution

The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 made two significant reforms to corporate taxation:

1. **15 percent corporate alternative minimum tax (CAMT):** Applied to corporations with average annual adjusted financial statement income exceeding $1 billion. Estimated to raise $222 billion over ten years.

2. **1 percent stock buyback excise tax:** Raised approximately $74 billion over ten years as estimated.

These were real achievements. They do not come close to addressing the scale of corporate tax avoidance documented above. The 15 percent CAMT applies to book income — accounting profits — which is itself subject to aggressive management. The 1 percent buyback tax is too low to meaningfully alter corporate behavior. The full reform package proposed here builds on the IRA's foundation and goes substantially further.

## Constitutional and Legislative Authority

**Constitutional basis:** Article I, Section 8 of the U.S. Constitution grants Congress the power "to lay and collect taxes." The 16th Amendment provides: "The Congress shall have power to lay and collect taxes on incomes, from whatever source derived." This is broad, unambiguous taxing authority. There is no constitutional impediment to taxing corporate income, buybacks, or unrealized gains at death.

**Legislative authority:**
- Internal Revenue Code (26 U.S.C.) — the primary statutory vehicle for all corporate tax provisions
- Budget reconciliation — corporate tax rate changes and most of the package are revenue-raising measures, qualifying for reconciliation with a simple Senate majority
- OECD Pillar 2 implementation — can be accomplished through domestic legislation aligning GILTI rates with international commitments

## The Competitive Myth

### The Argument That a Higher Rate Will Drive Investment Away Does Not Hold Up

The corporate rate debate is dominated by the claim that higher rates will drive corporate investment offshore. The evidence does not support this claim at the levels under discussion.

- The United States maintained a 35 percent corporate tax rate from 1993 to 2017 — a period of sustained economic growth, significant corporate investment, and the rise of the most valuable companies in the world (Amazon, Google, Apple, Facebook).
- The TCJA's cut to 21 percent did not produce the promised investment surge. Business investment as a share of GDP did not increase in the two years following the TCJA's passage.
- The Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta studied the TCJA and found that "the evidence of large investment responses to the TCJA is weak."
- At 28 percent, the U.S. rate would be consistent with or below the OECD average; it would not be a global outlier.

The companies that paid zero in taxes are not investment-constrained. Amazon, Boeing, and Chevron did not need a zero-dollar tax bill to make investment decisions. They needed their accountants and lawyers to exploit available provisions. The reform package closes those provisions.

## What Opponents Argue — and Why They Are Wrong

### The Corporate Tax Lobby's Playbook

**Argument: "Raising the corporate rate will kill jobs."**

This is the most frequently made and least supported claim in corporate tax policy debates. The empirical record does not support it. The United States maintained a 35 percent corporate rate from 1993 to 2017 — during which U.S. employment grew from approximately 120 million to 150 million workers. The TCJA's cut to 21 percent did not produce the promised employment surge; job growth in the two years following the TCJA continued at approximately the same pace as the prior two years. The Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco's analysis of post-TCJA investment found no statistically significant increase in business fixed investment attributable to the rate cut.

**Argument: "American companies will move overseas."**

Corporate inversions are already addressed in the reform package through economic substance rules. More fundamentally: the companies that profit from U.S. markets, U.S. infrastructure, U.S. intellectual property protections, and the U.S. rule of law cannot simply relocate. Amazon's U.S. customers are in the United States. Google's advertising revenue depends on the U.S. market. Boeing's defense contracts require being an American company. The threat of relocation is largely a negotiating posture, not a business reality.

**Argument: "Higher corporate taxes hurt small businesses."**

The 28 percent rate applies to C corporations — primarily large publicly traded companies and their subsidiaries. Small businesses organized as S corporations, LLCs, sole proprietorships, and partnerships are taxed through the individual income tax, not the corporate income tax. The pass-through deduction created by the TCJA disproportionately benefited wealthy individuals with high pass-through income — real estate investors, large law firms, private equity — not the small businesses that politicians claim to protect. Reforming the corporate rate does not raise taxes on a family-owned restaurant or a Main Street retailer.

**Argument: "The 15 percent IRA minimum is enough."**

The 15 percent corporate alternative minimum tax established by the Inflation Reduction Act is a significant step. It is not enough. It applies only to corporations with average annual book income above $1 billion — approximately 150 companies. It is assessed on book income, which is itself subject to substantial accounting manipulation. It leaves the primary corporate rate at 21 percent, the offshore minimum at 10.5 percent, and the fundamental offshore profit-shifting incentives in place. The 15 percent CAMT is a floor, not a ceiling.

The revenue generated by comprehensive corporate tax reform is not a budgetary line item. It is the difference between a federal government that can invest in the future and one that cannot:

| Investment Area | Annual Cost Estimate | Corporate Tax Revenue Needed |

Restoring that revenue base is the precondition for governing.

## Implementation and Legislative Path

### What Can Be Done and When

**Day One Executive Actions:**
- Direct Treasury to issue guidance strengthening economic substance rules for corporate inversions
- Reinstate and strengthen Obama-era regulations restricting earnings stripping by inverted corporations
- Direct IRS to prioritize audit resources on large corporate returns, transfer pricing arrangements, and offshore income reporting

**Budget Reconciliation (Majority Vote — Senate):**
- Corporate rate restoration to 28 percent
- GILTI reform to 21 percent
- Stock buyback excise tax increase to 4 percent
- End of stepped-up basis on inherited business assets above threshold
- These are revenue-raising measures that qualify for reconciliation under the Congressional Budget Act

**Standard Legislation (Requires Filibuster Reform or 60 Votes):**
- Statutory economic substance rules codified in the Internal Revenue Code
- Country-by-country public reporting requirements
- OECD Pillar 2 alignment codified in domestic law

**Key Legislation:** Corporate Fairness and Investment Act — a comprehensive vehicle combining rate restoration, offshore minimum reform, buyback tax, stepped-up basis reform, and anti-inversion measures. Draft language should align with Biden-era Treasury proposals, updated for the current fiscal baseline.
