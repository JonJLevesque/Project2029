# REFORM #2: SENATE TERM LIMITS
## End Lifelong Political Careers. 12 Years Maximum.

---

## üö® THE PROBLEM

**Senators without term limits become:**
- Entrenched incumbents immune to accountability
- Creatures of special interests (decades of donor relationships)
- Out of touch with constituents (Washington insiders)
- Focused on re-election, not governance
- Obstacles to necessary change

**By the numbers:**
- Average Senate tenure: 10+ years (many serve 30-40+ years)
- Incumbent re-election rate: 90%+ (effectively lifetime appointments)
- 20+ Senators over age 70 (some over 80)
- Strom Thurmond served 48 years (died in office at 100)
- Dianne Feinstein served 31 years (died in office at 90)

**The result:**
- Gerontocracy unresponsive to younger generations
- Entrenchment prevents fresh ideas and perspectives
- "Old boys club" protects itself over the people
- Democratic accountability eroded

---

## ‚úÖ THE SOLUTION

### 31st Amendment (Senate Term Limits)

**Text:**
```
Section 1: No person shall serve more than two consecutive terms as
United States Senator (12 years total).

Section 2: Terms served prior to ratification do not count toward limit.

Section 3: Senators may run again after sitting out one full term (6 years).

Section 4: This amendment applies to all Senators elected after ratification.
```

**What this means:**
- Maximum 12 consecutive years in Senate
- Can return after 6-year break (prevents total ban)
- Encourages turnover and fresh perspectives
- Grandfathers current Senators (prevents chaos)

### Why 12 Years (Two Terms)?

**Long enough to:**
- Develop expertise and effectiveness
- Build relationships and coalitions
- Accomplish major legislative goals
- Serve through multiple political cycles

**Short enough to:**
- Prevent entrenchment and corruption
- Maintain connection to constituents
- Allow generational turnover
- Keep ambitions focused on governing, not re-election

### Implementation Details

**Transition Period:**
- Amendment takes effect for Senators elected AFTER ratification
- Current Senators not forced out (grandfathered)
- Natural turnover over 12 years as limits apply

**After 6-Year Break Rule:**
- Senators can run again after sitting out one term
- Prevents "musical chairs" but allows return of proven leaders
- Example: Serve 12 years ‚Üí 6 years out ‚Üí eligible again

## üí¨ TALKING POINTS

**"Don't we lose experienced legislators?"**
‚Üí Yes, but we also lose CORRUPT legislators. 12 years is plenty to develop expertise. House has no term limits and still functions. The loss of institutional knowledge is worth the gain in accountability and fresh perspectives.

**"Won't this empower lobbyists and staff?"**
‚Üí Lobbyists already have too much power BECAUSE of long-term Senator relationships. Turnover makes lobbying harder, not easier. Staff turnover happens anyway‚Äîthis doesn't change that.

**"Shouldn't voters decide?"**
‚Üí Voters DO decide‚Äîthey'll ratify this amendment. But 90% incumbent re-election shows system is rigged (gerrymandering, donor advantage, name recognition). Term limits level the playing field so voters have REAL choices.

**"Why 12 years specifically?"**
‚Üí Balances expertise vs. entrenchment. Shorter than 12 = not enough time to be effective. Longer than 12 = too much entrenchment. Two full terms is the sweet spot.

**"Won't Senators become 'lame ducks'?"**
‚Üí Better to be a "lame duck" focused on policy than a desperate incumbent focused on re-election. Final terms often produce best legislation (no re-election fears). Plus, Senators can return after 6 years if they're good.
