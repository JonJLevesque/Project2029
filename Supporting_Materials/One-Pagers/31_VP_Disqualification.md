# Reform #31: Two-Term President VP Disqualification

## Proposed 35th Constitutional Amendment

**The Problem:** The 22nd Amendment bars a two-term president from seeking a third term as president — but contains no explicit prohibition on that same person serving as Vice President and then ascending to the presidency through succession or election. This is a structural loophole that undermines the entire purpose of presidential term limits.

**The Solution:** The 35th Amendment closes this loophole permanently. No person who has served two terms as president (or more than four years by succession) may hold the office of Vice President or succeed to the presidency by any constitutional mechanism.

---

## What the 22nd Amendment Already Does

The 22nd Amendment (ratified 1951) states that no person shall be elected President more than twice. But legal scholars have long noted:

- It does not bar a former two-term president from serving as VP
- Under the 12th Amendment, the VP must be constitutionally eligible to be president — but the meaning of "eligible" is disputed when a former two-term president is on the ticket
- Constitutional ambiguity invites litigation, manipulation, and democratic erosion

The 35th Amendment resolves this ambiguity by constitutional text — not court interpretation.

---

## Proposed Amendment Text

**Section 1.** No person who has been elected to the office of President twice, or who has served as President for more than four years by virtue of succession or otherwise, shall be eligible to hold the office of Vice President of the United States.

**Section 2.** No such person shall be eligible to succeed to the Presidency through any constitutional provision, including but not limited to Article II and the Twenty-Fifth Amendment.

**Section 3.** Congress shall have power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation.

**The core principle:** A limit is only a limit if it cannot be evaded. The 22nd Amendment represents the democratic consensus that no individual should dominate the presidency indefinitely. The 35th Amendment ensures that consensus is honored in fact, not just in form.

## Does the 12th Amendment Already Cover This?

**The argument that it does:** The 12th Amendment (1804) states that "no person constitutionally ineligible to the office of President shall be eligible to that of Vice-President." If a two-term former president is "constitutionally ineligible" to be president under the 22nd Amendment, then by this logic, they're automatically ineligible to be VP too — no new amendment needed.

**Why that argument is insufficient:**

**1. The textual problem.** The 22nd Amendment bars *election* to a third presidential term — it does not declare a two-term president "ineligible" to hold the office in a broader constitutional sense. A former president could be *appointed* VP under the 25th Amendment succession provisions. The 12th Amendment only applies to election — not to succession. That gap is real, and it's textually unresolved.

**2. No court has ever ruled on this.** The interaction between the 12th and 22nd Amendments has never been adjudicated. That means any attempt to put a two-term former president on a VP ticket — or to have them ascend through succession — would immediately trigger litigation. That litigation could take years. It could go to a Supreme Court without obvious precedent. The outcome is genuinely uncertain.

**3. The "ineligible" language was written in 1804.** When the 12th Amendment was drafted, the only presidential eligibility requirements were Article II's age, citizenship, and residency criteria. Courts must determine whether "constitutionally ineligible" as written in 1804 incorporates a term-limit bar added 147 years later. Textualists and originalists will argue it does not. That is a serious legal argument, not a frivolous one.

**4. Conservative scholars have staked out the other side.** Prominent conservative legal academics — including those with significant influence over the current Supreme Court majority — have argued that the 12th Amendment does not incorporate the 22nd Amendment's bar. Under this reading, a two-term former president could constitutionally appear on a ticket as VP. *The current Court might agree.*

**5. "Probably constitutional" is not the same as "constitutionally closed."** A norm that depends on litigation outcomes and judicial temperament is not a structural safeguard. It is a bet. The 35th Amendment converts an uncertain judicial question into clear constitutional text — which is exactly what the amendment process is for.

**Bottom line:** The 12th Amendment *might* cover this. But the whole point of a constitutional amendment is to close the gap with certainty rather than leave it to courts to resolve under political pressure. The Framers did not rely on "probably fine" — they wrote it down. The 35th Amendment does the same.

## The Loophole in Plain Language

Imagine this scenario:
1. President serves two full terms.
2. President is constitutionally barred from running for president again.
3. President runs for — and wins — Vice President on a different ticket.
4. That president becomes incapacitated or resigns.
5. The two-term former president is now president again.

This is not a hypothetical. It is a structural vulnerability in the current constitutional text. The 35th Amendment forecloses it.

## Historical Context: Has This Been a Real Issue?

The VP loophole has not been exploited in American history — but it has come closer to becoming reality than most people realize, and structural vulnerabilities do not require prior exploitation to demand closure.

**The 2024 scenario:** During the 2024 presidential campaign cycle, discussions emerged — including in conservative legal commentary — about whether Donald Trump could constitutionally appear on a ticket as Vice President if he were to lose the Republican primary or choose to position himself for the 2028 cycle. While Trump ultimately secured the Republican nomination and won the presidency outright, the legal question was taken seriously enough to generate substantial law review commentary.

**The FDR precedent that shaped the 22nd Amendment:** Franklin Roosevelt's four presidential terms (1933-1945) directly prompted the 22nd Amendment. The framers of the 22nd Amendment were focused on presidential terms — not on the VP loophole — because the concept of a popular former president strategically running as VP to circumvent term limits had not yet been conceived as a practical political maneuver. The 22nd Amendment was drafted with the direct problem in mind, not its structural workaround.

**Historically floated scenarios:**
- Bill Clinton in 2008: Some commentators asked whether Clinton could constitutionally run as VP for Barack Obama. Clinton himself declined to engage the question seriously. Legal scholars were split.
- Obama in 2016 and beyond: Similar commentary emerged about whether Barack Obama could serve as VP or be appointed VP under the 25th Amendment. The question has been raised repeatedly without resolution.
- The common thread: Each time, the conclusion is the same — the answer is genuinely uncertain, litigation would be immediate, and the outcome would depend on a Supreme Court ruling under political pressure.

## The Succession Chain Problem: 25th Amendment Exposure

The VP loophole is not just about a two-term former president running for VP in a general election. It also creates a secondary vulnerability through the 25th Amendment succession mechanism.

**The scenario:**

1. A sitting President appoints a VP nominee under the 25th Amendment, Section 2 (used when a VP vacancy occurs mid-term).
2. The nominated VP is a two-term former president.
3. Congress confirms the nomination (requires majority vote of both chambers).
4. The sitting President becomes incapacitated, resigns, or dies.
5. The two-term former president is now President again — by succession, not election.

**This path does not require a single voter to cast a ballot for the two-term former president as President.** It requires only: a VP vacancy, a willing sitting President, and a congressional majority willing to confirm the nomination.

**Why this matters:** A two-term former president serving as VP is simultaneously:
- First in the line of presidential succession
- Potentially the dominant political figure in the administration
- Structurally positioned to return to the presidency through mechanisms entirely outside voter control

The 35th Amendment closes this by barring the two-term former president from holding the VP office through any means — election, appointment, or succession — and from succeeding to the presidency through any constitutional mechanism.

## Opposition Arguments and Detailed Rebuttals

### Objection 1: "Voters should decide. If people want a two-term former president to be VP, who are we to stop them?"

**The rebuttal:** The 22nd Amendment already answers this question. Voters accepted — through ratification by 41 states — that no individual should serve more than two terms as president. The consensus was clear: term limits represent a structural value that overrides any particular electoral preference. The 35th Amendment extends that same principle to its logical conclusion. "Voters should decide" is the same argument that was made against the 22nd Amendment itself. The Framers' answer was: some structural limits are not subject to popular override, precisely because popular sentiment can be manipulated in ways that undermine democratic continuity. A charismatic two-term former president running as VP is exactly the scenario where "voters should decide" reasoning becomes most dangerous — the structural safeguard is most necessary precisely when a popular figure makes circumventing it seem appealing.

### Objection 2: "This is hypothetical. No one has actually done this."

**The rebuttal:** Constitutional vulnerabilities do not require exploitation before closure. No president had exploited the absence of term limits before FDR — the 22nd Amendment was passed prophylactically, not reactively. The structural risk is real: the loophole exists in the constitutional text, legal scholars have identified it, and the political incentives to exploit it are obvious. Waiting for exploitation means waiting until the damage is done and a constitutional crisis is already underway. The amendment process exists to address structural vulnerabilities — not just to react to completed harms.

### Objection 3: "The 22nd Amendment already covers this — it bars a former two-term president from being 'elected' to the presidency again, which means they can't be VP under the 12th Amendment."

**The expanded rebuttal:**

This is the most sophisticated objection and deserves a thorough response.

The argument runs: The 12th Amendment (1804) states that "no person constitutionally ineligible to the office of President shall be eligible to that of Vice-President." Since a two-term president is ineligible to be president under the 22nd Amendment, they are automatically ineligible to be VP — no new amendment needed.

**Why this argument is insufficient on five independent grounds:**

**Ground 1 — Textual precision.** The 22nd Amendment bars *election* to a third presidential *term*. It does not declare a two-term president globally "constitutionally ineligible to the office of President" in the sense the 12th Amendment uses that phrase. A two-term president is still eligible to hold the office — they simply cannot be *elected* to it a third time. This textual distinction matters: a two-term president could be *appointed* VP under the 25th Amendment, and could then *succeed* to the presidency. Neither path involves an "election" to a presidential term, so the 22nd Amendment bar, read narrowly, does not apply.

**Ground 2 — The 1804 meaning problem.** The 12th Amendment was drafted in 1804, when the only "constitutionally ineligible" persons were those who failed Article II's age, citizenship, and residency requirements. Originalist and textualist justices — the current Supreme Court majority's dominant interpretive methodology — will argue that "constitutionally ineligible" as written in 1804 refers to those original Article II requirements, not to a term-limit bar added 147 years later. This is a legitimate, serious textual argument, not a fringe position.

**Ground 3 — No binding precedent.** No federal court at any level has ever ruled on whether the 12th and 22nd Amendments interact to bar a two-term former president from the VP office. The question is entirely open. Any attempt to put a two-term former president on a ticket immediately generates a lawsuit, proceeds through the federal courts at emergency speed, and lands before the Supreme Court in a politically charged environment with no precedent to guide it. The outcome is genuinely uncertain.

**Ground 4 — Conservative legal scholarship goes the other way.** Prominent constitutional scholars associated with the Federalist Society and the originalist interpretive tradition have argued that the 12th Amendment's "constitutionally ineligible" language does not incorporate the 22nd Amendment's bar. Their argument is not frivolous — it is anchored in serious textualist methodology. Given the composition of the current Supreme Court, there is a meaningful probability that this argument would prevail.

**Ground 5 — "Probably covered" is not structural protection.** Constitutional safeguards should not rely on favorable litigation outcomes before a politically appointed court. The entire purpose of a constitutional amendment is to convert an uncertain legal question into clear, binding constitutional text. The Framers did not say "we probably don't need a Bill of Rights because the structure implies individual rights." They wrote it down. The 35th Amendment applies the same principle: we do not gamble on judicial interpretation when we can provide textual clarity.

## State Analogues: Governors and Term Limit Enforcement

Several states have confronted the question of whether a term-limited executive can seek lower executive office — and have resolved it by clear statutory or constitutional text.

**States with explicit bars on term-limited governors returning through succession or other office:**
- Virginia's Constitution bars its governor from succeeding themselves but includes explicit provisions governing when a lieutenant governor may succeed. Virginia's approach illustrates the need for precise drafting — vague term limit provisions generate litigation.
- California's term limit structure (Proposition 28, 2012) applies a lifetime cap on legislative service rather than consecutive-term limits, eliminating the workaround entirely.
- At the state level, the pattern is clear: wherever term limit language is ambiguous, politicians exploit the ambiguity. Where it is precise, the limit holds.

**The principle:** State experience with gubernatorial term limits confirms that clear constitutional text is the only reliable protection. Ambiguous limits generate creative workarounds; precise limits do not.

## The "Kingmaker" Scenario

Perhaps the most underappreciated risk of the VP loophole is not that a two-term former president would directly circumvent term limits through succession — but that they would exercise de facto presidential power without any of the electoral accountability that the 22nd Amendment was designed to impose.

**The scenario:**
- A two-term former president with high approval ratings and a devoted political base runs as VP on a ticket with a politically weaker candidate
- The ticket wins because of the former president's popularity
- The sitting President — aware that the former president commands greater loyalty within the party, the cabinet, and the electorate — defers to the VP's judgment on major decisions
- The former president effectively governs as a co-president or shadow president, exercising presidential power without the constitutional bar on their doing so directly
- If the sitting President leaves office, the former president succeeds — completing the circumvention

**This is not hypothetical in character.** It describes a structurally predictable dynamic whenever a dominant political figure is barred from the top job but can occupy the second slot. The constitutional text provides no protection against this scenario — only the 35th Amendment does.

## Q&A: Five Common Questions

**Q: Does the 35th Amendment apply if a former president served less than two full terms?**

A: No. The amendment text tracks the 22nd Amendment's standard precisely: it applies to a person who has been *elected* to the office of President twice, or who has *served as President for more than four years by virtue of succession or otherwise*. A person who served a partial term by succession (for example, a VP who completed a predecessor's term) would only be covered if that partial term exceeded four years. This matches how the 22nd Amendment calculates eligibility.

**Q: What if the two-term former president is appointed VP under the 25th Amendment during a crisis — doesn't this create a problem if there's no one else available?**

A: The 25th Amendment VP appointment process requires confirmation by both chambers of Congress. The 35th Amendment simply adds that a two-term former president is not eligible for that appointment. This does not create a void in the succession chain — it simply means the sitting President must nominate someone else. The universe of qualified persons for VP is not so small that a two-term former president is the only option.

**Q: Couldn't Congress just pass a statute clarifying that a two-term former president can't be VP?**

A: No statute can override the Constitution. If the 12th Amendment, read by a court, permits a two-term former president to be VP, then a statute to the contrary is unconstitutional. The 35th Amendment is the only way to guarantee closure of the loophole at the constitutional level.

**Q: Won't this amendment be used to disqualify popular former presidents who the people genuinely want in the government?**

A: The premise of the 22nd Amendment — and the 35th Amendment — is that some structural protections are more important than any individual's popularity. The same argument was made against the 22nd Amendment: FDR was enormously popular, and some believed restricting his service was undemocratic. The counter-argument — that no individual should dominate executive power indefinitely, regardless of popularity — prevailed. The 35th Amendment extends that logic.

**Q: Is there any scenario where the 35th Amendment is too broad?**

A: The amendment is carefully targeted. It does not bar two-term former presidents from other government service — they may serve in the Cabinet, as diplomats, in the judiciary, or in Congress. It bars only the specific combination of VP office and presidential succession that creates the term-limit circumvention risk. It is narrow in scope and precise in application.

## Ratification Strategy: How This Amendment Gets Passed

The 35th Amendment faces the same ratification challenge as any constitutional amendment: 2/3 of both chambers of Congress, then 3/4 of state legislatures (38 states). This is a high bar — but the VP loophole amendment has characteristics that make ratification more achievable than it might appear.

**The bipartisan case:** Presidential term limits enjoy broad public support across party lines — consistently 70%+ in polling. The argument for closing the VP loophole is fundamentally about structural integrity, not partisan advantage. Republicans who supported the 22nd Amendment's term limits have no principled basis for opposing an amendment that enforces those same limits. Democrats who support democratic accountability have equal reason to support it. The strongest case is not partisan — it is structural.

**The state legislature path:** Unlike a constitutional convention (Article V's alternative route), Congress initiates this amendment in the standard way. State legislative ratification follows. Because the amendment is not politically threatening to state-level officials — it applies only to federal executive positions — state legislators face no personal stake in opposing it. The ratification politics are different from amendments that redistribute power between states and the federal government.

**Timing:** The strongest moment to advance this amendment is during a period when no specific individual is positioned to exploit the loophole — i.e., when it is clearly prospective rather than targeted at a current political figure. Amendments that appear targeted at individuals generate partisan resistance; amendments that appear structural and forward-looking attract broader coalitions. This amendment should be introduced and advanced during periods when its application is clearly hypothetical.

**Presidential advocacy:** The sitting President's endorsement of this amendment — particularly if that President is or will be term-limited — is the single most powerful argument for ratification. A two-term president saying "I want to make sure no one can use the VP slot to get around the limit I am subject to" is an unanswerable statement of principle.

The 35th Amendment works alongside:
- **[36th Amendment](32_Felon_Disqualification.md):** Bars convicted felons from the presidency — together, these two amendments close the two most significant eligibility gaps in current constitutional text
- **[29th Amendment](../../Section6_Safeguarding_Democracy_And_Accountability/Chapter_29_Structural_Safeguards.md):** No presidential immunity for crimes committed in office — eliminates the incentive to use the presidency as a shield against prosecution
- **[Ch 29: Structural Safeguards](../../Section6_Safeguarding_Democracy_And_Accountability/Chapter_29_Structural_Safeguards.md):** Full structural accountability framework
- **[Ch 31: Constitutional Hardball](../../Section6_Safeguarding_Democracy_And_Accountability/Chapter_31_Constitutional_Hardball.md):** Using all constitutional tools to lock in structural reforms before they are needed

The 22nd Amendment established the principle: no indefinite presidential power. The 35th Amendment enforces it.
