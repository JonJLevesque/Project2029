## THE PROBLEM

**Six corporations now control 90% of what Americans watch, hear, and read. A single billionaire family's political operation runs through the federal licenses of 185 local TV stations. The airwaves belong to the American people. The people are not getting a return on that asset.**

### The Concentration Timeline

| Year | Companies Controlling 90% of U.S. Media |
|------|----------------------------------------|
| 1983 | 50 |
| 1990 | 29 |
| 2000 | 10 |
| 2010 | 6 |
| 2024 | **6** |

**One statistic defines the crisis:** In 1983, fifty independent companies competed for American audiences. Today, six corporations — and in broadcast specifically, a handful of billionaire-controlled conglomerates — control the same 90% share. This is not a market outcome. It is the result of deliberate regulatory decisions, primarily the 1996 Telecommunications Act and the 2017 Ajit Pai FCC deregulation, that removed the ownership limits Congress and the FCC spent decades building.

### Who Controls What: The Broadcast Landscape

| Owner | Key Broadcast Holdings | Reach |
|-------|----------------------|-------|
| Rupert Murdoch / News Corp / Fox Corp | Fox News, Fox broadcast network, local Fox affiliates in every major market, Wall Street Journal, NY Post | Hundreds of millions of viewers/readers |

| iHeart (formerly Clear Channel) | 850+ radio stations | 150+ markets nationwide |

**Local TV news remains the primary news source for approximately 35% of Americans** — more than cable television, more than social media. When Sinclair controls 185 of those stations and iHeart controls 850 radio frequencies, the word "local" becomes a fiction.

**The federal government auctioned $81 billion worth of public spectrum in a single year and returned nothing to the local journalism that spectrum licenses are supposed to serve. The FCC renews broadcast licenses at a near-100% rate regardless of whether licensees have met the "public interest, convenience, and necessity" standard that is the legal foundation of every broadcast license in America.**

## THE CONSTITUTIONAL ANCHOR: AIRWAVES ARE PUBLIC PROPERTY

**This is the core legal fact that opponents of media reform consistently obscure: broadcast licensees do not own the electromagnetic spectrum. They borrow it from the American public under a federal license. That license comes with conditions. Congress can set any conditions it chooses that serve the public interest.**

### The Controlling Precedents

| Case | Holding |
|------|---------|
| *NBC v. United States*, 319 U.S. 190 (1943) | FCC has broad authority to regulate broadcast in "public interest, convenience, and necessity"; spectrum scarcity justifies regulation that would be unconstitutional for print media |
| *Red Lion Broadcasting Co. v. FCC*, 395 U.S. 367 (1969) | FCC content requirements (Fairness Doctrine) are constitutional; broadcast licensees use a public resource and have no First Amendment right to use it however they wish |
| *FCC v. National Citizens Committee for Broadcasting*, 436 U.S. 775 (1978) | SCOTUS **unanimously** upheld cross-ownership limits as constitutional under the public interest standard |
| *FCC v. League of Women Voters*, 468 U.S. 364 (1984) | Reaffirmed spectrum scarcity doctrine as constitutional foundation for broadcast regulation |

**The statutory text is equally clear.** 47 U.S.C. § 301 vests ownership of the electromagnetic spectrum in the United States. No private entity owns spectrum. Every broadcast license is a revocable grant of the public's property. 47 U.S.C. § 309(h) explicitly provides that no broadcast license "shall be construed to create any right, beyond the terms, conditions, and periods of the license." A license is not property. You are borrowing the public's airwaves. The public sets the terms.

## THE LEGAL HISTORY: HOW WE GOT HERE

### The Architecture of Protection — Then Its Demolition

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1934 | Communications Act establishes FCC; public interest standard for all broadcast licensing (47 U.S.C. § 307) |
| 1943 | *NBC v. United States*: Supreme Court upholds FCC authority to limit "chain broadcasting" (network monopoly) |
| 1970 | FCC adopts Financial Interest and Syndication Rules (Fin-Syn); limits network ownership of programming |
| 1975 | FCC adopts newspaper-broadcast cross-ownership ban in same market |
| 1978 | *FCC v. National Citizens Committee*: SCOTUS unanimously upholds cross-ownership limits |
| 1996 | **Telecommunications Act of 1996**: Congress dramatically loosens ownership rules; a single entity can now own multiple stations per market; Clear Channel immediately begins buying hundreds of stations |
| 2003 | FCC under Michael Powell attempts further deregulation; Congress and courts push back |
| 2017 | **Ajit Pai FCC eliminates nearly all remaining ownership limits**; newspaper-broadcast cross-ownership ban eliminated; local ownership caps gutted |
| 2017-2024 | Sinclair, iHeart, and Nexstar consolidate to near-monopoly levels in local broadcast |

**The 1996 Telecommunications Act is the proximate cause of the consolidated media landscape Americans live in today.** It deregulated broadcast ownership in the name of competition and produced oligopoly. The Pai FCC deregulation of 2017 completed the demolition of the ownership architecture that SCOTUS had unanimously upheld four decades earlier.

## THE SINCLAIR PROBLEM: A CASE STUDY IN REGULATORY FAILURE

**Sinclair Broadcast Group is the most instructive case study in what happens when the FCC stops enforcing public interest conditions.**

Sinclair controls 185+ television stations in 86 markets. It reaches approximately 40% of U.S. households — a concentration that would have been flatly illegal under the pre-1996 ownership rules. But the scale of Sinclair's holdings is not the most acute problem. The mechanism is.

**Sinclair requires all 185+ of its stations to air identical, centrally produced "must-run" segments** — political commentary written at Sinclair's corporate headquarters and aired by local news anchors who appear to viewers to be delivering local journalism.

The result: a local news anchor in Duluth, a local news anchor in Dallas, and a local news anchor in Denver read the same script on camera, on the same day, on what appears to be their local newscast. Viewers in all three cities see someone who looks like their local anchor delivering what appears to be local journalism. It is not local journalism. It is a single corporation's political operation running through 185 federally licensed broadcast stations.

**This is not press freedom. Press freedom is the right to say what you believe. This is the use of a federal public-interest license to run a centralized propaganda operation that systematically deceives the audiences whose trust those licenses are supposed to serve.**

## THE FOUR REFORMS

### Reform 1: No Billionaire Broadcast Ownership

No individual with a net worth exceeding $1 billion, or any company more than 25% controlled by such an individual, may hold an FCC broadcast license. Existing licensees exceeding this threshold must divest within 3 years of enactment.

**Legal authority:** The FCC's public interest standard (47 U.S.C. § 307(a)) has always permitted ownership conditions on broadcast licenses. *FCC v. National Citizens Committee* explicitly upheld structural ownership conditions as constitutional. The billionaire threshold is a structural public interest condition, not a viewpoint-based regulation.

### Reform 2: Reinstate and Strengthen Concentration Limits

Restore pre-2017 FCC ownership rules, with strengthened caps:

| Rule | Current Status | Proposed Standard |
|------|---------------|-------------------|
| National broadcast TV audience cap | Effectively eliminated (2017) | Maximum 5% of national broadcast TV audience per single entity |
| Local radio ownership limit | Weakly enforced | Maximum 50 stations nationally; maximum 3 per market |
| Newspaper-broadcast cross-ownership | Banned reinstated | Reinstated: no common ownership in same market |
| Local TV duopoly rule | Weakened | No entity may own 2 of the top 4 stations in any market |

**Legal authority:** All of these limits were previously upheld as constitutional. *FCC v. National Citizens Committee*, 436 U.S. 775 (1978). The Ajit Pai deregulation that eliminated them was not constitutionally required — it was a policy choice that a new FCC can reverse. The Administrative Procedure Act (5 U.S.C. §§ 553 et seq.) governs the notice-and-comment rulemaking required to restore them.

### Reform 3: Public Airwave Reclamation

The electromagnetic spectrum is a national commons. License conditions must reflect that.

- Establish a **Spectrum Public Interest Fund** using 5% of all spectrum auction revenue, dedicated to grants for local, independent journalism
- Reduce the FCC license renewal period from 8 years to **3 years**, with documented public interest compliance required at each renewal
- Require all licensees to submit annual public interest compliance reports, publicly available, as a condition of license renewal
- Mandate FCC enforcement of the license renewal standard: the current near-100% renewal rate regardless of compliance is not enforcement, it is abdication

**Legal authority:** 47 U.S.C. § 301 vests spectrum ownership in the United States. Congress has plenary authority to set the terms of spectrum licenses, including renewal conditions and revenue allocation. *NBC v. United States*, 319 U.S. 190 (1943).

### Reform 4: Journalism Licensing Standards

As a condition of license renewal, all broadcast licensees must demonstrate compliance with minimum journalism accountability standards:

- **Factual accuracy standard:** Factual errors corrected within 24 hours, with equal or greater prominence than the original error
- **News/opinion labeling:** Any content designated "news" must meet defined journalism standards; opinion content must be labeled as opinion with equivalent visual prominence to a news designation
- **Local content minimum:** Licensees must devote a minimum of 3 hours per day to locally produced, non-syndicated news content
- **No must-run centralized political content:** Prohibition on requiring local stations to air centrally produced political commentary as though it were local news

**Legal authority:** *Red Lion Broadcasting Co. v. FCC*, 395 U.S. 367 (1969) upheld FCC content requirements as constitutional for broadcast licensees using public spectrum. The Fairness Doctrine (Reform #05) provides the companion regulatory framework.

## PROPOSED LEGISLATION

### Broadcast Ownership Accountability Act

- **Purpose:** Restore and strengthen FCC structural ownership limits; establish billionaire wealth threshold; mandate divestiture timeline
- **Key Provisions:**
  - Restores all pre-2017 FCC ownership rules as minimum standards
  - Adds billionaire net worth threshold ($1 billion) as ownership disqualification
  - 3-year divestiture timeline for non-compliant licensees
  - FCC authority to enforce through license non-renewal
  - Prohibits "must-run" mandate of centrally produced political content as local news
- **Constitutional Authority:** Spending Clause (Art. I, § 8, cl. 1); FCC public interest authority (47 U.S.C. § 307); *NBC v. United States*, 319 U.S. 190 (1943)

### Public Airwave Reclamation Act

- **Purpose:** Reaffirm spectrum as national commons; create Spectrum Public Interest Fund; strengthen renewal requirements
- **Key Provisions:**
  - Reaffirms 47 U.S.C. § 301 spectrum ownership in the United States
  - Establishes Spectrum Public Interest Fund (5% of auction revenue) for local journalism grants
  - Reduces license renewal period from 8 years to 3 years
  - Mandates documented public interest compliance at each renewal
  - Requires annual public interest compliance reports, publicly filed
- **Constitutional Authority:** Property Clause (Art. IV, § 3, cl. 2); Commerce Clause (Art. I, § 8, cl. 3); 47 U.S.C. § 301

### FCC Journalism Standards and Accountability Act

- **Purpose:** Establish minimum journalism accountability standards as condition of broadcast license renewal
- **Key Provisions:**
  - Factual accuracy standard with 24-hour correction requirement at equal prominence
  - Mandatory news/opinion labeling with defined standards for each designation
  - Local content minimum: 3 hours per day of locally produced non-syndicated news
  - FCC enforcement authority: fines up to $1 million per violation; non-renewal for pattern violations
  - Public complaint process with mandatory FCC response timeline
- **Constitutional Authority:** *Red Lion Broadcasting Co. v. FCC*, 395 U.S. 367 (1969); 47 U.S.C. §§ 307, 309; FCC public interest authority

## THE OPPOSITION ARGUMENTS — AND THE ANSWERS

**"This is government control of the press. It violates the First Amendment."**

> Broadcast licensees are not the press in the constitutional sense that print publishers are. They use a public resource — the electromagnetic spectrum — that the federal government allocates. *NBC v. United States* (1943) established this distinction 80 years ago. *Red Lion* (1969) applied it to content requirements. The First Amendment protects a newspaper editor's right to say whatever she wants on her own paper. It does not give Rupert Murdoch's corporation an unqualified right to use the American public's airwaves however it chooses. Those are different legal categories. Every federal court to address this question has said so.

**"Broadcast licenses are property rights. You can't just impose new conditions or require divestiture."**

> 47 U.S.C. § 309(h) is explicit: a broadcast license "shall not be construed to create any right, beyond the terms, conditions, and periods of the license." Congress wrote that language specifically to prevent the argument now being made. Sinclair, iHeart, and Nexstar do not own the spectrum they use. They hold revocable licenses to borrow it. Courts routinely order divestiture in antitrust cases involving actual property rights. Requiring divestiture of a borrowed public resource is less intrusive, not more.

**"These rules target conservatives. Fox News, Sinclair, and iHeart happen to be right-leaning."**

> Ownership limits and public interest conditions are content-neutral structural regulations. They apply to the number and concentration of stations, not to the political viewpoint expressed on them. A hypothetical progressive billionaire buying 185 television stations would face the same divestiture requirement. The rules Ajit Pai eliminated in 2017 were designed by Democratic and Republican FCCs over four decades. The FCC under Republican Chairman Richard Nixon's appointees, Republican Chairman Mark Fowler's successors, and Democratic chairmen all maintained concentration limits — because the public interest in competitive local broadcasting is not a partisan question.

**"A 3-year divestiture timeline will destroy asset value and collapse the market."**

> Federal courts order corporate divestiture in antitrust cases on timelines comparable to or shorter than this proposal. *United States v. AT&T* (1982) broke up the largest private corporation in American history. Divestiture of excess broadcast stations is not confiscation — it is the orderly transfer of borrowing rights from one entity to another. The stations continue to exist. The licenses continue to be held. The only thing that changes is who holds them.

## TALKING POINTS

> The airwaves belong to the American people. Every broadcast license is a government grant of a public resource. When we hand Rupert Murdoch's family or Sinclair's David Smith a broadcast license, we are not giving them the right to use the public's spectrum however they want. We are lending it to them on conditions. One condition should be: you cannot own 40% of America's local news market.

> Sinclair Broadcasting required local news anchors in 86 cities to read the same corporate script on camera. Viewers in Duluth and Dallas and Denver watched what appeared to be their local news anchor — same face, same desk — delivering the same centrally written political content. That is not press freedom. That is a billionaire's propaganda operation running through a federal license.

> The 1996 Telecommunications Act said let the market sort it out. The market sorted it into six corporations controlling 90% of what Americans hear and see. This is not a market outcome we should protect. It is a market failure we need to correct.

> We regulate broadcast because there are only so many frequencies. You cannot start a new TV station on a spectrum that is already allocated. That is why the general principle — government stays out of the press — has always had an exception for broadcast. When government allocates the airwaves, it sets the terms. The Supreme Court said so in 1943. It said so again in 1969. It said so unanimously in 1978.

> Local news is dying. One in five Americans now lives in a news desert. Sinclair's 185 stations reach 40% of U.S. households and run centralized content produced at corporate headquarters. iHeart's 850 radio stations syndicate national programming to local markets where local voices used to be. The billionaire ownership model killed local news. The divestiture model is how we get it back.

## THE BOTTOM LINE

**The electromagnetic spectrum is a public resource. The federal government allocates it through broadcast licenses that come with a legal obligation to serve the public interest. For 40 years — from the 1934 Communications Act through the pre-1996 FCC ownership regime — that obligation was enforced through structural ownership limits that the Supreme Court unanimously upheld as constitutional.**

**Then Congress and the FCC dismantled that framework. Clear Channel bought hundreds of radio stations. Sinclair bought 185 television stations. Six corporations consolidated control of 90% of American media. The FCC renewed every license regardless of whether the public interest standard was met. One in five Americans now lives in a news desert. A single broadcast company runs centrally produced political content through the apparatus of local news in 86 markets.**

**The legal authority to fix this has existed since 1934. The Supreme Court ratified it in 1943, 1969, and 1978. The spectrum belongs to the American people. The FCC can set the conditions on which the public's airwaves are borrowed. Those conditions must include: you cannot own 40% of local television. You cannot require your local anchors to read your corporate political script. You must produce local news. And no individual with a billion-dollar fortune can hold these licenses at all.**

**Break up the concentrated ownership. Return the spectrum to its public purpose. Restore local news.**
