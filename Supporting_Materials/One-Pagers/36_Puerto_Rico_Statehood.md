# Reform #36: Puerto Rico Statehood

## The Puerto Rico Status Act — Full Representation for 3.2 Million American Citizens

**The Problem:** Puerto Rico is home to 3.2 million American citizens — more people than live in 20 U.S. states. They pay federal payroll taxes, serve in the military at higher per-capita rates than most states, and are subject to every federal law passed by Congress. They cannot vote for president unless they relocate to a state. They have no voting representation in the Senate. They have one non-voting delegate in the House. The federal government has treated Puerto Rico not as a community of citizens but as a colonial possession — denying full representation, limiting federal program participation, and failing catastrophically in crisis response. After multiple referendums in which Puerto Rican voters have chosen statehood, Congress has yet to act.

**The Solution:** Congressional admission of Puerto Rico as the 51st or 52nd state under Article IV, Section 3 of the Constitution. The enabling legislation — the Puerto Rico Status Act — passed the House of Representatives in 2022 and was blocked in the Senate. Paired with DC statehood legislation, Puerto Rican admission would complete the unfinished work of American democratic inclusion.

| Oregon | ~4.2 million | 2 | 6 | Yes |

**Puerto Rico has more people than 20 U.S. states.** Every single one of those states has two senators and voting House representation. Puerto Rico has none. This is not a constitutional quirk. It is a political choice — maintained for over a century, renewed by Senate inaction after each House passage of statehood legislation — to govern 3.2 million American citizens without their meaningful consent.

**Federal taxes paid:** Puerto Rico residents pay federal payroll taxes (Social Security, Medicare) in full. They also pay federal excise taxes and import duties. The taxes that fund the federal government flow from Puerto Rico; the representation that controls that government does not flow back.

**Military service:** Puerto Ricans have served in every U.S. conflict since World War I. More than 200,000 Puerto Ricans served in World War II. Puerto Ricans die in wars declared and authorized by a Congress they cannot vote for. The ratio of Puerto Rican military service members to total population consistently exceeds the national average.

## The Jones Act and the Colonial Legal Framework

Puerto Rico's relationship to the United States was established not through mutual consent or democratic process but through conquest. Spain ceded Puerto Rico to the United States in the Treaty of Paris following the Spanish-American War in 1898. Puerto Ricans had no vote on the terms of that treaty and no voice in the decision that made them subjects of American imperial power.

The **Jones-Shafroth Act of 1917** granted Puerto Ricans U.S. citizenship — but citizenship without the full rights that citizenship carries on the mainland. Citizenship conferred the obligation to serve in the military (useful in the context of World War I) without conferring the right to vote for the president who would send Puerto Ricans to war.

The legal framework established by the insular cases — a series of Supreme Court decisions from 1901 onward — created the doctrine of "unincorporated territories": places where the Constitution applies only partially, where Congress's plenary power is virtually unlimited, and where residents are citizens but not fully equal citizens. This doctrine was fashioned in the immediate aftermath of the Spanish-American War by justices who wrote explicitly about the unsuitability of "alien races" for full constitutional rights. The racist foundation of the insular cases doctrine is not a historical footnote — it is the operative legal rationale that continues to justify Puerto Rico's subordinate status.

## What Puerto Ricans Have Actually Voted For

The argument that Congress should wait for a "clear" Puerto Rican referendum is advanced by those who have had four clear referendums to respond to and have done nothing. The record is unambiguous.

| Year | Referendum Type | Result | Turnout |
|------|----------------|--------|---------|
| 1967 | Status plebiscite | 60% for Commonwealth, 39% for statehood | ~66% |
| 1993 | Status plebiscite | 48% Commonwealth, 46% statehood | ~74% |
| 1998 | Status plebiscite | 47% "None of the above," 46% statehood | ~72% |
| 2012 | Two-question status vote | 54% rejected current status; of those selecting a preferred status, 61% chose statehood | ~78% |
| 2017 | Status vote | **97% for statehood** — but widely boycotted by pro-Commonwealth parties, turnout ~23% | ~23% |

**The 2020 referendum is the operative mandate.** It was the most competitive, most legitimate, and highest-turnout statehood vote in Puerto Rican history. A majority of Puerto Ricans voted yes under conditions where both pro-statehood and anti-statehood forces actively participated. Congress has not acted on it.

The "we need a clearer vote" argument has been applied to each successive referendum and then set aside when the next referendum produces a clearer result. It is not a principled criterion. It is a moving goalpost deployed to justify continued inaction.

## Hurricane Maria and the Federal Failure of Territories

No single event has made the case for Puerto Rican statehood more powerfully than Hurricane Maria.

On September 20, 2017, Hurricane Maria made direct landfall on Puerto Rico as a Category 4 storm with 155 mph winds. The storm destroyed the island's electrical grid, its road network, its communications infrastructure, and its agricultural sector. It produced the largest humanitarian disaster on American soil since Hurricane Katrina.

**The federal response:**
- The Trump administration initially reported 64 deaths. The actual death toll, established by a comprehensive Harvard University study published in the *New England Journal of Medicine*, was approximately **2,975 people** — making Maria one of the deadliest natural disasters in U.S. history.
- FEMA's response was underfunded, understaffed, and systemically inadequate. A contract for 30 million emergency meals went to a company (Tribute Contracting) with one employee and no experience in disaster relief. 50,000 of those meals were never delivered.
- The federal government deployed significantly more resources to Texas and Florida — states with voting senators — following Hurricanes Harvey and Irma that same season.
- Months after the hurricane, hundreds of thousands of Puerto Ricans remained without power or clean water. The president threw paper towels at survivors during a photo opportunity.

**What statehood would have changed:** States have more leverage in extracting federal disaster response than territories do. Their senators and representatives can threaten budget votes, delay confirmation hearings, and organize political pressure in ways that Puerto Rico's non-voting delegate cannot. The differential disaster response between Puerto Rico and the mainland states hit by hurricanes in the same season reflects — at least in part — the differential political power that representation provides.

## The Jones Act as Colonial Economic Policy

The **Merchant Marine Act of 1920**, universally known as the Jones Act, requires that all goods shipped between U.S. ports be carried by U.S.-flagged vessels, built in the United States, owned by U.S. citizens, and crewed by U.S. citizens. The statute applies to Puerto Rico as an island territory dependent on maritime shipping for the vast majority of its goods.

The economic consequences are severe and documented:
- Puerto Rico pays **significantly higher prices** for goods than comparable island economies precisely because the Jones Act eliminates price competition from foreign-flagged vessels
- A study by the Puerto Rico Federal Affairs Administration estimated the Jones Act costs Puerto Rico **$537 million annually** in additional shipping costs
- During Hurricane Maria, the Jones Act requirement had to be temporarily waived to allow non-U.S. vessels to bring emergency relief — an acknowledgment that the law functions as a routine barrier to efficient commerce

The Jones Act was not designed with Puerto Rico in mind. It was designed to protect the U.S. merchant marine industry from foreign competition on domestic routes. Its application to Puerto Rico is a consequence of territorial status — had Puerto Rico been a state in 1920, the law would still apply, but Puerto Rican senators and representatives would have participated in shaping it and would have ongoing power to seek amendments.

This is the colonial economic structure in concrete form: policies are made for Puerto Rico, not by it or with it.

## The Economic Case for Statehood

Puerto Rico's economy has been in structural distress for over two decades — driven by a combination of federal policy choices, hurricane damage, demographic decline (driven by emigration to the mainland), and a debt crisis that culminated in a federal oversight board imposing austerity on the island without democratic input from Puerto Ricans.

**What statehood would change economically:**

| Program | Current Puerto Rico Status | Under Statehood |
|---------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Medicaid | Capped federal matching funds ($400M cap vs. uncapped for states) | Full federal matching at state rates (~$2.5B additional annually) |
| Supplemental Security Income (SSI) | Excluded entirely — Puerto Rico residents do not receive SSI | Full participation |
| Earned Income Tax Credit | Puerto Rico residents largely excluded | Full eligibility |
| Nutrition Assistance (SNAP equivalent) | Block grant with fixed cap — not the full SNAP entitlement | Full SNAP entitlement |
| Federal education funding | Formula-based with territorial status disadvantage | Full state formula participation |
| Section 8 housing | Capped and underfunded relative to need | Full participation |

The Government Accountability Office and multiple academic economists have estimated that full federal program participation under statehood would increase federal transfers to Puerto Rico by **$10-15 billion annually** — transforming the island's fiscal situation and providing resources for economic development, infrastructure, and public services.

Puerto Rican residents already pay federal payroll taxes funding these programs. They are currently excluded from the programs their taxes fund. Statehood ends that exclusion.

## Congressional Representation Under Statehood

**Senate:** 2 senators — the standard allocation for every state regardless of population. Puerto Rico would join the Senate on equal terms with Wyoming and California alike.

**House of Representatives:** Based on the most recent apportionment formula and Puerto Rico's population of approximately 3.2 million, Puerto Rico would receive **4-5 voting representatives** — compared to its current 1 non-voting resident commissioner.

**Electoral College:** 2 senate seats + 4-5 house seats = **6-7 electoral votes** for presidential elections. Puerto Ricans would vote in presidential elections for the first time in the history of U.S. citizenship for the island's residents.

**Voting behavior:** Puerto Rico does not vote in presidential elections currently — its voting pattern in those elections is not a direct indicator of future behavior. In Puerto Rican gubernatorial and legislative elections, the political landscape is locally specific, with the New Progressive Party (pro-statehood) and Popular Democratic Party (pro-Commonwealth) as the dominant forces rather than direct analogs to the mainland Democratic and Republican parties. Some Republican senators have supported Puerto Rican statehood. The attempt to reduce statehood to a simple partisan calculation is an oversimplification that conveniently serves as a reason to deny representation.

## The Puerto Rico Status Act

The **Puerto Rico Status Act** passed the House of Representatives in December 2022 with bipartisan support. It was blocked in the Senate during the lame-duck session of the 117th Congress.

### What the Act Does

**Stage 1 — A binding plebiscite with clear options:** The Act would have Congress authorize and fund a definitive plebiscite in Puerto Rico presenting three clearly defined options:
1. Statehood
2. Independence
3. Sovereignty in free association with the United States

Unlike prior referendums, which involved contested ballot language and the inclusion of options like "Commonwealth" whose meaning is disputed, the Status Act would present legally precise options whose consequences Congress has pre-committed to honor.

**Stage 2 — Congressional implementation:** If Puerto Ricans vote for statehood, Congress commits to pass an admission act within a defined timeframe. If Puerto Ricans vote for independence or free association, Congress commits to negotiate and implement those alternatives.

**Why the Status Act approach matters:** The Act removes Congress's ability to demand another referendum after each referendum — the indefinite deferral strategy. By committing to honor the result in advance, it transforms the plebiscite into a genuine act of self-determination rather than another advisory vote for Congress to ignore.

**Constitutional authority:** Article IV, Section 3 grants Congress plenary power to admit new states and to govern territories. Congress can admit Puerto Rico as a state without a constitutional amendment — exactly as it has admitted every other state since the original 13.

## The Racial Justice Dimension

Puerto Rico's population is approximately **98% Hispanic or Latino** — one of the most racially and ethnically distinct American populations in the country. The permanent exclusion of Puerto Rican residents from national political representation is the permanent exclusion of 3.2 million Hispanic and Latino citizens from the exercise of national political power.

This is not incidental to the politics of statehood opposition. The insular cases — the legal foundation for Puerto Rico's subordinate status — were decided by justices who were also ruling on the constitutionality of racial segregation, and the reasoning in those cases reflected the same hierarchy of racial fitness for self-governance that animated Plessy v. Ferguson (decided five years earlier). The doctrine that Puerto Ricans are citizens but unequal citizens was fashioned from the same intellectual material as Jim Crow.

The contemporary political resistance to Puerto Rican statehood does not use the explicit racial language of the insular cases. But the effect is identical: the permanent disenfranchisement of a majority-minority population of American citizens, maintained by a majority-white political institution, with consequences that fall entirely on that minority population.

## Connection to DC Statehood: Paired Legislation

The admission of DC and Puerto Rico as states are distinct questions with distinct histories, distinct constitutional paths, and distinct constituencies — but they are appropriately considered together as the unfinished work of American democratic inclusion.

**Similarities:**
- Both involve large populations of American citizens denied voting congressional representation
- Both have clear enabling legislation that has passed the House and been blocked in the Senate
- Both are majority-minority populations disenfranchised by a majority-white Senate
- Both have constitutional authority — Article IV, Section 3 — for admission by statute without amendment

**Differences:**
- DC statehood requires resolving the 23rd Amendment question; Puerto Rico does not
- Puerto Rico's colonial history, territorial status, and the insular cases framework add layers of legal complexity absent from DC
- Puerto Rico has an active, ongoing question of self-determination that includes options beyond statehood; DC's residents have consistently sought statehood specifically

**Paired legislation strategy:** Advancing both admissions simultaneously creates political reinforcement — it is harder to block both than either alone — and frames both as components of a single democratic commitment: American citizens deserve American representation.

## Opposition Arguments — and the Answers

### "Puerto Rico hasn't clearly voted for statehood."

In 2020, 52.5% of Puerto Rican voters chose statehood in a competitive, high-participation referendum in which pro-Commonwealth and independence forces actively participated. That is a majority. It is the clearest democratic mandate Puerto Rico has produced. Congress has not acted on it. The "needs a clearer vote" argument is infinitely applicable — there will always be a reason why the latest vote was not clear enough.

### "Let Puerto Rico vote on independence first — maybe they'd prefer that."

Puerto Rico has voted on status in six referendums over 55 years. Independence has never received more than 5% of the vote. Puerto Ricans are not asking for independence. They are asking for equality within the United States they are already citizens of. The argument that they should first be offered independence before statehood is advanced by people who know that independence will lose — and who prefer that Puerto Ricans remain in their current status indefinitely rather than gain congressional representation.

### "Statehood would add Democratic senators."

Puerto Rico's political identity is not a simple national Democratic/Republican overlay. The Puerto Rico status parties do not map onto mainland partisan alignments. More fundamentally: denying 3.2 million American citizens congressional representation because of how you assume they will vote is an explicit argument for partisan disenfranchisement. That argument disqualifies itself.

### "Puerto Rico should fix its economy and governance first."

Puerto Rico's fiscal distress is inseparable from its status as a territory. The debt crisis that produced the federal PROMESA oversight board — unelected, unaccountable to Puerto Ricans — was accelerated by unequal federal program participation, Jones Act shipping costs, and the inability of Puerto Rico's non-voting delegate to protect the island's interests in Congress. Telling Puerto Rico to fix its governance before it can have representation is like telling someone to get healthy before they can see a doctor.

### "Statehood is permanent — we shouldn't rush."

The United States has been governing Puerto Rico as a territory for over 125 years. Congress has not rushed anything. The 2020 statehood referendum produced a majority vote. The Puerto Rico Status Act passed the House. The Senate has had every opportunity to act. "Don't rush" is not caution. It is the position of those who prefer the current arrangement indefinitely.

## PROMESA and the Democratic Deficit in Puerto Rico's Governance

In 2016, Congress passed the Puerto Rico Oversight, Management, and Economic Stability Act (PROMESA) — establishing the Financial Oversight and Management Board (FOMB) to manage Puerto Rico's debt restructuring. The Board, appointed by Congress and the President under PROMESA's terms, has broad authority over Puerto Rico's budget, fiscal policies, and government operations.

**What the FOMB does:** The Board reviews and can override Puerto Rico's fiscal plans, certify or reject the government's budget, and impose austerity measures on public services and pensions. Its decisions are not subject to meaningful review by Puerto Rico's elected government.

**The democratic problem:** The FOMB is an externally imposed fiscal authority with power over Puerto Rican governance — appointed by federal officials whom Puerto Ricans cannot vote for, exercising authority over a government elected by Puerto Ricans. In the years since 2016, the Board has approved cuts to public pensions, university funding, and government services that no democratically accountable body in Puerto Rico would have voted for. This is not fiscal management. It is governance without consent.

**What statehood changes:** A state cannot have a PROMESA-style oversight board imposed on it from outside. When Detroit went bankrupt in 2013, Michigan established an emergency manager — but under Michigan law, with Michigan democratic accountability. Puerto Rico as a state would negotiate its fiscal crisis through the normal bankruptcy processes available to states (Chapter 9) and through its elected congressional delegation, not through an unaccountable external board.

Statehood is not just about voting rights. It is about the basic democratic principle that the people who are governed by a government should have power over that government.

## The Insular Cases: The Legal Foundation That Must Fall

The Supreme Court's insular cases — principally *Downes v. Bidwell* (1901), *Balzac v. Porto Rico* (1922), and related decisions — established the doctrine that the Constitution applies only partially to "unincorporated territories." Under this doctrine, Congress's power over Puerto Rico is virtually unlimited, and Puerto Ricans have only those constitutional rights that the Supreme Court deems "fundamental" rather than "formal."

**The racial context of these decisions is not historical footnote.** Justice Henry Brown, writing in *Downes v. Bidwell*, warned of the danger of incorporating "alien races" into the American constitutional system. The justices who created the insular cases doctrine were the same generation that upheld racial segregation in *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896). The intellectual framework — that some people are sufficiently American to merit full constitutional rights and some are not — is the same framework.

**The insular cases remain in force.** No Supreme Court case has overturned them. In 2020, the Court declined to revisit the doctrine in *Financial Oversight and Management Board for Puerto Rico v. Aurelius Investment*. In 2022, the Court decided *United States v. Vaello Madero* — rejecting the argument that Congress's exclusion of Puerto Rico from SSI violates the equal protection component of the Fifth Amendment. Justice Gorsuch, concurring, explicitly called for reconsideration of the insular cases: "A century ago, this Court adopted the Insular Cases and concluded that the Constitution does not apply with full force in territories like Puerto Rico. I write separately to suggest we should consider [reconsidering them]." Justice Thomas joined that concurrence.

**Puerto Rican statehood renders the insular cases moot** as applied to Puerto Rico — statehood makes Puerto Rico a state, and states are covered by the full Constitution. But the doctrine itself, which continues to apply to the other U.S. territories (Guam, the U.S. Virgin Islands, American Samoa, the Northern Mariana Islands, and their approximately 300,000 residents), demands legislative and judicial challenge regardless.

## Implementation: What Statehood Admission Looks Like

**Step 1 — The Status Plebiscite (Puerto Rico Status Act framework):**
Puerto Rico holds a binding plebiscite with three legally precise options: statehood, independence, sovereignty in free association. Congress commits in advance to honor the result. Assuming statehood wins, as it has in every recent competitive vote:

**Step 2 — The Puerto Rico Admission Act:**
Congress passes an admission act under Article IV, Section 3. Key provisions:
- Puerto Rico is admitted as a state effective upon presidential signature
- Puerto Rico's existing constitution (1952, as amended) becomes the state constitution pending a new state constitutional convention within 4 years
- Puerto Rico's existing commonwealth government structure becomes the transitional state government
- Congressional apportionment is adjusted at the next census to reflect Puerto Rico's population

**Step 3 — Federal program integration (phased over 5 years):**
- Full Medicaid matching immediately upon statehood
- SSI eligibility immediate
- Full SNAP entitlement immediate
- Full EITC eligibility immediate
- Jones Act reform legislated separately as part of economic transition package

**Step 4 — First elections:**
- Special elections for Puerto Rico's two Senate seats within 90 days of admission
- Puerto Rico participates in the next general House election with allocated representatives
- Puerto Rico participates in the next presidential election with its electoral votes

**Timeline:** If legislation passes in 2027, Puerto Rico could participate in the 2028 presidential election as a full state.
