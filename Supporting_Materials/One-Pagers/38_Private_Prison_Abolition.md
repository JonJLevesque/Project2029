# Reform #38: Private Prison Abolition

## The Public Safety, Not Private Profit Act

**The Problem:** The United States has outsourced the punishment of human beings to publicly traded corporations whose stock price rises when more people are incarcerated for longer. GEO Group and CoreCivic — the two dominant private prison companies — generate over $4 billion in combined annual revenue from contracts paid by the federal government and the states. They are not contractors providing neutral services. They are lobbyists, campaign donors, and political actors with a direct financial interest in the expansion of incarceration and immigration detention. Every mandatory minimum they lobbied for, every immigration enforcement contract they secured, every early release program they helped defeat — these are not policy positions. They are business development.

**The Solution:** A federal executive order directing all Department of Justice and Department of Homeland Security components to terminate private prison and detention contracts as they expire and to issue no new contracts with private prison companies. A companion statute extending the prohibition permanently, converting existing private prison jobs to federal employment, and establishing independent oversight of all remaining government-operated detention facilities.

## The Profit Motive Problem

Private prison companies have one business model: they get paid per day, per prisoner. Their profitability depends on two variables — the number of people incarcerated and the length of incarceration. This creates direct financial incentives to increase both.

### Lobbying for Incarceration

The private prison industry does not lobby for abstract policy positions. It lobbies for outcomes that fill beds:

**Mandatory minimums:** CoreCivic (then the Corrections Corporation of America) was a founding member of the American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC) and helped draft mandatory minimum sentencing legislation that was subsequently adopted across dozens of states. ALEC's "Truth in Sentencing" model legislation — which requires prisoners to serve at least 85% of their sentences — was championed by private prison companies that benefit from longer sentences.

**Three-strikes laws:** Private prison companies have consistently supported legislation reducing parole and early release eligibility. California's three-strikes law, versions of which were adopted in more than 25 states, generated substantial private prison contract opportunities.

**Immigration detention expansion:** Following the passage of the Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act of 1996 and the post-9/11 expansion of DHS, the private prison industry moved aggressively into immigration detention. GEO Group and CoreCivic now operate the majority of ICE detention facilities. They lobbied directly for increased immigration enforcement budgets and expanded detention capacity — not because they have policy views on immigration, but because detained migrants are revenue.

**Arizona SB 1070 (2010):** The support network for Arizona's controversial immigration enforcement law — which required police to check immigration status — included former ALEC members with direct ties to the private prison lobby. The law was expected to generate increased immigration detentions, increasing demand for detention beds.

This is documented in lobbying disclosure records filed with the U.S. Senate and the House of Representatives under the Lobbying Disclosure Act of 1995 (2 U.S.C. § 1601 et seq.). The documents are public. The pattern is unambiguous.

## The Cost Myth

The private prison industry's standard justification is efficiency: private management saves taxpayer money. The evidence does not support this claim.

### What the Research Shows

**Government Accountability Office (2014):** A GAO review of federal Bureau of Prisons contracts with private facilities found that BOP could not demonstrate cost savings from privatization. Per-prisoner costs at private facilities were not consistently lower than at comparable federal facilities.

**In the Public Interest (2013):** An analysis of private prison contracts in 60 jurisdictions found that most contracts included **"lockup quotas"** — provisions requiring governments to keep facilities between 80-90% full, or pay for empty beds regardless. Arizona, Louisiana, Oklahoma, and Virginia had contracts requiring 90% occupancy guarantees. Governments paid for empty cells during periods of declining crime.

**Arizona State University (2010):** Researchers compared cost-adjusted outcomes at private and public facilities in Arizona. After controlling for population characteristics (private prisons systematically house healthier, less violent inmates, reducing per-prisoner costs), the cost differential disappeared.

**The "cherry picking" problem:** Private prison companies select lower-risk populations for their facilities — inmates with shorter sentences, fewer medical needs, and lower security classifications. This artificially reduces their per-prisoner costs relative to government facilities serving higher-need populations. Any cost comparison that does not control for this selection bias is not a valid comparison.

**Recidivism:** A 2019 study published in the *Journal of Law and Economics* found that inmates released from private facilities had **higher recidivism rates** than those released from public facilities, controlling for individual characteristics. If the purpose of incarceration is public safety, private prisons produce worse outcomes at equivalent or higher costs.

## Documented Conditions

Private prison profit margins depend on controlling the largest cost center: labor. Private facility staff are paid less, trained less, and supervised less than their public counterparts. The consequences are documented in federal court records, Inspector General reports, and investigative journalism.

### Understaffing

- **Adams County Correctional Center (Mississippi, 2012):** A riot at the CoreCivic-operated facility resulted in the death of a correctional officer. A subsequent investigation found that CoreCivic had been systematically understaffing the facility to reduce labor costs. The staffing ratio at the time of the riot was below the contractually required level.
- **Aurora ICE Detention Center (Colorado):** GEO Group-operated facility documented by the ACLU as having staffing levels below those required for safe operation of a facility of its size.

### Medical Neglect

- **Medical care in private immigrant detention:** The DHS Office of Inspector General has repeatedly found that medical care at privately operated ICE detention facilities falls below required standards. A 2020 OIG report found deficiencies in medical screening, medication administration, and emergency response at multiple GEO and CoreCivic facilities.
- **Deaths in custody:** Between 2016 and 2021, ICE documented 46 deaths in immigration detention. A disproportionate share occurred at privately operated facilities.

### Labor Exploitation

GEO Group has operated a work program at its Aurora, Colorado ICE detention facility in which detained immigrants are paid **$1 per day** for labor — cleaning, cooking, and facility maintenance. A federal class action lawsuit (*Menocal v. GEO Group*, 10th Circuit, 2018) found sufficient evidence for a jury trial on claims that this program constituted forced labor in violation of the Trafficking Victims Protection Act (18 U.S.C. § 1589). The case settled. The program continued.

## The Obama Phase-Out — and the Reversals

### Obama Administration Action (2016)

On August 18, 2016, Deputy Attorney General Sally Yates issued a memorandum directing the Bureau of Prisons to reduce and ultimately end its use of private prisons. The memorandum cited safety concerns, lower rehabilitation programming, and the inability to demonstrate cost savings. At the time, approximately 22,000 federal inmates were housed in private facilities.

### Trump Reversal (2017)

On February 21, 2017 — one month into Trump's first term — Attorney General Jeff Sessions rescinded the Yates memorandum. The following week, the stock prices of GEO Group and CoreCivic rose sharply. GEO Group subsequently donated $225,000 to a pro-Trump super PAC — a first for the company, which had historically avoided direct presidential political donations, apparently concerned about the appearance of conflict.

### Biden Announcement (2021)

President Biden signed an executive order in January 2021 directing DOJ not to renew private prison contracts. DHS contracts — covering ICE detention — were not included. Implementation was partial; the order applied only to DOJ facilities, and contracts were allowed to run until expiration rather than being terminated immediately.

### Trump Reversal (2025)

On January 20, 2025, one of President Trump's first executive actions reversed Biden's DOJ private prison policy and directed DHS to dramatically expand ICE detention capacity — explicitly inviting private prison companies to bid on new contracts. GEO Group and CoreCivic stocks rose more than 20% in the week following the inauguration. By mid-2025, ICE detention capacity was expanding at the fastest rate in the agency's history, with the majority of new beds going to private contractors.

The pattern is clear: private prison policy toggles with administrations because it has never been legislatively anchored. Each reversal demonstrates that executive action is insufficient. A statute is required.

## The Executive Order: What Day One Looks Like

**Presidential authority over federal contracting is broad and well-established.** The President, acting through the Attorney General and the Secretary of Homeland Security, can direct executive branch agencies to:

1. **Issue no new contracts** with private prison companies for the housing of federal prisoners or immigration detainees effective immediately upon signing
2. **Decline to renew** any existing private prison or detention contract upon its expiration — no new options exercised, no contract extensions
3. **Direct BOP** to begin planning for repatriation of all federal inmates currently in private facilities to federal Bureau of Prisons facilities, on a timeline consistent with available federal capacity
4. **Direct ICE** to develop a plan for reducing immigration detention population consistent with due process requirements, prioritizing electronic monitoring and community supervision alternatives over detention
5. **Suspend any pending solicitations** for new private prison or detention contracts across all federal agencies

**The federal authority is limited to federal facilities and contracts.** States that contract with private prison companies are not directly affected by a federal executive order. They require either federal incentives or state-level legislation.

## Legislative Requirements

### The Public Safety, Not Private Profit Act

**Purpose:** Codify the executive order as a permanent statutory prohibition; prevent future administrations from reversing course; extend the prohibition to immigration detention; establish the worker transition program.

**Key Provisions:**

- **Permanent prohibition** on federal contracts with private prison companies for incarceration or immigration detention of persons in federal custody
- **Application to ICE detention:** The prohibition applies to all DHS detention contracts — not just BOP — closing the gap in the Biden executive order
- **Retroactive prohibition:** Existing contracts must not be renewed upon expiration; no contract extension options may be exercised
- **Definition of covered companies:** Any for-profit corporation that receives more than 50% of its revenue from incarceration or detention contracts with a government entity
- **Whistleblower protections** for employees of existing private facilities who report safety violations during the transition period

**Constitutional Authority:** Article I, Section 8 (appropriations); Article II (executive contracting authority); 18 U.S.C. § 4082 (BOP contracting authority); Immigration and Nationality Act § 241(g) (DHS detention authority)

### The Federal Prison Worker Protection and Transition Act

**Purpose:** Convert private prison jobs to federal civil service employment, ensuring no worker loses income or benefits during the transition from private to public operation.

**Key Provisions:**

- All employees of private prison facilities operating under federal contracts at the time of contract expiration are offered **federal civil service positions** at GS-grade salary equivalents upon conversion to federal operation
- Private prison employees with 5+ years of service receive **priority hiring consideration** for BOP positions at the same facility or a comparable facility
- Training and certification programs funded by BOP to bring transitioning employees up to federal training standards
- No displacement: federal BOP will not reduce total headcount at any converted facility below the private operator's documented staffing level at conversion

**The worker argument for abolition:** Private prison jobs pay less, carry fewer benefits, have higher turnover, and are more dangerous than equivalent federal positions. The private prison industry deploys the specter of job losses to defeat abolition legislation. The fact is that public operation of the same facilities produces better jobs. The Federal Prison Worker Protection Act makes this explicit by guaranteeing it.

### The Immigration Detention Reform Act

**Purpose:** Address the specific dynamics of immigration detention, where private operators control over 80% of beds and have the most aggressive lobbying presence.

**Key Provisions:**

- **Detention alternatives mandate:** ICE must demonstrate that detention is the **least restrictive means** of ensuring appearance at immigration proceedings before housing any individual in a detention facility; electronic monitoring and community supervision programs receive mandatory funding
- **Per diem cap:** Any immigration detention contracts in existence during the transition must have per-diem rates capped at documented cost — eliminating profit margin from the detention equation
- **Mandatory staffing ratios** in any remaining detention facility, with civil penalties for violations payable to detained individuals
- **Prohibition on lockup quotas:** No federal immigration detention contract may include minimum occupancy requirements or guaranteed payment for empty beds
- **Inspector General jurisdiction:** DHS IG given mandatory inspection authority over all immigration detention facilities, public or private, with results published publicly within 30 days of inspection

## Constitutional Dimensions

### The 13th Amendment

The 13th Amendment to the Constitution (ratified 1865) abolished slavery and involuntary servitude "except as a punishment for crime whereof the party shall have been duly convicted." This exception has historically been used to justify prison labor programs, including those operating in private facilities.

The private prison labor program at GEO Group's Aurora facility — paying detained immigrants $1 per day for labor that maintains the facility and reduces GEO's operating costs — sits in constitutional tension with the involuntary servitude prohibition. Detained immigrants have not been convicted of crimes. The criminal punishment exception does not apply. The 10th Circuit litigation in *Menocal* established that these claims are legally viable. Abolishing private detention eliminates the structural incentive to run these programs.

### The 8th Amendment

The 8th Amendment's prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment sets a constitutional floor for conditions of confinement. The Supreme Court in *Farmer v. Brennan*, 511 U.S. 825 (1994), held that "deliberate indifference" to known risks of serious harm to prisoners constitutes an 8th Amendment violation. Documented conditions at private facilities — chronic understaffing, inadequate medical care, failure to protect inmates from known violence risks — provide the factual predicate for 8th Amendment claims.

**Abolition is not the only remedy for 8th Amendment violations, but it is the most reliable one.** Profit motive and constitutional compliance are in structural tension at private facilities. The conflict is not resolvable through better oversight alone.

## The States

A federal executive order and statute govern federal contracting. States contract independently with private prison companies for state inmates — approximately 7% of state prison populations are privately housed. Federal policy can reach states through:

1. **Edward Byrne Memorial Justice Assistance Grants (JAG):** The largest federal criminal justice grant program (34 U.S.C. § 10151 et seq.) can be conditioned on states not entering new private prison contracts — modeled on the conditional spending framework of *South Dakota v. Dole*, 483 U.S. 203 (1987)
2. **Technical assistance:** The Department of Justice can provide states with cost analysis demonstrating that private prisons do not save money, supporting state-level legislative efforts
3. **Model legislation:** DOJ can develop and disseminate model state legislation for private prison abolition with worker transition provisions

## Public vs. Private: The Comparative Record

The core claim of the private prison industry — better outcomes at lower cost — fails on both dimensions when the data is examined honestly.

### Cost Comparison

| Metric | Private Facilities | Federal BOP Facilities |
|--------|--------------------|------------------------|
| Claimed per-diem savings | 10-15% (industry claim) | — |
| Actual per-diem after controlling for population | **No consistent savings** (GAO 2014) | — |
| Lockup quota provisions | **Present in most contracts** | Not applicable |
| Contract penalty for low occupancy | **Paid to private operator** | Not applicable |
| Transparency of cost data | Limited (proprietary) | Full (public records) |

### Safety and Outcomes

| Metric | Private Facilities | Public Facilities |
|--------|--------------------|-------------------|
| Staff turnover rate | **Significantly higher** | Lower |
| Staff-to-inmate ratio | **Below contractual requirements** (documented) | Regulated by BOP standards |
| Assault rates | **Higher in multiple studies** | Lower |
| Recidivism (5-year post-release) | **Higher** (*Journal of Law and Economics*, 2019) | Lower |
| In-facility programming (education, vocational) | **Reduced** (cost center) | Mandated by BOP policy |
| Grievance resolution | Internal, limited oversight | Federal review available |

**The private prison industry's business model requires underfunding the inputs** — staff, programming, medical care — **that produce the outcomes** the system is supposed to deliver. This is not a management failure. It is the predictable consequence of extracting profit from a captive population.

## What This Reform Is Not

This reform does not propose to eliminate incarceration. It proposes to eliminate the financial incentive to maximize incarceration. The distinction is fundamental.

Prisons operated by governments with public employees respond to government incentives: public safety, rehabilitation rates, recidivism reduction, community impact. Prisons operated by corporations with shareholders respond to shareholder incentives: revenue per bed, cost per prisoner, contract retention. These are not the same incentives. In a democracy, the punishment of human beings should respond to democratic accountability — not quarterly earnings calls.

## The Bottom Line

**The private prison industry's business model is the monetization of human captivity.** GEO Group and CoreCivic do not lobby for public safety. They lobby for the conditions under which public safety policy produces the most prisoners at the longest sentences for the highest per-diem rates. Every dollar they spend on lobbying is a dollar spent trying to influence the criminal justice and immigration enforcement systems in ways that fill their beds. This is not a distortion of the system. It is the system, operating exactly as designed.

**Abolition is not an ideological position. It is a management decision.** The federal government can house federal inmates in federal facilities operated by federal employees accountable to democratic oversight. It has done so for most of American history. The private prison experiment — roughly four decades old — has produced higher recidivism, documented abuse, understaffed facilities, and billions in profit extracted from government contracts funded by taxpayers. The experiment has failed by every measure except shareholder return.

**On Day One, the President can stop signing the contracts.** The legislative fix makes that permanent. The worker transition program ensures that no employee pays the price for a policy change that serves the public interest. The immigration detention reform ensures that the largest private detention market — ICE — is subject to the same accountability as federal prisons.

**These companies profit from keeping people locked up. That is a perverse incentive. We can eliminate it on Day One.**
